Best Credit Cards for New Graduates in Canada (2026)

No income requirements, no annual fees, strong rewards. Your first real credit card after school can set you up financially — or set you back. Here's how to choose right.

Why Your First Post-Grad Card Matters

Graduating is a financial turning point. You're transitioning from student income (or no income) to an entry-level salary, new rent obligations, and spending patterns that'll define the next 5–10 years. The credit card you choose now influences your credit score, your rewards potential, and — most importantly — whether you develop good habits or bad ones.

The trap most new grads fall into: applying for the wrong card (too complex, too high a fee, or with a high credit limit that leads to carrying a balance). The right first card is simple, free, and earns something on spending you'd be doing anyway.

Rule #1: Never carry a balance. At 19.99% interest, any rewards you earn are wiped out in weeks. All cards on this list are free or low-cost — but they're only valuable if you pay in full every month.

How to Pick the Right Card for Your Situation

If you eat out a lot or order delivery frequently

→ Simplii Financial Cash Back Visa (4% dining) or Tangerine World MC (2% restaurants). Simplii is better if dining is your #1 spend; Tangerine is better if you have 2+ other big categories too.

If you're still setting up your kitchen and cooking at home

→ PC Financial World Elite MC (4.5% at Loblaws/No Frills/Superstore) if you shop at Empire/Loblaws stores. BMO eclipse rise (5x groceries at any grocery store) if you shop anywhere.

If you just started your first job and want simplicity

→ Tangerine World MC. One 2% rate across your top 3 categories, automatic annual cash back deposit, no program to think about. Set it and forget it.

If you're a movie buff who goes to Cineplex

→ Scotiabank Scene+ Visa. Free movies are the most satisfying reward redemption. 5x at Cineplex, no fee, path to upgrade.

→ Start with the BMO eclipse rise (no fee, build BMO Rewards balance), then upgrade to BMO eclipse Visa Infinite (adds travel medical insurance) when your income qualifies.

How to Build Credit With Your First Card

  1. Use it regularly — Charge at least a few purchases every month. Cards that go dormant don't build credit efficiently.
  2. Pay in FULL every month — Set up pre-authorized full payment from your chequing account. No exceptions. Any balance carried at 19.99% interest will cost more than all rewards earned.
  3. Keep utilization under 30% — If your limit is $2,000, keep balances under $600 before the statement date. Lower utilization = higher credit score.
  4. Never miss a payment — One missed payment hits your credit score significantly. Set calendar reminders or auto-pay.
  5. Don't apply for multiple cards at once — Each application creates a hard inquiry. Stick to one card for 12–18 months, build history, then consider a second card if needed.
  6. Keep your first card open — Even after you upgrade, keep the original account open (no fee cards can stay open indefinitely). Age of credit history matters; closing old cards hurts your score.

Timeline reality check: It typically takes 12–24 months of consistent no-fee card use to reach a strong enough credit score (700+) to qualify for premium cards like the TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite ($60K income) or Scotiabank Gold Amex ($12/month). Be patient — your card selection after graduation affects what you'll qualify for in your late 20s.

Common New Grad Credit Card Mistakes

  • Getting a retailer card as your first card — Store cards (Canadian Tire Triangle, The Bay, etc.) have very high rates (29.99% typically) and limited utility. Start with a real Visa or Mastercard instead.
  • Choosing a premium card before you need it — The $120/year travel cards (Scotia Gold Amex, TD Aeroplan VI) are great later but not on a starting salary where the annual fee isn't clearly recovered.
  • Ignoring the income requirement — Apply before you technically qualify and you'll likely be declined (hard inquiry wasted). Wait until you have 2–3 months of pay stubs at the required income level.
  • Using a credit card for cash advances — Cash advance interest starts immediately (no grace period), at 22.99–24.99% depending on the card. Never use your card for cash.
  • Closing your student credit card — If you have an existing student card with any positive history, keep it open even if you rarely use it. The account age helps your score.
  • Applying for every welcome bonus offer you see — Churning credit card bonuses is an advanced strategy with real credit score risks. Build a solid foundation first.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a credit card with no income right after graduating? Yes — the BMO eclipse rise Visa, Tangerine World Mastercard, and Simplii Cash Back Visa have no published minimum income requirements. You'll still need to pass a credit check. If you have a thin credit file (limited history), some lenders may request income documentation anyway, even if no minimum is listed.
Should I get a student credit card or a regular card after graduating? Switch to a regular (non-student) card after graduation. Student cards often have lower limits and fewer benefits. The no-fee cards on this list — BMO eclipse rise, Tangerine World MC — are accessible to recent grads and offer significantly better rewards than typical student cards.
How many credit cards should I have? Start with one card. Get comfortable using it responsibly — paying in full every month — for at least 12 months before considering a second card. Most Canadians with strong credit histories have 2–4 cards with specific purposes (one for everyday rewards, one for travel, one for a specific store).
What credit score do I need for these cards? Most no-fee cards require a minimum score around 650–680. The Tangerine and Simplii cards are generally more accessible. Premium cards (World Elite) typically want 700+. Check your credit score for free via Borrowell or Credit Karma Canada before applying.
Is it worth getting a card with an annual fee right after graduation? Usually not — unless the welcome bonus clearly offsets the fee in year 1 and your income comfortably covers the requirement. In year 1, a $120/year card needs to earn you more than $120 in rewards (above what a free card would earn) to be worthwhile. That math often doesn't work on a starting salary with moderate spending. Start free, upgrade when the numbers make sense.
What if I'm new to Canada? New-to-Canada applicants often have thin or no Canadian credit history even with income. Look for cards marketed to newcomers: BMO Newcomer Banking offers, Scotiabank StartRight program, and CIBC newcomer packages often include credit cards with reduced qualification standards. Secured cards (where you deposit collateral) are also an option to build history from zero.
