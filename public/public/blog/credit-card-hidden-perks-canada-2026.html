1. Extended Warranty Protection

🛡️
Extended Warranty
Automatically doubles your manufacturer's warranty — up to 1 additional year
Worth: $100–$500+ per eligible claim

Most premium Canadian credit cards automatically double the original manufacturer's warranty on items you purchase with the card — up to a maximum extension (typically 1 additional year). This applies to electronics, appliances, tools, and most consumer goods. You don't register anything; the protection is automatic as long as you paid with the card.

Example: Your new laptop comes with a 1-year manufacturer's warranty. If you pay with an eligible card, your effective warranty becomes 2 years. When the manufacturer's warranty expires and your laptop develops a defect in month 15, your card's extended warranty covers the repair — at no cost to you.

Key limitations to know: Most cards exclude: items already excluded by the manufacturer's warranty, normal wear and tear, lost or stolen items (that's purchase protection, below), motorized vehicles, and items purchased for business use. Maximum eligible item values vary — typically $10,000 per item, with annual claim limits around $50,000. Always check your certificate of insurance.

2. Purchase Protection

🛒
Purchase Protection Insurance
Covers theft, damage, or loss for 90–180 days after purchase
Worth: Replacement cost of item (typically up to $60,000/year across all claims)

Purchase protection covers new items you buy with your card if they're stolen, accidentally damaged, or in some cases lost — for a window of 90 to 180 days after purchase, depending on your card. This is separate from extended warranty: purchase protection applies to unexpected damage (you drop your new iPhone, your bag is stolen with your new camera inside), while warranty covers manufacturing defects.

Classic use cases: New laptop falls off your desk and breaks in the first 90 days. New sunglasses stolen from your car. New AirPods accidentally run through the washing machine. If you paid with your credit card, you may have a claim.

Document immediately: Take photos of any damaged item before touching it further. File a police report for stolen items (most cards require it for theft claims). Keep the original receipt — you'll need it. Don't repair or dispose of the damaged item before the claim is processed.

3. Price Protection

💰
Price Protection
Get refunded the difference if the item drops in price within 60–90 days
Worth: Difference in price — sometimes $50–$300 on electronics

Price protection — also called price adjustment protection — refunds you the difference if an item you bought with your card goes on sale or is advertised at a lower price within a set window (typically 60–90 days). This used to be more common; some Canadian banks have phased it out. Check your current certificate of insurance.

This benefit is most valuable for Black Friday / Cyber Monday purchases in October and November, when items are often discounted weeks after a regular-price purchase. If you bought a TV in early November and it goes on sale on Black Friday, you can claim the price difference through your card — no returns-and-rebuys needed.

Price protection is disappearing: Several Canadian banks have quietly removed price protection from their cards in recent years. It's most common on Visa Infinite Privilege tier and certain Amex cards. Before counting on it, call your bank or download your current certificate of insurance to verify it's still included.

4. Mobile Device Insurance

📱
Mobile Device Insurance
Covers your phone against theft, accidental damage, and mechanical failure
Worth: $100–$800+ per claim (versus $12–$15/month standalone insurance)

Mobile device insurance is one of the most overlooked and most valuable credit card benefits. Several Canadian credit cards — particularly Visa Infinite and Mastercard World Elite tier cards — include coverage for your cell phone against theft, accidental damage (cracked screen), and sometimes mechanical breakdown after the manufacturer's warranty expires.

Critical activation requirement: You almost always must pay your monthly cell phone bill with the card to activate and maintain coverage. The phone itself does not need to be purchased on the card — but your monthly service bill must be charged to it. Set your plan payment on auto-pay to the eligible card and leave it there.

5. Roadside Assistance

🚗
Roadside Assistance
Towing, battery boost, flat tire, lockout — free of charge
Worth: Replaces CAA membership ($90–$180/year)

Many premium Canadian credit cards include roadside assistance — covering towing, battery boost, flat tire change, fuel delivery, and lockout service. CAA membership (Canada's most popular roadside service) costs $90–$180/year depending on tier. If your credit card already includes equivalent coverage, CAA is redundant.

Important nuance: Credit card roadside assistance is typically a dispatch service — they coordinate a local provider and send someone to help you. CAA's own network of branded trucks offers more consistent response times, and CAA Plus/Premier tiers include longer towing distances (160–320 km vs typically 10 km on credit card plans). For city drivers, credit card roadside assistance is usually sufficient. Highway and rural drivers may prefer CAA Plus.

6. Concierge Services

🎩
24/7 Concierge
Restaurant reservations, event tickets, travel research — by phone or app
Available on: Visa Infinite, Mastercard World Elite, Amex Platinum/Gold tier cards

Visa Infinite, Mastercard World Elite, and Amex Centurion/Platinum cards include 24/7 concierge services — a human assistant (or app-mediated service) that can research and book restaurant reservations, source difficult event tickets, arrange travel itineraries, make gift recommendations, and more. Think of it as having a personal assistant available on demand.

Concierge is especially useful for: booking popular restaurants during peak times, sourcing concert tickets when primary sales are sold out, and researching travel logistics across multiple legs of a trip. The Amex Platinum concierge is considered among the best in Canada — they have direct relationships with many venues and can sometimes secure reservations that aren't publicly available.

How to access: Call the number on the back of your card and ask for concierge, or use the card's companion app (TD MyInsurance, RBC Mobile, Amex Canada app). Visa Infinite concierge: 1-888-853-4458. Mastercard World Elite: 1-800-GOLD-111.

7. Overlooked Travel Insurance Components

Most Canadians know that premium cards include trip cancellation insurance — but several components of travel insurance go almost entirely unclaimed:

Flight delay insurance: If your flight is delayed 4–6 hours or more, your card may reimburse hotel, meals, and incidentals during the delay. Cards like the TD Infinite Privilege cover up to $500/person for qualifying delays. Most people simply wait in the airport lounge — not knowing they could expense a hotel room nearby.

Baggage delay insurance: If your checked luggage is delayed arriving at your destination for 4+ hours, your card typically covers emergency clothing and toiletry purchases up to $500–$1,000. Buy replacement items, keep receipts, file the claim.

Rental car collision/loss damage waiver (CDW/LDW): Almost all Visa Infinite and Mastercard World Elite cards include rental car collision coverage — replacing the $20–$40/day CDW sold at the rental counter. Important: You must decline the rental counter's CDW to use the card's coverage (they can't both apply), and you must pay the full rental on the card. Some cards offer primary coverage (pays first); others are secondary (pays after your personal auto policy).

Rental car CDW — card vs. counter: When the rental agent asks "do you want the collision damage waiver?", you should decline and use your card's coverage — but only if your card actually includes it. Visa Infinite and Mastercard World Elite typically do. Standard (non-infinite/world) Visa and Mastercard typically do not include rental car coverage. Check your certificate before declining the counter's CDW.

How to Make a Claim — Step by Step

The claims process is where many people give up. The insurance is real — but you need to follow the right steps.

  1. Find your certificate of insurance Log into your bank's online portal or app and download the current Benefits Guide or Certificate of Insurance for your card. This is the authoritative document — it lists every covered benefit, exclusions, and claim procedures.
  2. Locate the claims number The certificate will list a specific claims number (not the regular card customer service line). This connects you to the insurance underwriter — typically Assurant, Manulife, or similar. Call promptly; many benefits have 30–60 day claim notice windows.
  3. Gather your documentation For most claims: original receipt, card statement showing the purchase, and incident documentation (police report for theft, photos for damage, repair quote or itinerary for travel delays).
  4. File within the window Extended warranty and purchase protection claims typically must be filed within 30–90 days of the incident. Travel insurance claims (flight delay, baggage delay) typically require filing within 30 days of return from travel.
  5. Follow up in writing After calling, request a claim confirmation number and email summary. If the claim is denied, ask for the denial reason in writing — many initial denials are reversed on appeal with additional documentation.
