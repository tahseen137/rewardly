How to Maximize Credit Card Rewards in Canada 2026

Most Canadians leave hundreds of dollars in rewards on the table every year. Here's how to capture all of it — without changing your spending habits.

Rewardly Strategy Guide · July 2026

The average Canadian household spends $55,000–$65,000 per year. Put every dollar on the right credit card, and you can realistically earn $800–$1,500+ in cash back, travel points, or statement credits annually — without spending more than you already do.

Most people are earning far less than that because they're using a single general-purpose card, haven't optimized for their spending categories, or aren't maximizing welcome bonuses. This guide shows you exactly how to fix that.

Strategy 1: Capture the Welcome Bonus First

💡 Welcome bonuses are worth more than a year of spending

A typical premium card offers 50,000–100,000 points or $200–$500 in cash back as a welcome bonus after meeting a minimum spend (usually $3,000–$5,000 in the first 3 months). At 0.8–1¢ per point, a 75,000-point welcome bonus is worth $600–$750.

That's more than most Canadians earn in 12 months of normal card usage at 1–2%. Welcome bonuses are the highest-return activity in credit card optimization. If you're not regularly collecting welcome bonuses on new cards, you're leaving the biggest dollars behind.

How to use this: When you need a new card, apply for one with a strong welcome bonus timed to a period of higher natural spending (renovation, vacation planning, back-to-school). Meet the minimum spend with spending you'd do anyway — don't manufacture spending.

Strategy 2: Build a Category-Optimized 2-Card Wallet

💡 Most Canadians need exactly 2 cards

The goal is to earn the highest possible rate on every spending category. One card rarely dominates all categories — a single card earning 4% on groceries might earn only 1% on dining and travel. A 2-card setup fills the gaps.

The classic Canadian 2-card wallet:

  • Card 1 — High grocery/daily spend: Scotiabank Momentum Visa Infinite (4% grocery + 4% bills) or Scotiabank Gold Amex (6% dining + grocery)
  • Card 2 — Travel / everything else: Scotiabank Passport Visa Infinite (no FX fee, flexible points) or Amex Cobalt (5× dining + grocery)

Strategy 3: The Optimal Wallet by Spending Profile

🛒 Grocery-First Family ($900/mo groceries, $200 dining)

  • Primary: Scotiabank Momentum Visa Infinite — 4% on $900 groceries + $300 bills = $48/mo
  • Dining: Amex Cobalt or Scotia Gold Amex — 5–6% on $200 dining = $10–$12/mo
  • Total monthly earn: ~$58–$60 = $700–$720/yr vs. ~$180 on a single 1% card

✈️ Frequent Traveler ($2,000/mo total, 6+ flights/yr)

  • Primary: Amex Cobalt — 5× on dining + groceries; best MR earn in Canada
  • Travel booking + international: Scotiabank Passport VI — no FX fee, 6 PP visits
  • Bonus: Carrier co-brand (WestJet RBC or TD Aeroplan) for free bags + status
  • Total: $1,200–$1,800/yr in travel value

🏙️ Urban Millennial ($600/mo dining, $200 groceries, streaming)

  • Primary: Scotiabank Gold Amex — 5× dining + 6× grocery = exceptional
  • Backup Mastercard (for Costco): Rogers Red World Elite — 1.5% everywhere
  • Total: $900–$1,100/yr

Strategy 5: Point Transfers — The Secret to 5–10× Value

💡 Transferring to airline programs unlocks outsized value

When Amex MR points transfer to Aeroplan at 1:1 with a 10% bonus (on transfers of 10,000+ points), and you then redeem Aeroplan for a business class flight worth $3,000 at 100,000 points (3¢ per point), you've gotten 3× more value than cash-out at 1¢/point.

This is advanced territory, but the math is compelling for those willing to learn it. The three highest-value Canadian transfer opportunities:

  • Amex MR → Aeroplan: 1,000 MR = 1,100 Aeroplan pts (10% bonus). Best deal in Canada.
  • Scene+ → Aeroplan: 1,000 Scene+ = 1,000 Aeroplan pts. At no additional cost.
  • Amex MR → Marriott Bonvoy: 1,000 MR = 1,200 Bonvoy pts. (1:1.2 ratio)

Travel redemptions at 3–5¢ per point effectively quadruple the value of the 1¢ redemptions most people use.

Strategy 6: Charge Everything — No Category Left Behind

💡 Put every dollar on a rewards card

This sounds obvious, but many Canadians still pay insurance premiums, property taxes, CRA balances, tuition, or professional services in cash, cheque, or bank transfer. Every dollar paid that way earns zero rewards.

Categories that Canadians commonly miss putting on cards:

  • Recurring bills: Netflix, Spotify, internet, phone, utilities (4% on Scotia Momentum if set to autopay)
  • Insurance premiums (home, car, life) — many are now payable by credit card
  • Property taxes — some municipalities accept credit card (check yours)
  • Professional services (accountants, lawyers, contractors) — ask if they accept cards
  • Healthcare: dental, physio, optometry — almost always card-friendly
  • Gas: easy 2–4% on dedicated gas cards vs. 1% on a generic card
