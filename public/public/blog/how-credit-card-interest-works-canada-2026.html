How Credit Card Interest Works Canada 2026

Most Canadians don't understand exactly how credit card interest accumulates — or how to avoid paying it entirely with the right habits. Here's the complete picture.

The Billing Cycle — How It Works

Every credit card has a billing cycle — typically 28–31 days. At the end of each cycle, your bank generates a statement showing all transactions and your "statement balance." You then have a grace period (usually 21–25 days) to pay that statement balance in full before interest is charged.

Day 1: Billing Cycle Opens

New cycle begins. All purchases made this month appear on your upcoming statement.

Day 30: Statement Date (end of cycle)

Bank generates your statement. It shows total balance owing, minimum payment due, and payment due date.

Day 30–54: Grace Period (21–25 days)

You have this window to pay your statement balance in full. NO interest accrues during this time IF you paid your previous statement in full.

Day 54: Payment Due Date

If you pay the full statement balance by this date, zero interest is charged. If you pay only the minimum (or any partial amount), interest starts accruing on the unpaid balance — AND on all new purchases retroactively.

The Grace Period Trap: The grace period only applies if you paid your PREVIOUS statement balance in full. If you carried any balance from last month, you lose the grace period entirely — interest accrues on ALL purchases from their transaction date, not from the payment due date. This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of credit card interest.

How Daily Interest Is Calculated

Canadian credit cards charge interest daily, not monthly. The annual rate (typically 19.99% for purchases, 22.99% for cash advances) is divided by 365 to get the daily rate.

Example: $1,000 balance at 19.99% APR

Annual rate19.99%
Daily rate (÷ 365)0.054767%
Daily interest charge on $1,000$0.548
Monthly interest charge (30 days)$16.43
Annual interest on $1,000 unpaid balance$199.90

Interest compounds — meaning interest is charged on previous interest. If you carry a $1,000 balance for a full year and only pay the minimum, you'll pay significantly more than $199.90 because each month's interest is added to the principal.

5 Ways to Never Pay Interest on a Canadian Credit Card

1. Set Up Full-Balance Autopay

Autopay for the statement balance (not just minimum) eliminates interest charges automatically. Never miss a payment again.

2. Pay Before the Statement Closes

Pay your balance before statement date (day 30). Your statement then shows $0 owing, and no minimum payment is required.

3. Use the Card as a Debit Card Mentally

Only charge what's already in your bank account. Never use credit for spending that isn't budgeted and funded.

4. Check Your Balance Weekly

Awareness prevents overspending. Most Canadian banks have alert features that notify you when balance exceeds a set amount.

5. Keep One Card, One Purpose

Using multiple cards makes it harder to track your total credit exposure. Start with one card until you're confident in the habit.

6. Know What Triggers Immediate Interest

Cash advances, gambling transactions, and cryptocurrency purchases typically have NO grace period — interest starts immediately from transaction date.

Frequently Asked Questions

If I pay the minimum payment, do I still get charged interest?
Yes — paying only the minimum payment means you're carrying a balance. Interest is charged on the unpaid portion at your card's purchase APR (typically 19.99% in Canada). The minimum payment covers some interest and a tiny bit of principal, but most of it goes toward interest, not reducing your actual debt. Paying only the minimum on a $3,000 balance at 19.99% takes over 10 years to pay off and costs $3,000+ in interest alone.
When does interest start on a new credit card purchase?
For a purchase made on a card where you paid last month's statement in full: interest doesn't start until after your payment due date, if you haven't paid the new statement balance. For a card where you're already carrying a balance: interest starts from the transaction date — there's no grace period. This is why carrying any balance is costly — you lose the grace period on all new purchases.
What's the difference between APR and daily interest rate?
APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated yearly rate — 19.99% on most Canadian credit cards. Your bank divides this by 365 to get the daily periodic rate (0.05477%), which is applied to your average daily balance. Your monthly interest charge = average daily balance × daily rate × number of days in billing period. Most Canadian card statements show the daily rate in the fine print.

The key rule for rewards cards: Credit card rewards are only valuable if you pay your balance in full every month. At 19.99% interest on a carried balance, the interest cost dwarfs any reward earn rate. A 5% rewards card carrying a $2,000 balance costs $33/month in interest — you'd need to spend $660 each month to earn $33 in rewards to break even. Always pay in full.
