Best Credit Cards for Travel Insurance in Canada 2026

Emergency medical, trip cancellation, baggage, rental car — which card covers you best?

Home / Blog / Best Travel Insurance Credit Cards Canada 2026

Credit card travel insurance can save you thousands — but the coverage differences between cards are enormous. A hospital stay in the US can cost $5,000–$50,000. A trip cancellation can cost $1,000–$10,000. Knowing which card actually covers you — and for how much, for how long — could be the most important financial decision you make before any trip.

What Credit Card Travel Insurance Actually Covers

🏥

Emergency Medical

Hospital, doctor, ambulance costs abroad. The most critical coverage — US hospitals can bill $10,000/day+. Look for: coverage amount ($1M–$5M+), duration (how many days), and age restrictions.

✈️

Trip Cancellation

Reimburses non-refundable trip costs if you cancel before departure for covered reasons (illness, death in family, job loss, etc.). Look for: per-person limit and total limit.

🔄

Trip Interruption

Covers extra costs to return home if trip is cut short mid-trip (medical emergency, death, travel disruption). Usually higher limit than trip cancellation.

🧳

Baggage Loss/Delay

Reimburses lost or stolen bags, or emergency purchases when bags are delayed. Range: $500–$3,000 per person. Often undervalued by cardholders.

⏰

Flight Delay

Covers meals/hotel if flight is significantly delayed (typically 4–6 hours). Usually $500/incident. Lesser coverage but frequently useful.

🚗

Rental Car Insurance

Covers damage/theft to rental vehicles instead of buying rental company's insurance ($20–$40/day). Most cards cover up to $65,000 MSRP. Duration matters: 31 vs 48 days.

Critical Things to Check Before Relying on Card Insurance

⚠️ The "Charge It to Your Card" Rule

Most credit card travel insurance only activates if you charge the covered expense (flights, hotels, car rental) to the specific card offering the insurance. If you book your trip on a different card, you may not be covered by the travel insurance — even if you have the card in your wallet.

Always book your flights, hotels, and car rentals on the card whose insurance you want to use. This is the #1 reason travel insurance claims get denied.

⚠️ Pre-Existing Conditions

Most credit card emergency medical insurance excludes pre-existing conditions — defined as any medical condition you had in the 90–180 days before travel. If you or a covered family member had recent treatment for heart disease, cancer, diabetes, or respiratory conditions, credit card insurance may not cover related emergencies. Purchase supplemental travel insurance if applicable.

⚠️ Age Cutoffs and Duration Reductions

Medical coverage duration drops sharply with age. A card offering 30 days at age 54 might offer only 15 days at age 65 and nothing at 75+. If travelling at an older age, always read the certificate of insurance and buy top-up coverage for any days not covered.

Do You Need Separate Travel Insurance?

Credit card travel insurance is a great supplement but has limits. Consider purchasing standalone travel insurance when:

You're over 65 — most card coverage drops to 8–15 days and excludes many pre-existing conditions. Standalone senior travel insurance is important.

You're taking a long trip — if you're away more than 25–30 days, card coverage expires mid-trip. Medical bills in the US without coverage can reach $100,000+ for a serious incident.

Trip cost is very high — most cards cap trip cancellation at $1,500–$2,500/person. If your trip is $10,000 per person (cruise, adventure tour, honeymoon), the gap is significant.

Pre-existing conditions exist — credit card insurance typically excludes them. Standalone policies with a medical questionnaire may still cover you.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is credit card travel insurance as good as separate travel insurance?
For most trips under 21 days for travellers under 65 without pre-existing conditions: yes, credit card insurance is adequate and saves you $50–$150 in separate premiums. For older travellers, longer trips, or those with health conditions, standalone insurance is essential. Many experienced travellers use card insurance as their primary coverage and add standalone insurance only for longer or more expensive trips.
What happens if I have two credit cards with travel insurance?
Most credit card travel insurance policies are "excess" or "coordinating" — they coordinate with each other rather than stacking. If you have two cards and one covers 80% of a claim, the other won't necessarily cover the remaining 20%. The specific coordination rules depend on each card's Certificate of Insurance. Booking on the card with the better insurance is usually the simplest strategy.
Does credit card travel insurance cover adventure activities?
It depends on the activity. Most cards exclude extreme or high-risk activities: skydiving, bungee jumping, professional sports. Some exclude skiing and snowboarding. Hiking and snorkeling are typically included. Check your Certificate of Insurance for the specific exclusion list before your trip — or buy a separate adventure sports rider if needed.
Which card is best for a US trip?
For a US trip, prioritize emergency medical coverage — US healthcare is the most expensive in the world. The National Bank World Elite ($5M, 60 days), CIBC Aventura ($5M, 31 days), or BMO World Elite ($5M, 21 days) all provide strong US medical coverage. Additionally, use a no-FX card like Scotiabank Passport for any US spending to avoid the 2.5% forex surcharge.
