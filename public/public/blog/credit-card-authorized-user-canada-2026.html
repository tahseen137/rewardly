Credit Card Authorized Users Canada 2026

Adding a spouse as an authorized user consolidates household rewards and can help build their credit. But it also creates shared liability. Here's everything you need to know.

What Is an Authorized User (Supplementary Cardholder) in Canada?

An authorized user (also called a supplementary cardholder in Canadian bank terminology) is a person added to an existing credit card account by the primary cardholder. The authorized user receives their own physical card with their name on it, but the account itself belongs to the primary cardholder.

Key characteristics of authorized users in Canada:

All spending by the authorized user appears on the primary cardholder's account and statement. The primary cardholder is legally responsible for all charges made by the authorized user. The authorized user can generally make purchases but cannot change the account terms, increase the credit limit, or add more authorized users.

Credit Score Impact — Primary Cardholder

When you add an authorized user, your credit score is generally unaffected in the short term — adding users doesn't trigger a credit inquiry. However, there are secondary effects to consider:

Credit utilization: If your authorized user significantly increases spending on the account, your credit utilization ratio rises (balance ÷ limit). High utilization hurts your score. Ensure you're paying the full balance monthly regardless of who spent what.

Payment history: You remain entirely responsible for the bill. If the authorized user spends beyond what you can pay and you miss a payment, YOUR credit score is damaged — not the authorized user's.

No hard inquiry: Adding an authorized user does not trigger a hard credit inquiry on the primary cardholder's file. This is unlike applying for a new card yourself (which does cause a temporary score dip).

Critical liability point: The primary cardholder is 100% legally responsible for all authorized user spending. If your authorized user goes on a spending spree and you can't pay the bill, you bear the full financial and credit consequences. Only add authorized users you trust completely — and ideally discuss spending expectations with them beforehand.

Credit Score Impact — Authorized User

In Canada, most major banks DO report authorized user accounts to credit bureaus (Equifax and TransUnion) — unlike the US, where this is more variable. This means being added as an authorized user can help build a thin credit file.

How it helps: The authorized user's credit report will show the account, including the credit limit, balance, and payment history. A long, positive payment history on the account benefits the authorized user. High credit limit on the account improves the authorized user's available credit, potentially lowering their overall utilization ratio.

Strategic use for new Canadians and young adults: A new immigrant or young adult with no Canadian credit history can be added as an authorized user on a parent's or spouse's long-standing account. The account's full payment history appears on the authorized user's credit report, immediately giving them a credit history. This is one of the fastest ways to establish credit in Canada without a hard inquiry.

Note: Being an authorized user is not as impactful as being a primary account holder. Credit scoring models weight primary account responsibility more heavily. Eventually, the authorized user should apply for their own card and build an independent credit history.

Points Strategy: Consolidating Household Rewards

One of the best reasons to add a spouse or partner as an authorized user is to pool all household spending onto one account, accelerating rewards accumulation.

Example: If you and your spouse each have separate Aeroplan cards and each earn 10,000 Aeroplan points/year individually, it takes 2 years to accumulate 20,000 points each. By adding your spouse as an authorized user on your Aeroplan card instead, all household spending combines — you may accumulate 20,000 points in one year. A round-trip Economy rewards flight within Canada starts around 12,500–25,000 Aeroplan points — pooling spend reaches this threshold twice as fast.

Best practice for couples: Designate one person's card as the "household primary" — typically whichever earns more from your household's top spending category (e.g., Amex Cobalt if dining is your #1 spend). Add the other person as a supplementary cardholder. Use a second no-fee card (Rogers Red WE MC or Tangerine) as backup for merchants that don't accept the primary card type.

How to Add and Remove an Authorized User

  1. Adding an Authorized User

    Log in to your bank's online portal or call the number on the back of your card. You'll need the person's full legal name, date of birth, and typically their SIN (Social Insurance Number) — the SIN allows the bank to report the account to credit bureaus on the authorized user's file. Some banks allow online addition; others require a phone call.

  2. The New Card Arrives

    The bank will mail a new card to your address (or the authorized user's address, depending on the bank's policy). The card will have the authorized user's name on it but the same account number prefix. The authorized user typically has their own 4-digit PIN for their card.

  3. Removing an Authorized User

    Call the number on the back of your card or use online banking. Request removal of the supplementary cardholder. The bank will block the supplementary card from future use. Important: collect and destroy the physical card from the authorized user at the same time — a blocked card number should not be used, but it's good practice to recover the physical card.

  4. Credit Bureau Impact of Removal

    When an authorized user is removed, the account typically disappears from the authorized user's credit report within 1–3 months. Any credit score benefit from being an authorized user is lost at removal. This is why building an independent credit history as a primary account holder is important for the authorized user long-term.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can an authorized user be a minor (child) in Canada?
Some Canadian banks allow minors (typically 13+) to be added as authorized users, though this varies by institution. Adding a teenager as an authorized user on a parent's account with a clear spending conversation can be a responsible way to introduce credit concepts and begin building a credit file early. The primary cardholder remains responsible for all charges. Set clear spending expectations and consider whether giving a teen unrestricted card access is appropriate for your family's circumstances.
Does the authorized user need good credit to be added?
No — the authorized user's credit history is typically not checked when they are added to your account. You, as the primary cardholder, are responsible for all charges. This is why authorized user status is used to help people with limited or damaged credit histories: they can benefit from your good account without needing to independently qualify for credit.
Do authorized users get the same travel benefits as primary cardholders?
It depends on the card. For most standard cards (Amex Cobalt, Scotia Gold, TD Aeroplan VI), supplementary cardholders receive the same insurance benefits as the primary cardholder when they use the card for travel purchases. For ultra-premium cards like Amex Platinum, authorized user (supplementary) benefits are card-tier dependent — a supplementary Platinum card ($175 fee) grants full Centurion Lounge access and most primary benefits; a supplementary Gold card ($50 fee) grants fewer benefits. Always confirm with your specific card's cardholder guide.
