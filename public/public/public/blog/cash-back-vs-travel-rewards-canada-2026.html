Cash Back vs Travel Rewards in Canada 2026 — Which Is Better?

The honest answer: travel points beat cash back in maximum value but require more effort. Cash back is simpler, always worth the same, and never expires. Which is better depends entirely on how much you travel and how much complexity you're willing to manage.

The Core Difference

💵 Cash Back

  • Worth exactly what it says — $1 = $1
  • Deposited as statement credit or bank transfer
  • No expiry, no blackout dates, no redemption minimums
  • Works even if you never travel
  • Simple to understand and track
  • Typically earns 1–4% on purchases
  • No transfer partners, no sweet spots

✈️ Travel Points

  • Variable value — 0.5¢ to 6¢+ per point depending on redemption
  • Can dramatically outperform cash back for premium travel
  • Require planning and flexibility for best value
  • Transfer partners unlock outsized value
  • Welcome bonuses are larger (worth $500–$1,200+)
  • Travel insurance included on most premium cards
  • Programs can be devalued by issuers over time

Which Is Better for Your Profile?

🏠 The Occasional Traveler (1–2 domestic trips/year)

Annual travel spend: $1,000–$3,000. Primarily flies economy, stays in hotels. Doesn't have flexibility to choose dates around award availability.

Verdict: Cash back likely wins. You'll never unlock premium cabin sweet spots. Travel points at 1¢/pt = cash back at 1%. Simplicity and zero complexity has real value. Try Simplii 4% dining or CIBC Dividend VI 4% grocery.

✈️ The Active Traveler (3–5 trips/year, including international)

Willing to plan ahead for award availability. Values airport lounge access. Would like to fly business class once or twice a decade. Has flexibility in travel dates.

Verdict: Travel points wins. You have enough trips to use points, flexibility for award availability, and interest in premium redemptions. Amex Cobalt + Aeroplan + a Visa for Amex gaps is the optimal stack. Potential to get $1,500+ in premium flights from $600 in earned points value.

👶 The Young Professional (No annual trips yet, building savings)

Tight budget, prioritizes financial freedom, earns rewards to offset daily expenses. May not travel for years.

Verdict: Cash back wins. Points that sit for years risk devaluation and program changes. Cash back applies to today's groceries. Start with no-fee cards (Tangerine, BMO eclipse rise). Graduate to fee cards when lifestyle allows.

💼 The Business Traveler (Flies 10+ times/year on company expense)

Company pays for flights. Personal card charges hotel and meals. Keeps personal card points. High spend, high flexibility.

Verdict: Travel points wins decisively. High spend + company-paid base flights = unlimited opportunity to earn points on expense reimbursements. Amex Cobalt or Business Platinum for dining/hotels. Points go toward upgrade redemptions or personal leisure flights. Cash back pales next to the leverage available.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is cash back better than travel points for most Canadians?
For most Canadians — particularly those who travel infrequently, don't have flexibility in travel dates, or prefer simplicity — cash back is likely the better practical choice. Cash back has zero complexity, never devalues, and works for any purchase. Travel points offer theoretically higher returns but require active engagement with points programs to achieve those returns. If you're not willing to research redemption options, cash back will consistently outperform neglected travel points.
Can I convert cash back to travel rewards?
Not directly — cash back cards earn cash, which you can use for anything including travel, but you can't convert dollars to airline points. Some cards blur the line: the Tangerine Money-Back card earns cash, but you spend that cash on whatever you choose (including booking flights directly). This "cash for travel" approach is simpler than managing travel points programs but misses the premium-cabin leverage that transfer partners offer.
Do travel points expire if I don't use them?
Expiry rules vary by program. Aeroplan points do not expire as long as your account shows activity at least once every 12 months (one qualifying transaction is enough). Scene+ points don't expire while your account is active. BMO Rewards and Avion Rewards don't expire while your card account is open. Air Miles cash miles don't expire. Points expiry is rarely a practical concern for active cardholders — but if you're inactive for a year or more, check your program's rules.
