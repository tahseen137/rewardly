The RBC Avion Visa Infinite is Canada's most recognizable travel rewards card — largely because RBC is Canada's largest bank and millions of Canadians hold it by default. But default isn't the same as optimal. We'll cut through the brand familiarity and tell you exactly when this card earns its $120 fee and when it doesn't.

*Avion point value ranges from 1¢ (merchandise/cashback) to 2¢+ (airline redemptions). Wide variance — see Points Value section.

The flat 1 pt/$1 earn rate on everything is the card's core weakness compared to competitors that offer 2–5× on specific categories. A Scotiabank Momentum Visa Infinite earns 4% on groceries, streaming, and recurring bills (2% on gas). The Amex Cobalt earns 5× on dining. The Avion earns 1× everywhere — which is good for simplicity but leaves significant value on the table for category spenders.

Welcome Bonus

The standard welcome offer is 35,000 Avion points — typically 25,000 after first purchase and 10,000 after meeting a spend threshold (around $1,500 in 3 months). At ~1.5¢/pt on airline redemptions, 35,000 points = ~$525 in travel value.

That's a 437% return on the first-year fee — making year one clearly worth it even if you cancel afterward. RBC periodically elevates this to 55,000 points for new customers, especially during January and September promotions.

The key insight: if you always redeem through the RBC Rewards portal at a flat 1¢/pt, you get the same effective return as 1% cashback. To do better, you need to transfer to airline partners — British Airways Avios offers 4,500 point short-haul awards that are excellent value, and Cathay Pacific Asia Miles can unlock business class to Asia at 2–4¢/pt.

Transfer Partners

Avion points transfer to the following at 1:1 unless noted:

  • British Airways Avios — best for short-haul North American awards (YYZ–NYC, YYZ–ORD, etc.) and for accessing oneworld partner airlines
  • Cathay Pacific Asia Miles — excellent for business class to Asia and Oneworld partners
  • Japan Airlines Mileage Bank — great for JAL First and Business class awards
  • WestJet Dollars — simple 1:1, good for domestic flights if you prefer WestJet
  • Aimia (Air Canada Maple Leaf Lounges) — for paying for lounge passes, not flight redemptions

Notably absent: Air Canada Aeroplan. Avion points do NOT transfer to Aeroplan. This is the most significant limitation — for most Canadians who fly Air Canada, the TD or CIBC Aeroplan cards are a better fit because they earn directly in Aeroplan's ecosystem.

If you fly Air Canada primarily, the TD Aeroplan card is the better choice — it earns more Aeroplan miles on everyday spend (1.5× vs 1×), covers your checked bags (saving $120+ per family trip), and directly credits Aeroplan miles to your account. The Avion wins on the welcome bonus value and mobile device insurance, but those are one-time benefits.

If you want flexibility to use non-Air Canada carriers (WestJet, British Airways, JAL), or you want to access oneworld business class sweet spots, the Avion's transfer partners make it the better choice.

Who Should Get This Card

  • RBC banking customers who want one integrated rewards ecosystem — Avion points show up right in RBC Online Banking, and redemptions are frictionless
  • Multi-airline travelers who don't want to lock into Air Canada/Aeroplan
  • British Airways Avios collectors — the 1:1 transfer makes Avion one of the easiest ways to accumulate BA Avios in Canada
  • People who spend evenly across categories — the flat 1×/1.25× rate disadvantages category spenders but benefits those with very diverse purchases
  • New-to-travel-rewards Canadians — the RBC Rewards portal at 1¢/pt is the simplest possible redemption; no partner airline complexity required

Who Should Look Elsewhere

  • Air Canada loyalists — TD Aeroplan or CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite earns more Aeroplan miles per dollar and includes free checked bags
  • Category spenders — Amex Cobalt (5× dining/grocery), Scotiabank Momentum (4× gas/grocery), or a two-card setup will massively outperform
  • International spenders — 2.5% FX fee negates the earn on any foreign currency purchase; pair with a no-FX card
  • Travelers over 64 — no emergency medical coverage; separate travel insurance is required

Final Verdict

Rating: 4.0/5. The RBC Avion Visa Infinite is a solid, flexible travel card — but not the best-earning one in Canada. Its strength is flexibility: points transfer to British Airways, Cathay Pacific, and Japan Airlines, opening premium cabin sweet spots unavailable through Aeroplan. The welcome bonus is the best at this fee tier. But for most Canadians who fly Air Canada and spend heavily on groceries and gas, the TD or CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite delivers more value per dollar spent. Choose Avion for transfer partner flexibility; choose TD/CIBC Aeroplan for Air Canada loyalty and everyday earn rates.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do Avion points expire?

Avion points do not expire as long as your RBC credit card account remains open and in good standing. If you close the card, you have 90 days to redeem any remaining balance before points are forfeited.

Can I transfer Avion points to Aeroplan?

No. RBC Avion points do NOT transfer to Air Canada Aeroplan. The two loyalty programs are competitors. If you want to earn Aeroplan miles, you'll need a TD Aeroplan, CIBC Aeroplan, or American Express card (which transfers MR points to Aeroplan 1:1).

What is the RBC Avion Visa Infinite Privilege?

The Avion Visa Infinite Privilege ($399/yr) is the premium tier offering airport lounge access (Priority Pass), higher earn on travel (1.25× elevated to 1.5×), enhanced travel medical (31 days), and a $100 annual travel enhancement credit. Worth it if you use Priority Pass 4+ times per year and travel frequently.

How do I get the best value from Avion points?

The best value comes from transferring to British Airways Avios for short-haul North American awards (4,500 Avios for short flights in the same zone) or to Cathay Pacific Asia Miles for business class to Asia. Redeeming through RBC's own travel portal at 1¢/pt is convenient but leaves value on the table.

Is the first year free?

RBC occasionally waives the first-year fee during promotions, but it's less frequent than TD's first-year-free offers. The standard offer is paid first year at $120. Check RBC's current promotions before applying — the welcome bonus alone delivers 4–5× the annual fee in value even without a waiver.
