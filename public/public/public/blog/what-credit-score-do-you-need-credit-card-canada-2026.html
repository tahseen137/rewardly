What Credit Score Do You Need for a Credit Card in Canada 2026?

Secured cards: 300+. No-fee rewards: 560+. Standard rewards: 660+. Premium cards (Cobalt, TD Aeroplan VI): 725+. Here's the card-by-card breakdown.

Canadian credit scores range from 300 to 900. Scores above 660 are considered "good" and open the door to most standard rewards cards. Scores above 725 unlock premium rewards cards with strong welcome bonuses and travel perks. The average Canadian credit score is approximately 680–700.

A 730 score with $25K income and 6 recent inquiries may be declined; a 680 score with $120K income and clean history may be approved for the same card.

Beyond the Score: What Else Issuers Look At

Canadian credit card approvals are not score-only decisions. Issuers assess your entire application: income (all major cards have minimum income thresholds, typically $12K–$80K), debt-to-income ratio (how much of your income goes to existing debt payments), number of recent hard inquiries (3+ in 12 months raises flags), derogatory marks (late payments in the last 2 years, collections, consumer proposals), and length of credit history (shorter history = more uncertainty).

American Express is different: Amex approvals in Canada lean heavily on income and credit history length rather than score alone. It's possible to be approved for the Amex Cobalt with a 680 score if you have strong income and 5+ years of clean history, while being declined with a 720 score if you have only 1 year of credit history and recently opened 4 other accounts.

What To Do If Your Score Isn't High Enough Yet

The fastest levers to raise your score: lower your credit utilization (pay balances before statement dates — this alone can move a score 20–50+ points in one cycle), ensure no missed payments are present (set up autopay for at least the minimum to prevent new derogatory marks), and let time pass (account age is 15% of your score — there's no shortcut for this one).

If you're under 650 and want to start building toward rewards cards, begin with a secured card or a no-fee basic card. Use it for one small recurring purchase per month, pay in full automatically, and check your score every 3 months. Most people can reach 700 within 12–18 months of consistent, responsible credit use from a 580–620 starting point.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does applying for a credit card hurt my score?
Yes, but modestly and temporarily. A credit card application triggers a hard inquiry on your credit report, which typically reduces your score by 5–15 points for 6–12 months. The inquiry stays on your report for 3 years but has decreasing impact after the first year. Applying for 3+ cards in a short period has compounding impact and can signal risk to lenders. Space applications at least 3–6 months apart.
Can a high income compensate for a low credit score?
Partially. High income reduces the income-related risk of an application, but it doesn't compensate for poor credit history (missed payments, high utilization, collections). Most rewards cards have both an income threshold AND an implied score threshold. You need to meet both. Income helps more with entry-level and mid-tier cards than with premium cards like Amex Platinum.
If I'm denied, how long should I wait before trying again?
Wait at least 3–6 months before reapplying for the same card. Use that time to improve the factors that likely caused the denial — pay down balances (to lower utilization), ensure all payments are on time, and avoid other new applications. If your denial reason was income-related, there's less waiting required — you may be able to reapply once your income situation changes. Some issuers will tell you the denial reason in the letter; use that information directly.
