Credit Card Cash Advances Canada 2026 — True Cost & Better Alternatives

Cash advances are the most expensive credit card feature. 22.99–24.99% APR with no grace period, plus a 3% fee upfront. There are almost always cheaper options.

What Is a Credit Card Cash Advance?

A credit card cash advance is when you use your credit card to withdraw physical cash — from an ATM, or by depositing it to your bank account. Unlike regular purchases, cash advances have significantly worse terms. Several other transactions are also treated as cash advances by Canadian banks:

ATM cash withdrawals using a credit card. Foreign currency purchases at a currency exchange. Purchasing cryptocurrency, gift cards, or money orders on some cards. Wire transfers or money orders. Casino chips or gambling chips at some institutions. Sending money via PayPal using your credit card (usually treated as a cash advance).

The True Cost — Three Layers of Fees

Cash advances are expensive for three compounding reasons:

1. Higher interest rate: Most Canadian credit cards charge a separate, higher APR for cash advances — typically 22.99% to 24.99% vs. 19.99% for purchases.

2. No grace period: Regular purchases have a grace period (typically 21+ days) where no interest accrues if you pay in full by the due date. Cash advances have NO grace period — interest begins accruing the instant you take the advance, even if you pay your full statement balance on the due date.

3. Cash advance fee: Most Canadian banks charge an upfront fee of 1–3.5% of the advance amount (typically $2.50–$5.00 minimum), charged immediately.

Example: $500 Cash Advance, Repaid After 30 Days

Cash advance fee: $500 × 3% = $15.00

Daily interest rate: 22.99% ÷ 365 = 0.063%/day

30 days of interest: $500 × 0.063% × 30 = $9.45

ATM fee (if non-bank ATM): $2.50–$5.00

Total extra cost: $26.95–$29.45 on a $500 advance repaid in 30 days = 5.4–5.9% effective rate

Example: $500 Cash Advance, Repaid After 90 Days (Making Minimums)

Cash advance fee: $15.00

90 days of interest: $500 × 0.063% × 90 = $28.35

Plus the balance earns compound interest on the accumulated interest...

Total extra cost: $43–$50+ — approximately 9–10% on a 3-month borrowing of $500

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a cash advance affect my credit score?
The cash advance itself doesn't directly impact your credit score more than a regular purchase. However, cash advances increase your credit card balance, which raises your utilization ratio — and high utilization hurts your score. Additionally, if the high interest rate on the cash advance makes it difficult to pay down your balance, the resulting high ongoing utilization and potential missed payments will damage your score over time.
How do I know if a transaction will be treated as a cash advance?
The safest way: check your card's cardholder agreement for the list of "cash equivalent" transactions. As a general rule in Canada: ATM cash withdrawals on a credit card are always cash advances. Gift card purchases, money orders, and cryptocurrency are often cash advances. The merchant category code (MCC) determines how your card treats a purchase — some fintech payment processors code as cash advances even for non-cash transactions. When in doubt, use debit or a prepaid card.
