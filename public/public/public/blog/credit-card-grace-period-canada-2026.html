Credit Card Grace Period in Canada 2026

The interest-free window between your statement date and due date — how it works, what can destroy it, and exactly when you need to pay.

What Is the Grace Period?

The grace period on a Canadian credit card is the interest-free window between your statement closing date and your payment due date. During this window, you owe no interest on new purchases — provided you pay your full statement balance by the due date.

By law, Canadian credit card issuers must provide a minimum 21-day grace period on new purchases. Most major banks give 21–25 days. This means if your statement closes on July 1st and your payment is due July 25th, any purchase made between July 1st and July 25th is effectively interest-free until the following due date.

The 5 Rules of the Grace Period

1

You must pay the FULL statement balance — not just the minimum

The grace period only applies when you pay your entire statement balance by the due date. If you pay the minimum or any partial amount, interest begins accruing from the original purchase dates — retroactively. This is the most misunderstood aspect of credit card interest in Canada.

2

Minimum 21 days — required by Canadian law

Federal regulations require all Canadian credit card issuers to provide at least a 21-day grace period from the statement date to the payment due date. Most major banks (TD, RBC, Scotiabank, CIBC, BMO) give 21–25 days. American Express typically gives 28 days.

3

The grace period applies to purchases — NOT cash advances

Cash advances (ATM withdrawals on your credit card, buying money orders, or cryptocurrency purchases on some cards) have NO grace period. Interest starts accruing the day of the transaction, typically at 22.99%–24.99% — higher than the purchase interest rate.

4

Carrying a balance kills the grace period on NEW purchases

If you carry a balance from a previous month (didn't pay in full), your next month's purchases begin accruing interest immediately — no grace period. The grace period only applies when you start each billing cycle with a $0 balance from the previous statement. This is why carrying a balance is so expensive.

5

Statement date ≠ cycle date — know both

Your statement closing date is when your billing cycle ends and your statement is generated. Your payment due date is typically 21–25 days after that. You have until the due date (not the statement date) to pay without interest. Confusing these two dates is the most common mistake new cardholders make.

What Kills the Grace Period

Carrying Any Balance Forward

This is the most important thing to understand. If you paid less than the full statement balance last month — even $1 short — there is no grace period on your current month's purchases. Every new transaction starts accruing interest at 19.99%–22.99% from the day you make it. To restore your grace period, you need to pay the full balance in full one cycle, then maintain it.

Cash Advances and Cash-Like Transactions

Cash advances have no grace period under any circumstances — even if you pay your full balance every month. This includes: ATM withdrawals on your credit card, buying gift cards at some retailers (coded as quasi-cash), purchasing cryptocurrency on certain exchanges, casino chips, and money orders paid by credit card. Interest on cash advances typically starts the same day at 22.99%–24.99%.

Promotional Balance Transfers

Balance transfer amounts typically do not benefit from the grace period on new purchases. When you carry a balance transfer, some issuers apply your payments to the lowest-interest balance first, meaning your new purchases accrue interest at the full rate. Read the balance transfer offer's fine print carefully.

The Cost of Missing the Grace Period

Canadian credit cards charge 19.99% purchase interest annually, compounded daily. If you carry a $1,000 balance for one month at 19.99%, you pay approximately $16.67 in interest that month. Over 12 months of carrying that balance, that's $200+ in interest — with no rewards value that offsets it.

Critical math: Even the best credit card reward (Amex Cobalt's 5x on dining = ~3.75%) is completely erased if you carry a balance. Rewards cards are designed to reward people who pay in full. If you regularly carry a balance, a low-interest credit card (9.99%–12.99% APR) is more financially sound than a high-rewards card at 19.99%.

Frequently Asked Questions

If my payment is due on the 25th, can I pay on the 25th?

Yes — the payment is due by end of business on the due date. However, be aware that posting times vary. Online bank transfers typically process same-day if initiated before the bank's cut-off time. If you're cutting it close, pay 1–2 days early to be safe. Late payments trigger interest from the statement date AND may result in a late payment fee.

Does paying early give me more of a grace period?

No — paying early doesn't extend your grace period. The grace period is always measured from your statement closing date to your payment due date. Paying on Day 1 or Day 20 of the grace period yields the same interest-free result, as long as you pay the full balance.

What's the difference between statement date and payment due date?

Your statement date (or closing date) is when your billing cycle ends — the date your monthly statement is generated. Your payment due date is the deadline to pay that statement balance without interest — typically 21–25 days later. You should pay the amount on your statement (your statement balance) by your due date, not by your statement date.

I made a large purchase two days before my statement closed. Do I need to pay it this month?

Yes — it will appear on your current statement and is due by your payment due date (21–25 days after the statement closed). If you don't pay the full balance, that purchase starts accruing interest at 19.99%. The timing of your purchase within the billing cycle doesn't change your due date.

Does the grace period apply to Amex cards the same way?

Yes, but American Express typically offers a 28-day grace period — slightly more than the Canadian legal minimum of 21 days. The same rules apply: pay the full statement balance by the due date to avoid all interest. Amex also charges no interest if you pay in full, but charges 20.99%–21.99% on carried balances.
