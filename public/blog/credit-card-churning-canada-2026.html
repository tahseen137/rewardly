Credit Card Churning Canada 2026 — Is It Worth It?

Earning $2,000–$5,000 in welcome bonuses per year by strategically applying for credit cards is legal, common, and achievable — if you know the rules.

What Is Credit Card Churning?

Credit card churning is the practice of applying for credit cards primarily to earn welcome bonuses, meeting the minimum spend requirement, collecting the bonus, and then either keeping the card, downgrading to a no-fee version, or cancelling before the next annual fee. The goal is to maximize the total value of sign-up bonuses earned over time.

In Canada, churning is perfectly legal. Banks disclose eligibility restrictions in their terms, and it's up to you to follow them. The primary constraints are: (1) bank-specific cooling periods that prevent collecting the same bonus twice within 12–24 months, and (2) the impact of hard credit inquiries on your credit score.

Realistic yield from churning: A disciplined churner in Canada can earn $2,000–$4,000/yr in travel and cash rewards from welcome bonuses alone — on top of regular earn from their everyday spending. At 2–3 new cards per year with $500–$1,000 bonuses each, the math works quickly.

How Credit Card Churning Works in Canada

1

Choose a card with a strong welcome bonus

Target cards with welcome bonuses worth at least $300–$500 after meeting minimum spend. Evaluate both the bonus value and the annual fee (first year often free or rebated in offer).

2

Apply and meet minimum spend requirement

Most welcome bonuses require $1,000–$5,000 in spending within the first 3 months. Time your application before a large planned expense (insurance renewal, car repair, travel booking, tuition) to meet the threshold naturally.

3

Collect your bonus

Bonus points or cash back post within 1–8 weeks of meeting the spend requirement. Transfer points to airline programs before cancelling the card.

4

Decide: keep, downgrade, or cancel

Before year 2 annual fee posts: call the bank and ask to downgrade to a no-fee version of the same card (preserving credit history and account age). Or cancel entirely and wait for the cooling period to reset before churning again.

5

Wait for the cooling period, then churn again

Most banks impose a 12–24 month cooling period before you can earn the welcome bonus on the same card again. Use this time to churn other cards from different issuers.

Credit Score Impact of Churning

Every new credit card application generates a "hard inquiry" on your credit report, which typically reduces your score by 5–15 points temporarily. Multiple inquiries in a short period can add up. However:

Hard inquiry (each application)-5 to -15 pts per inquiryFades after 12 months, drops off after 24 months
New credit account (reduces avg age)-5 to -20 pts initiallyRecovers as account ages; builds mix

Credit score reality for churners: A well-managed churner with a 760+ credit score who applies for 2–3 cards per year will typically see their score fluctuate between 720–760 — still well within "excellent" range for all lending purposes. The key: always pay in full on time, keep utilization low, and don't apply for multiple cards in the same month.

Don't churn if you need a mortgage soon: If you're planning to apply for a mortgage within the next 6–12 months, avoid churning. Lenders see multiple recent credit inquiries and new accounts as a risk signal. Pause churning 12 months before any major credit application (mortgage, car loan, business loan).

Frequently Asked Questions

Is credit card churning legal in Canada?
Yes, completely legal. Banks disclose eligibility restrictions in their terms and conditions, and you are entitled to apply for any card you meet the criteria for. There's nothing illegal or fraudulent about applying for a card, earning the welcome bonus, and cancelling or downgrading it. Banks know churning happens — they build expected churn into their acquisition economics. Some issuers (notably Amex) may decline applications from people who've cancelled multiple cards recently, but declining an application is their prerogative, not a legal issue.
Can I manufacture spending to hit the minimum spend requirement?
Manufactured spending — using financial instruments to generate artificial credit card transactions — is a grey area. Common tactics like paying rent via Chexy (which charges a 1–2% fee but gives you full points), buying Visa prepaid gift cards, or paying insurance premiums by credit card are generally accepted and widely practiced. True manufactured spending (buying money orders with credit cards, gift card reselling loops) is against card terms and can result in your account being closed and bonus being clawed back. Stick to organic spending and service-fee-based payments for safe minimum spend.
What happens if I cancel a card after earning the bonus?
Your earned points stay in your loyalty program account (Aeroplan, Scene+, Avion, etc.) after you cancel the credit card — they are not clawed back after the standard 90-day review period. However: if you cancel within 90 days of the bonus posting, some banks will reverse the bonus. And if you cancel a card that has points in a bank-proprietary program (like BMO Rewards or TD Points) rather than a third-party program, those points may be forfeited at cancellation. Transfer points to Aeroplan before cancelling to be safe.
Should I downgrade or cancel my card after churning?
Downgrading (product change to a no-fee version) is usually better than cancelling — it preserves your account age, which helps your credit score length of history factor (15% of your score). Call the bank 1–2 months before your annual fee posts and ask to be "product changed" to a no-fee card in the same family. If no no-fee option exists, cancelling is fine — the credit score impact is minor and recovers within 12 months. Never pay a second year's annual fee on a churned card unless it delivers enough ongoing value to justify it.
