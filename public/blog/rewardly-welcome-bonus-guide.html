Welcome bonuses are the single most powerful lever in Canadian credit card rewards — and most Canadians leave them on the table by applying for the wrong card at the wrong time, or not applying at all.

A well-timed welcome bonus can be worth $500 to $1,500 in travel, cashback, or points value in a single year. Here's how to earn them intelligently.

What Is a Credit Card Welcome Bonus?

A welcome bonus (also called a sign-up bonus or new cardmember bonus) is a lump sum of points, miles, or cashback awarded to new cardholders after they meet a minimum spend threshold within a set window — typically 3 to 6 months.

Example: The American Express Cobalt Card offers 15,000 Membership Rewards points per month for the first 12 months when you spend $500 or more per month (up to 180,000 total MR points). At 1.5–2.5 cents per point for Aeroplan transfers, that's potentially $2,700–$4,500 in travel value over a year — though realistically most people earn closer to $500–$800.

Welcome bonuses matter because they front-load the value of a new card relationship. The first year is almost always the best year for any credit card.

How Welcome Bonuses Work in Canada

Most Canadian welcome bonus offers follow this structure:

  • Minimum spend: You must charge a certain amount within the bonus window (e.g., $3,000 in 3 months)
  • Bonus window: Typically 60–180 days from card approval
  • Bonus type: Points, travel miles, cashback, or a mix
  • First-year fee waiver: Many premium cards waive the annual fee in year one — making the welcome bonus pure upside

The key calculation: Welcome bonus value minus annual fee = net first-year gain.

The Minimum Spend Strategy: Hit the Threshold Without Overspending

The biggest mistake Canadians make with welcome bonuses is changing their spending behavior to hit the minimum threshold — buying things they don't need or paying bills early. That's not the goal.

The goal is to time your application around natural large purchases.

Timing Strategies That Work

  • Apply before a big purchase you were already planning — moving costs (first + last month's rent, movers, furniture), home renovation or appliances, a flight or vacation, car registration, annual insurance premiums
  • Pre-pay annual bills on the card — insurance premiums (if your insurer allows), annual software subscriptions (Microsoft 365, Adobe Creative Cloud, domain renewals), gym memberships
  • Use the card for every day-to-day purchase — for a $3,000/3-month threshold, you need $1,000/month. That's groceries + gas + dining + transit + subscriptions = achievable for most households without changing behavior
  • Pay federal and provincial taxes by credit card — CRA allows credit card payments via third-party processors like Plastiq. You'll pay ~1.5–2.5% processing fee, but if the welcome bonus value exceeds the fee, it's worth it. Best for larger tax bills (self-employed, investment income)
Key rule: Only pursue welcome bonuses on spending you were going to make anyway, paid in full every month. If you carry a balance, bonuses are not worth chasing.

Common Welcome Bonus Pitfalls to Avoid

1. Applying for multiple cards at once (for most people)
Each application triggers a hard inquiry. Multiple applications in 6 months can lower your credit score. Space applications at least 3–6 months apart.
2. Cancelling immediately after earning the bonus
"Churning" is increasingly restricted. Amex now limits cardholders from receiving a welcome bonus if they've held that card in the past 24 months. Read the fine print before applying.
3. Carrying a balance to hit the minimum spend
Credit card interest (typically 19.99–24.99% APR) destroys welcome bonus value in weeks. Never carry a balance to hit a threshold.
4. Missing the minimum spend window
The bonus window starts from card approval — not when you receive or activate it. Set a calendar reminder 3 weeks before your deadline.
5. Not checking for elevated offers
Amex, TD, Scotia, and others periodically run elevated offers (e.g., 60,000 points instead of 45,000). Search for elevated offers through referrals or specific landing pages before applying at standard rate.

The Math: Is a Welcome Bonus Worth It?

Example — TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite:

Welcome bonus: 50,000 Aeroplan points
Annual fee: $139 (waived year 1)
Point value: ~1.8 cents/point for domestic flights
Bonus value: 50,000 × $0.018 = $900 in flight value
First-year net after fee waiver: $900

Year 2: ongoing earn minus $139 fee = still positive if you fly Air Canada regularly.

The takeaway: Year 1 is almost always a no-brainer for fee-waived premium cards with strong welcome bonuses. Year 2 and beyond depends on your spending habits — run the math before keeping the card.

How to Find the Best Welcome Bonus Card for Your Spending

The best welcome bonus isn't the one with the highest face value — it's the one that matches how you'll actually spend to hit the threshold and earn ongoing rewards.

A card with a 50,000-point bonus but $5,000/3-month spend requirement may be harder to hit than a 30,000-point card with $1,500/3-month spend — especially without any large purchases planned.

The right card depends on your monthly spending habits, which categories you're heaviest in, whether you prefer airline-specific or flexible redemptions, and your credit score for premium cards.
