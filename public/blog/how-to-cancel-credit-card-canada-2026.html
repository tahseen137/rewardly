How to Cancel a Credit Card in Canada Without Hurting Your Credit Score

Closing a credit card can hurt your score if you do it wrong. Follow these steps and cancellation will have minimal impact on your Equifax and TransUnion scores.

Does Cancelling a Credit Card Hurt Your Score in Canada?

It can — but it doesn't have to. Cancelling a credit card potentially affects two important credit score factors in Canada: your credit utilization ratio and your average account age. Understanding both helps you minimize the impact.

Step-by-Step: How to Cancel a Credit Card Safely

1

Redeem all rewards points before cancelling

Points, miles, and cash back earned on most Canadian cards are forfeited when the account closes. Log in to your card portal, check your rewards balance, and redeem everything before initiating cancellation. Exceptions: Aeroplan miles are in your Aeroplan account (separate from the card) and are safe. PC Optimum points stay in your PC Optimum account. But BMO Rewards, TD Rewards, CIBC Rewards, Scotia Rewards — these die with the card if not redeemed first.

2

Pay your balance to $0

Ensure the card has a $0 balance before requesting cancellation. Wait for your final statement and pay in full, including any pending transactions that may not have posted yet. You cannot close a card with an outstanding balance — and trying to do so can complicate your credit file.

3

Move all autopay subscriptions to a different card

Before cancelling, audit every automatic payment charged to this card: streaming services (Netflix, Spotify, Amazon Prime, Disney+), utility auto-pays, gym memberships, insurance premiums, cloud storage, software subscriptions. Update each to a different card. Missed payments after card closure can send accounts to collections — which devastates your credit score far more than cancellation ever would.

4

Call the issuer directly — do not cancel online

In Canada, most issuers require you to call the number on the back of the card to close an account. When you call, they will likely offer you a retention offer (fee waiver, bonus points, reduced rate). Consider the offer — if the annual fee is the reason you're cancelling, a one-time waiver may solve the problem without closing the account. If you still want to cancel, confirm the account is closed and ask for a confirmation number.

5

Get written confirmation

Request a written confirmation of account closure — email or mailed letter. Check your credit report (free at Equifax.ca and TransUnion.ca) 30–60 days after closing to confirm the account shows "Closed" status and a $0 balance. Occasionally, errors keep accounts open or report incorrect balances.

6

Destroy the card

Cut the card into multiple pieces (include the chip and magnetic stripe) and dispose of them separately. Do not simply put the intact card in the recycling bin — card skimmers can retrieve and test old card numbers against pending transactions.

When NOT to Cancel — Consider Downgrading Instead

Before cancelling an annual-fee card, ask the issuer if you can downgrade (also called a "product change") to a no-fee version of the same card. For example:

Downgrading vs. cancelling: A product change keeps the account open (preserving your credit history and available limit), just with a different product. This is almost always better for your credit score than outright cancellation. Call the issuer and ask specifically: "Can I do a product change to a no-fee card instead of cancelling?"

How Long Does a Closed Account Stay on Your Credit Report?

In Canada, a closed credit card account in good standing remains on your Equifax and TransUnion credit reports for 6 years from the date of last activity. During those 6 years, the positive payment history (on-time payments) continues to benefit your credit score. After 6 years, the account drops off — which may slightly reduce your average account age.

Negative accounts stay longer: If you had missed payments, collections, or defaults on the card before closing, those negative marks also stay on file for 6 years from the date of the negative event — not from the date of closure.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much will my credit score drop if I cancel a credit card in Canada?
The impact depends on your specific credit profile. If the cancelled card is your oldest account, expect a modest drop of 10–30 points initially as your average account age decreases. If you have a high utilization ratio across remaining cards, the score impact could be higher. If you have many other cards with large limits and long histories, the impact may be negligible. The best way to minimize impact: never cancel your oldest card, always pay down balances on remaining cards before and after cancellation, and consider downgrading instead of cancelling if possible.
Can you cancel a credit card with a balance in Canada?
Generally no — Canadian card issuers will not close an account with an outstanding balance. You must pay the balance to $0 first. After closing, if somehow a small pending transaction posts, it will be charged to the account even after closure — call the issuer and pay it immediately. Leaving a small unpaid balance after card closure is a common mistake that can send accounts to collections unexpectedly.
Should I cancel a credit card I never use?
Usually not — unless it carries an annual fee. An unused no-fee card helps your credit profile by increasing your available credit limit (lowering utilization) and keeping your account history alive. If the card charges an annual fee you're not recouping in benefits, either cancel it, downgrade to no-fee, or negotiate a fee waiver. If you keep an unused card, use it for one small purchase per year and pay it off immediately to keep it from being automatically closed by the issuer for inactivity.
