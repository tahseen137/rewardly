Are Annual Fee Credit Cards Worth It in Canada 2026?

The question isn't whether to pay $120/yr. The question is whether the card earns more than $120/yr in your hands. For most Canadians who put daily spending on their card, the math usually says yes.

The Simple Math

A $0 annual fee card typically earns 1–2% on most purchases. A premium $120 fee card typically earns 3–6% on key categories. The fee pays off when the extra rewards earned exceed the fee amount.

Breakeven Example: $120 Annual Fee Card at 4% vs. No-Fee Card at 1%

The average Canadian household spends roughly $8,000–$15,000/year on credit card purchases (grocery, gas, dining, bills, occasional travel). At 3% extra earn on $12,000 = $360 in additional rewards versus $120 fee = $240 net benefit per year from the fee card.

The Hidden Benefits Value (That Most People Ignore)

Most fee card analyses focus only on reward earning. But premium cards include benefits worth hundreds of dollars per year:

$200–$600+

Travel Insurance (Emergency Medical)

A standalone 2-week travel medical policy for a couple in their 40s: $200–$400. Fee cards with medical coverage effectively include this. A 60-day policy (National Bank WE MC coverage equivalent) would cost $600–$1,200 standalone.

$105–$210

Airport Lounge Access

Scotia Passport VI includes 6 Dragon Pass visits/yr (≈ $35 value each = $210 value). Pay-as-you-go lounge access costs $30–$50/visit. National Bank WE MC's unlimited lounge access could be worth $500+/yr for frequent travelers.

$30–$60

Car Rental Insurance (CDW)

Declining the rental agency's CDW saves $15–$30/day per rental. Two rentals per year = $30–$60 saved. Fee cards with car rental CDW coverage pay for themselves on this alone if you rent cars.

$50

NEXUS Credit (TD Aeroplan VI)

TD Aeroplan VI rebates $50 every 4.5 years for NEXUS membership — effectively $11/yr. Not massive, but real value for NEXUS members (US/Canada trusted traveler program).

$120–$150

First-Year Fee Waiver (Common)

Many premium Canadian cards waive the annual fee in year 1 (Scotia Gold Amex, Scotia Momentum VI, BMO eclipse VI). Year 1 = zero cost. In year 1, you get all the benefits for free and evaluate whether the ongoing fee is worthwhile.

When a Fee Card Is NOT Worth It

Fee card isn't worth it if:
• You pay only the minimum balance each month (interest charges at 19.99% dwarf any rewards earned)
• Your annual card spend is under $4,000–$5,000 total
• You never travel internationally (travel insurance is valueless if you don't use it)
• You shop primarily at one store that has a co-branded no-fee card (like PC Financial at Shoppers)
• You consistently forget to pay the annual fee early enough to avoid interest
• The card's program is misaligned with your spending (e.g., a dining card when you primarily cook at home)

How to Evaluate if Your Current Fee Card Is Worth Keeping

Annual retention checklist:
1. Download your annual reward statement (most banks provide this)
2. Add up total rewards earned in the past 12 months
3. Add up the dollar value of benefits used (insurance claims, lounges visited × $35, rentals where CDW was used)
4. Compare total value to annual fee
5. If total value < fee: cancel or downgrade before the next fee charge
6. If total value > fee: keep it — you're getting a positive return

💡 Most banks will waive the fee for a year or offer a retention bonus (points or fee credit) if you call and say you're considering canceling. Always call the retention line before canceling a card you've held for 2+ years.

The Year-1 Welcome Bonus Math

Fee cards almost universally offer a welcome bonus (typically 20,000–80,000 points or $200–$500 in rewards) for meeting a minimum spend in the first 3 months. In year 1, the welcome bonus can dwarf the annual fee:

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I cancel a fee card before the annual fee hits or after?
Cancel before the annual fee is charged if you can. Timing matters: if you cancel within 30 days of the fee posting, most Canadian banks will refund the fee pro-rated or in full. Call your bank to confirm their specific policy. Canceling well before the renewal date (30+ days before the anniversary month) avoids the fee entirely.
Does canceling a credit card hurt my credit score?
Canceling a card reduces your total available credit, which increases your credit utilization ratio — this can temporarily lower your score. The impact is greater if the canceled card had a high limit or if you don't have other cards with available credit. However, the effect is typically small and temporary (3–6 months). If the card is one of your oldest accounts, canceling may also shorten your average credit history length, which has a minor long-term effect.
Can I downgrade to a no-fee version instead of canceling?
For many cards, yes. Most issuers allow you to "product change" (downgrade) from a fee card to a no-fee version from the same bank — for example, from TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite to the no-fee TD Aeroplan Visa. This preserves your credit history and available credit while eliminating the fee. Call your bank and ask about downgrade options before canceling outright.
