Credit Card Purchase Protection & Extended Warranty Canada 2026

Two of the most underused credit card benefits. Your card may already cover your next expensive purchase — from theft to breakdown — at no extra cost.

Purchase Protection vs. Extended Warranty — The Difference

These are two distinct benefits often bundled on the same card, but they cover different events at different times.

🛡️ Purchase Protection

  • Covers theft, loss, or accidental damage
  • Applies from the date of purchase
  • Typically lasts 90–120 days
  • Examples: laptop stolen, TV dropped and cracked
  • You pay for the item on your covered card

🔧 Extended Warranty

  • Extends the manufacturer's warranty
  • Activates after manufacturer warranty ends
  • Typically adds 1 extra year
  • Examples: fridge breaks in year 2 of a 1-year warranty
  • Covers manufacturing defects — not accidents

Key requirement for both benefits: You must pay for the item (at least partially) with your eligible credit card. If you pay cash, debit, or a different card, neither benefit applies.

What IS and ISN'T Covered

Purchase Protection — Covered

  • ✓Theft (must file police report)
  • ✓Accidental damage (dropped/broken)
  • ✓Loss of item
  • ✓Vandalism

Purchase Protection — NOT Covered

  • ✗Items left unattended in a car
  • ✗Normal wear and tear
  • ✗Motorized vehicles, boats
  • ✗Animals, live plants
  • ✗Medical equipment
  • ✗Cash, gift cards, tickets
  • ✗Items used professionally/commercially

Extended Warranty — Covered

  • ✓Manufacturing defects
  • ✓Component failures (not from misuse)
  • ✓Items with original manufacturer warranty

Extended Warranty — NOT Covered

  • ✗Accidental damage (use purchase protection)
  • ✗Items without original manufacturer warranty
  • ✗Software, consumables, batteries
  • ✗Items with warranties longer than 5 years
  • ✗Motorized vehicles, boats
  • ✗Commercial-use items

How to Make a Claim — Step by Step

1

Act Immediately — Note the Date

Most claims must be filed within 30–60 days of the incident (theft, damage, or breakdown). Don't wait. Check your cardholder certificate for the exact deadline — Amex is usually 30 days from the incident.

2

File a Police Report (Theft/Loss Only)

For theft or loss claims, most card insurers require a police report filed within 48–72 hours of discovering the loss. Without it, your claim will be denied. Keep the report number.

3

Gather Your Documentation

You'll need: (a) original receipt or credit card statement showing the purchase, (b) card benefit certificate/guide showing coverage, (c) photos of the damaged item if applicable, (d) police report number if applicable, (e) for extended warranty: original manufacturer warranty document.

4

Contact the Insurance Provider (NOT Your Credit Card Issuer)

Credit card purchase protection is administered by a third-party insurer (e.g., Amex Global Assist, Travelers Insurance for TD). The number is on the back of your card or in your cardholder certificate. Call them — not your bank's general customer service line.

5

Submit and Follow Up

Submit all documentation as directed (mail, email, or online portal). Keep copies of everything. Claims typically resolve in 5–15 business days. For extended warranty claims, the insurer may arrange repair rather than replacement.

Critical tip: Register expensive purchases in a spreadsheet when you buy them — note the purchase date, price, and which card you used. When something breaks or gets stolen months later, you won't remember which card you paid with. Most people lose benefits simply because they can't prove they paid with the covered card.

Extended Warranty — Real Dollar Value

Extended warranty plans sold at retail (AppleCare, Best Buy Totaltech, extended warranty add-ons at checkout) typically cost 15–25% of the item's purchase price. On a $1,200 laptop, that's $180–$300 for one extra year.

Your credit card's extended warranty provides this same 1-year extension for free — as long as you paid with your card. On large electronics purchases, this benefit alone can be worth hundreds of dollars per year.

Best approach for large electronics: Decline the retailer's extended warranty at checkout (save $180–$300), pay with your credit card that includes extended warranty coverage, and rely on the card's 1-year extension instead. Document the original manufacturer warranty period in a note or spreadsheet.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does extended warranty double the manufacturer's warranty or add 1 year?
For most Canadian credit cards: it adds 1 year, up to doubling the original warranty (whichever is less). On a 1-year manufacturer warranty, you get 2 years total. On a 2-year manufacturer warranty, you get 3 years total (up to 1 extra year added). Some cards cap the total extended period at 1 year regardless of original warranty length. Amex Cobalt adds up to 1 year. Always confirm by reading your cardholder certificate.
Does the item need to be new? What about used or refurbished purchases?
Most cards require the item to be "new" at the time of purchase and to have an original manufacturer warranty. Certified refurbished items with a manufacturer warranty (such as Apple Certified Refurbished) typically qualify. Second-hand items from private sellers without manufacturer warranties typically do not qualify for extended warranty, but may still qualify for purchase protection against theft or accidental damage within the protection window.
Can I use purchase protection if I used points or cash back to pay for part of the purchase?
Yes — as long as the remaining balance (or any part of the purchase) was charged to your credit card, the full purchase price is typically eligible for purchase protection up to the per-item maximum. If you redeemed $200 in cash back against a $1,200 laptop and paid $1,000 on your card, the full $1,200 laptop is covered (up to the per-item maximum, typically $1,000).
What happens if my item was partially covered by home insurance AND my credit card protection?
Credit card purchase protection is typically secondary to home insurance — meaning the credit card insurer pays after your home insurance pays (the balance up to the credit card limit). If a stolen $2,000 laptop is covered by a $1,000 home insurance payout with a $500 deductible, your net from home insurance is $500. The credit card may cover the remaining $1,000 (up to its per-item maximum). However, this means you need to file with your home insurer first — which may affect your home insurance premium. Many people choose to use credit card purchase protection instead of home insurance for lower-value items to avoid premium increases.
