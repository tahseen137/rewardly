The WFH Spending Profile

Remote workers in Canada accumulate a distinct spending pattern that most credit card category guides don't address directly. Work-from-home expenses span electronics (monitors, keyboards, webcams), internet service, software subscriptions (Microsoft 365, Zoom, Adobe, Slack), office supplies, ergonomic furniture, and increasingly — coworking space day passes when the home office gets claustrophobic.

None of these categories earn bonus points on traditional grocery or dining cards. The WFH worker needs a different toolkit.

WFH Expense Categories: What's Best for Each

💻

Electronics & Home Office Equipment

Monitors, laptops, keyboards, webcams, headsets, standing desks, and office chairs typically code as electronics/general merchandise. No major Canadian card offers a specific electronics bonus category. Best approach: Rogers Red (2% flat) or Amex SimplyCash Preferred (2% flat). For big purchases ($1,000+ monitor, ergonomic chair), time it to hit a new card's welcome bonus threshold.

Best card: Rogers Red (2% flat) — use for welcome bonus timing
🌐

Internet Service

WFH workers often upgrade to faster internet tiers — this is a recurring monthly expense coded as utilities/telecom. Rogers Red earns 2% base (3% for Rogers customers paying Rogers internet). Tangerine's Recurring Bill Payments category earns 2% and is explicitly designed for internet bills. Consistent monthly expense that adds up.

Best card: Rogers Red (2–3%) or Tangerine Recurring Bill Payments (2%)
📦

Office Supplies (Online Purchases)

Paper, ink, pens, notebooks, USB cables — often bought on Amazon or Staples online. If purchased on Amazon.ca, the BMO Eclipse Visa Infinite earns 5x (Amazon is in its bonus category). Best Credit Card for Amazon Canada is a full guide — but for general office supplies online, the key is treating Amazon purchases separately from generic stationery store visits.

Best card: BMO Eclipse VI or Rogers card for Amazon orders (5x / 2%)
🖥️

Software Subscriptions (Microsoft 365, Zoom, Adobe, etc.)

Monthly SaaS subscriptions are recurring charges coded as digital services or software. Amex Cobalt earns 1x Amex MR on these (base rate — not dining/groceries/streaming). Rogers Red earns 2% flat. Tangerine can include subscription services in its Recurring Bill Payments 2% category. BMO Eclipse VI earns 5x on select streaming — but not general software.

Best card: Rogers Red (2% flat) — software doesn't fit any specific bonus category on most cards
☕

Coworking Spaces & Coffee Shops

Day passes at WeWork, Regus, or local coworking spaces typically code as commercial real estate services or office services — base rate on all cards. Coffee shop sessions (working from a café) code as dining — Amex Cobalt earns 5x on all dining including cafés, making it excellent for this use case.

For cafés: Amex Cobalt (5x) | For coworking spaces: Rogers Red (2% flat)
📚

Professional Development (Online Courses, Books)

Udemy, LinkedIn Learning, Coursera, and professional books on Amazon — these are digital/online purchases. If bought on Amazon.ca, BMO Eclipse VI earns 5x. If through the platform's own website, it's typically base rate. The Amex Cobalt earns 1x on these (not a bonus category). Rogers Red 2% flat is again the default winner.

Best card: BMO Eclipse VI for Amazon-purchased courses/books, otherwise Rogers Red

The Best Overall Cards for WFH Workers

BEST OVERALL — FLAT RATE

Rogers Red World Elite Mastercard

For the WFH worker whose expenses span multiple categories without clear bonus buckets, Rogers Red's 2% flat cashback on everything wins by default. No tracking, no categories to optimize, no annual fee. Your internet bill, office equipment, SaaS subscriptions, and ergonomic desk all earn the same 2%.

The income requirement ($80K personal / $150K household) disqualifies some workers. But for WFH professionals who qualify, Rogers Red is the simplest, most consistent choice for the fragmented WFH spending profile.

BEST FOR MIXED WFH + DINING

Amex Cobalt Card

If you work from cafés often or expense client lunches, Cobalt's 5x on dining dominates every other card. Pair it with Rogers Red: use Cobalt for café work sessions and client meals (5x), Rogers Red for equipment and internet (2%). This two-card stack captures the highest earn across all WFH categories.

Cobalt's monthly fee structure ($12.99/month = $155.88/year) means you need to spend enough on 5x categories to justify it. At a minimum, $260/month in dining to break even versus Rogers Red's 2%.

BEST FOR AMAZON OFFICE SUPPLIES

BMO Eclipse Visa Infinite

WFH workers who buy most of their office supplies, electronics accessories, and online courses through Amazon.ca get outsized value from BMO Eclipse VI's Amazon bonus category (5x points). Combined with Rogers Red for non-Amazon purchases, this covers the majority of WFH spend at above-market earn rates.

BMO Eclipse VI's 5x Amazon category includes everything from USB hubs to ergonomic accessories to Kindle books — the full range of "work supplies bought on Amazon" that WFH workers accumulate.

WFH Expenses as Business Expenses: Double the Benefit

If you're self-employed or a sole proprietor working from home, WFH expenses like internet, home office equipment, and a portion of utilities may be deductible on your Canadian tax return (Form T2125 — Statement of Business Activities). This creates a double benefit: you earn credit card rewards on the purchase AND potentially deduct the expense from your taxable income.

CRA rule: You can deduct the business-use portion of home office expenses if your home is where you principally carry on your business, or if you use it exclusively for meeting clients/customers. A dedicated home office used 40% of the time means 40% of applicable expenses may be deductible. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

For T4 employees who work from home, CRA allows a simplified flat-rate deduction of $2 per day (up to $400/year) or a detailed method using Form T777. Neither of these affects your ability to earn credit card rewards — you earn the rewards regardless of how you treat the expense for tax purposes.
