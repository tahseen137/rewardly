How EV Charging Codes on Credit Cards

This is where EV charging gets interesting — and frustrating. Unlike gasoline, which almost universally codes as MCC 5541 (Service Stations) or 5542 (Automated Fuel Dispensers), EV charging has no single dedicated MCC. Different networks use different codes, and even the same network can vary by location.

Common MCCs Used by EV Charging Networks

MCC Category Description Used by
5541 Service Stations / Gas Stations Some ChargePoint locations, FLO (inconsistent)
5552 Electric Vehicle Charging Stations Dedicated EV MCC — increasingly used by ChargePoint, Blink, some FLO
4900 Utilities (Electric, Gas, Water) Some home charging via utility billing, rare for public networks
5999 Miscellaneous Retail Fallback when network doesn't have a specific MCC
7521 Automobile Parking Lots/Garages Some mall-based charging stations that are part of parking payment

The critical implication: a card that earns bonus points on "gas" (MCC 5541/5542) MAY capture some EV charging sessions but NOT all — especially those that code as MCC 5552 or 5999. Cards with broad everyday spending multipliers are more reliable for EV charging.

Major Canadian Charging Networks and Typical MCCs

Network Typical MCC Notes
ChargePoint 5552 or 5541 Most widely deployed in Canada. Location-dependent MCC. Best to test one transaction first.
FLO (AddÉnergie) 5541 or 5552 Major Canadian network, especially strong in Quebec. Has shifted toward 5552 on newer installations.
Tesla Supercharger 5552 Tesla uses MCC 5552 consistently. Does NOT code as gas. Only Tesla vehicles can use Superchargers.
Electrify Canada 5552 DC fast charger network. Typically 5552 — not gas category.
Ivy Charging 5552 Ontario-based network (Hydro One + OPG). Uses MCC 5552.
SEMA Connect 7521 or 5999 Often co-located at parking facilities — may code as parking rather than EV charging.
Blink Charging 5552 Increasingly using dedicated 5552 MCC in Canada.
Why MCC 5552 matters now: Visa and Mastercard created MCC 5552 specifically for electric vehicle charging stations. As the EV ecosystem matures, more networks are migrating to this code. A card that bonuses "gas" (5541/5542) increasingly will NOT capture EV charging as a bonus transaction — while cards with general everyday spending multipliers will.

Home Charging: Earn on Your Electricity Bill

For most Canadian EV owners, home charging accounts for 70–80% of total charging costs. Your home electricity bill typically codes under MCC 4900 (Utilities). This is where utility bonus categories shine.

★
Tangerine Money-Back Card (Utilities Category)
Best for home charging — 2% on utilities
2% cashback on utilities (one of 2-3 chosen categories)

The Tangerine card lets you choose any 2 (or 3) spending categories for 2% cashback, including a specific "Utilities" category that captures electricity bills (MCC 4900). If home EV charging represents a significant portion of your monthly electricity bill, assigning the Utilities category to Tangerine earns you 2% on that spend. No annual fee. The limitation: only 0.5% on public charging (which is non-utility MCC).

The Rogers Red Mastercard also earns 2% on electricity bills as part of its flat 2% rate — and doesn't require you to use up a valuable category selection.

Home charging math: If your EV uses 15 kWh/100km, you drive 15,000km/year, and Ontario hydro is ~14¢/kWh, your home charging costs roughly $315/year. At 2% cashback, that's $6.30/year back. Not huge, but the same card likely earns more on groceries and dining — the utility category is a bonus earn on existing bill spend.

Optimal Strategy for Canadian EV Owners

The Two-Card Setup

Given MCC inconsistency across networks, the optimal approach uses two cards strategically:

Public charging (uncertain MCC): Rogers Red Mastercard
→ 2% cashback regardless of MCC (5541, 5552, 5999, 7521)
 
Home electricity bill: Tangerine (Utilities 2%) or Rogers Red (2%)
 
Result: 2% guaranteed on all EV charging, no MCC gamble

The High-Earn Gamble

If you primarily use ChargePoint or FLO (which sometimes code as gas MCC 5541), putting those sessions on a BMO World Elite (5% gas) or Scotia Gold Amex (3x gas) can yield a much higher earn rate. Test one small transaction first to confirm the MCC, then deploy accordingly.

RFID Cards and Mobile Wallets

Some charging networks (particularly ChargePoint and FLO) let you use RFID fobs or their app to initiate charging, then bill your linked credit card. The rewards still go to your linked card — make sure the right card is set as the default payment method in each charging app.

Tesla Supercharger payment: Tesla Superchargers in Canada are paid via your Tesla account (linked credit card), not by tapping at the charger. Log into your Tesla account and set the Rogers Red or your best flat-rate card as your default payment method to guarantee 2% on all Supercharger sessions.

Track EV-Specific Incentives by Province

Some provincial utilities and EV incentive programs offer bill credits or point systems for EV owners — separate from credit card rewards. BC Hydro's EV rates, Hydro-Québec's off-peak charging discounts, and Ontario's TOU pricing all affect the total cost of EV ownership. Optimize your credit card rewards within whatever charging structure your utility offers.

Bottom Line

EV charging in Canada is a rewards category without a single winning answer — because there's no single MCC. The practical recommendation:

Rogers Red Mastercard is the safest and simplest choice: 2% on every transaction regardless of MCC, no annual fee, no foreign transaction fees. For Rogers/Fido subscribers, it wins on public charging across every network.

For home charging, add a Tangerine Money-Back Card with the Utilities category selected — 2% on your electricity bill at zero annual fee.

If you're willing to test MCCs and potentially earn more, the BMO World Elite (5% gas) or Scotia Gold Amex (3x gas) can outperform on ChargePoint/FLO sessions that code as MCC 5541 — but carry a backup for the sessions that don't.
