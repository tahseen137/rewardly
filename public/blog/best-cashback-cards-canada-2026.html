What Makes a Great Cashback Card?

Three things matter:

  • Effective rate. The actual percentage you get back after accounting for annual fees.
  • Category coverage. Does it reward where you actually spend? Groceries and gas matter more than "select boutique hotels."
  • No hoops. Automatic cash back beats quarterly category registration or redemption minimums.

How to Pick the Right One

If you spend heavily on groceries and gas

Look for cards with 3-5% on those categories. The annual fee pays for itself if you spend $200+/month on groceries.

If you want simplicity

Go for flat-rate cashback (1.5-2% on everything). No category tracking, no quarterly activations. Swipe and forget.

If you're fee-sensitive

Plenty of no-fee cards offer 1-2% on specific categories. The returns won't blow your mind, but $0 annual fee means you can't lose.

Cashback vs Points: When Does Cash Win?

Cashback makes sense if:

  • You don't travel enough to justify premium travel cards ($400+ annual fees)
  • You prefer liquidity over redemption flexibility
  • You don't want to track point valuations or transfer ratios

Points cards win when you're churning sign-up bonuses or redeeming for business/first-class flights. For everyday spending? Cashback is often simpler and nearly as valuable.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Paying an annual fee for a card you don't use enough

A $120 annual fee requires $4,000 in bonus-category spending (at 3% cash back) just to break even vs a no-fee card.

2. Ignoring foreign transaction fees

That 2% cashback card charges 2.5% on USD purchases. You're losing money.

3. Not checking if Costco/Walmart count as "groceries"

Most cards classify them as "warehouse" or "superstore" — different category, lower rate. Check the fine print.
