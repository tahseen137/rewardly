How to Increase Your Credit Limit in Canada 2026

A higher credit limit lowers your utilization ratio, boosts your credit score, and gives you more spending flexibility for welcome bonuses. Here's exactly how to request one.

Why Request a Credit Limit Increase?

A credit limit increase has two concrete benefits: it gives you more purchasing power, and it lowers your credit utilization ratio — the percentage of your available credit you're using — which is a significant component of your Canadian credit score.

Credit utilization accounts for roughly 30% of your Equifax and TransUnion credit scores. If your limit is $5,000 and you typically spend $2,000/month on the card, you're at 40% utilization — higher than the recommended 10–30% range. Increasing the limit to $10,000 with the same $2,000 spending drops you to 20%, which can improve your score by 20–40 points.

Credit score impact of utilization: Under 10% utilization = excellent. 10–30% = good. 30–50% = fair. Over 50% = negative impact. A credit limit increase instantly lowers your utilization percentage without changing your spending behaviour.

Why Banks Grant Credit Limit Increases

📈

Rising income

If your income has increased since you applied for the card, updating this information and requesting a limit increase is often approved quickly. Banks want to lend to people who can repay — higher income signals higher repayment capacity.

✅

Strong payment history

6+ months of on-time, full-balance payments on your card is the strongest signal. Banks reward reliable customers with higher limits. If you've never missed a payment, you're an ideal candidate.

📊

Improved credit score

If your score has increased significantly (60+ points) since you opened the card, you present lower risk and may qualify for a higher limit. Most issuers will do a soft pull to check your current score for a limit increase request.

💳

Higher spending needs

Telling the bank you're hitting your limit regularly due to business expenses or planned large purchases gives them a reason to increase it. Banks prefer customers who use their cards frequently — it generates interchange revenue.

Hard Inquiry vs. Soft Inquiry for Limit Increases

This is the most important consideration before requesting a limit increase: some banks use a hard inquiry (which affects your credit score by 3–10 points) while others use a soft inquiry (which does not affect your score at all).

How to Request a Credit Limit Increase — Step by Step

1

Check your current utilization

Calculate your average monthly spend divided by your current limit. If you're consistently above 30%, a limit increase is justified. Below 10%? The bank may question why you need more credit.

2

Update your income information

Log into your bank's online banking or app and update your income before requesting. If your income has increased, this is the most impactful update you can make before a limit increase request.

3

Choose the right channel

Online/app requests are often processed with soft inquiries. Phone requests sometimes use hard inquiries. Start with the online banking or mobile app option — most major Canadian banks now support this.

4

Request a specific amount

Don't just ask for "more credit" — ask for a specific limit. A reasonable ask is 25–50% more than your current limit, or to a round number (e.g., from $8,000 to $12,000). Asking for double or triple your current limit in one request often results in denial.

5

Bank-by-bank instructions

TD: Online banking → Credit Cards → Credit Limit Increase. RBC: RBC App → Credit Cards → Manage → Request Limit Increase. Scotia: Scotia OnLine → My Accounts → Account Details. CIBC: CIBC Online Banking → Account Services. BMO: BMO Online Banking → My Accounts → Account Services.

6

If denied, wait and retry

Denials are common on first requests and don't mean you'll never get a higher limit. Wait 6 months, continue paying in full, and try again. You can also call the retention department and ask what would qualify you for a higher limit.

When NOT to Request a Credit Limit Increase

Don't request if you're planning to apply for a mortgage, car loan, or new credit card in the next 3–6 months. A hard inquiry from a limit increase request can reduce your score at the worst possible time. Wait until after the major credit application is approved.

Also avoid requesting immediately after a job change to a lower-income role, after multiple recent credit applications, or if your credit score has declined recently. The bank reviews your full credit profile on limit increase requests — a weak profile that didn't exist when you first applied will be visible now.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often can I request a credit limit increase?

Most banks allow requests every 6 months. Requesting too frequently can signal financial distress. Once every 12 months is a reasonable cadence for most cardholders. Some banks proactively review and increase limits for consistently good customers without requiring a request.

Will a credit limit increase hurt my credit score?

If the bank uses a soft inquiry, there is no impact on your score. If they use a hard inquiry, expect a temporary 3–10 point drop. However, if the increased limit lowers your utilization, your score will typically recover and exceed the pre-request level within 1–3 months.

What if the bank proactively offers me an increase?

Proactively offered increases from the bank (via email or statement notice) almost always use soft inquiries — or no inquiry at all, since the bank has already pre-approved the increase based on your account history. Accept these offers — they're free credit score improvements.

Should I accept automatic limit increases from Amex?

Yes. American Express Canada regularly proactively increases limits for cardholders in good standing. These are soft pulls and increase your total available credit — both of which benefit your utilization ratio and credit score. There's no reason to decline a proactive Amex increase.
