How to Increase Your Credit Card Limit Canada 2026

A credit limit increase can raise your credit score by 20–40 points if it reduces your utilization — and many Canadian banks will approve online requests with no hard inquiry.

Why a Credit Limit Increase Helps Your Score

Credit utilization — your balance divided by your total credit limit — accounts for approximately 30% of your Canadian credit score. The lower your utilization, the better your score. A limit increase lowers your utilization without you spending differently.

Example: You have a $5,000 limit and typically carry $1,500 in balances — that's 30% utilization. If your limit increases to $10,000 with the same spending, your utilization drops to 15% — a significant improvement that can reflect in your score within 30–60 days (the next time your bank reports to Equifax/TransUnion).

Hard Pull vs. Soft Pull — Critical Difference

This is the most important thing to know before requesting a limit increase. Some banks do a hard inquiry (hard pull) — which temporarily lowers your credit score by 5–15 points. Others do a soft inquiry (soft pull) — which has no impact on your score. Always ask or check the bank's policy before requesting.

Step-by-Step: How to Request a Limit Increase

1

Wait at least 6 months after opening the card

Banks rarely approve limit increases for cards less than 6 months old. Many recommend waiting 12 months for the best success rate. The longer your payment history on the card, the stronger your case.

2

Pay your balance in full for 3+ consecutive months first

Banks are more likely to approve an increase if your recent statements show full payment. Carrying high balances right before requesting signals financial stress — the opposite of what you want to show.

3

Log in to your bank's app or website

Find "Credit Limit Increase" in your credit card management section. At most Big Six banks it's under Account Services → Credit Limit → Request Increase. Online requests are faster and typically use soft pulls.

4

Enter your requested limit and updated income

Banks ask for your current annual income. If your income has increased since you opened the card, updating it can improve your approval odds. Request an amount 25–50% higher than your current limit for best results (very large jumps are more likely to require a hard pull or be denied).

5

Accept the offer (if instant) or wait 1–3 business days

Many banks provide an instant decision. If approved, your new limit takes effect immediately. If pending, you'll receive a decision via mail or email within 3–5 business days.

Don't immediately request a limit increase after a new card application: Applying for a new card already triggered a hard inquiry. Requesting a limit increase on that new card shortly after adds another inquiry — compounding the credit score impact. Wait at least 6 months after opening a new card before requesting its first increase.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often can I request a credit limit increase in Canada?
Most Canadian banks allow limit increase requests every 6–12 months. Requesting too frequently signals financial stress. The general advice: request once per year, when your income or financial situation has materially improved, or when your utilization is consistently high due to legitimate spending growth (not because you're overspending). Automatic increases from the bank don't count toward this calendar.
Will a credit limit increase hurt my credit score?
If done via online request at a major Canadian bank (soft pull), the request itself doesn't hurt your score. If the increase is approved, your utilization drops — which can improve your score. The only risk is if the bank uses a hard pull for the request. Always confirm before requesting. If you're unsure, online requests at the Big Six are generally safe.
What's the maximum credit limit I can request?
There's no published maximum, but banks consider your total available credit across all cards (not just this one card) relative to your income. If you already have $50,000 in total credit limits and are requesting to bring a single card to $30,000, the bank may decline due to concentration risk. Spreading credit across multiple cards is generally easier than getting a single card to a very high limit.
