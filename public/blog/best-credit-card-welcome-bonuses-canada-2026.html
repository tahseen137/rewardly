How to Evaluate a Welcome Bonus

Step 1: Calculate the actual dollar value

Not all points are equal. 50,000 Aeroplan miles redeemed for a business class flight can be worth $2,000+. The same 50,000 points cashed out for gift cards might only yield $350. Always calculate the value based on how you'll actually use the points, not the face number.

Step 2: Check the spend requirement realistically

A 60,000 MR bonus requiring $10,000 in 3 months is only accessible to people who genuinely spend that much. Forcing artificial spending to hit a threshold (buying gift cards you wouldn't otherwise buy) is a common trap that costs more in cash than the bonus is worth. Calculate your natural monthly spend and verify the threshold is achievable organically.

Step 3: Account for the annual fee

First-year fee waivers are worth real money. A $139 fee waived in year 1 is a $139 bonus stacked on top of the points offer. Factor this in to your first-year value calculation. In year 2, you need to re-evaluate whether the ongoing earn rate justifies keeping the card.

Step 4: Check if you're eligible

Most issuers restrict welcome bonuses to "new" customers or customers who haven't held that product in the last 12–24 months. If you cancelled a TD Aeroplan card 6 months ago and reapply, you likely won't receive the welcome bonus. Read the eligibility terms before applying.

Welcome Bonus Strategy: Churning vs. Keeping

"Churning" — applying for cards specifically for the sign-up bonus and then cancelling — is a practice some Canadians use to accumulate points quickly. It is legal, but comes with considerations:

  • Multiple hard inquiries can temporarily reduce your credit score
  • Many issuers now have rules restricting repeat applications (e.g., "not eligible if you've held this card in the past 24 months")
  • Cancelling cards affects your average account age, which is a factor in your credit score
  • Some premium perks (like lounge access or companion passes) require active card ownership year-round to use

A more sustainable approach: apply for 1–2 new cards per year, use them for ongoing earn, and hold them for 2–3 years before considering a product change or upgrade.

⚠️ Don't apply for multiple cards at once. Each application is a hard inquiry. Applying for 3 cards in 30 days sends a negative signal to lenders and stacks hard inquiries. Space applications at least 3–6 months apart.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get the welcome bonus on a card I already have?

No. Welcome bonuses are for new cardholders. If you already hold the card, you are not eligible. However, if you cancelled the card and a sufficient time has passed (the eligibility window varies by issuer — often 12–24 months), you may be eligible as a "new" applicant when you reapply.

Do referral bonuses stack with welcome bonuses?

Sometimes. If a friend refers you and you apply through their referral link, you may receive additional bonus points on top of the standard welcome offer. The referring friend also earns points. Always check whether the referral bonus and standard welcome bonus stack or if only one applies.

When are welcome bonus points deposited?

Timing varies. Many offers deposit the welcome bonus once you hit the spending threshold. Others distribute monthly (like Amex Cobalt's monthly bonus structure). Some issuers post the bonus in the first statement after qualifying; others may take 6–8 weeks after meeting the requirement.

What is the best welcome bonus for someone getting their first travel card?

The TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite or CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite is an excellent starting point for travel beginners. The $139 first-year fee is often waived, the 50,000 Aeroplan welcome bonus is substantial and the spend requirement ($7,500 over 6 months = $1,250/month) is achievable for most working Canadians. Aeroplan also has an easy-to-use search tool to find awards.
