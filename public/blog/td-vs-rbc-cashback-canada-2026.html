TD and RBC are Canada's two largest banks by assets — and both offer cashback credit cards that millions of Canadians carry. But which one actually earns more? The answer depends on how much you spend, what you spend on, and whether you're comfortable paying an annual fee.

We ran the numbers so you don't have to. Here's the full breakdown of TD's Cash Back Visa Infinite vs. RBC's Cash Back Mastercard — earn rates, caps, redemption mechanics, and the scenarios where each card wins.

TD Cash Back Visa Infinite — Deep Dive

The TD Cash Back Visa Infinite is TD's premium cashback card. The headline rate is 3% back on groceries, gas, and recurring bills — a strong earn rate by Canadian standards. The "recurring bills" category covers pre-authorized payments like streaming services (Netflix, Crave, Disney+), phone plans (Rogers, Bell, Telus), internet, and gym memberships — expanding the 3% earning potential significantly.

TD does not publicly disclose a spending cap on the 3% categories, which is a significant advantage over RBC. For heavy spenders, this card can generate substantial cashback without hitting a ceiling.

Annual Fee and Income Requirements

The card carries a $120 annual fee, waived in the first year. You'll need a minimum personal income of $60,000 (or $100,000 household) to qualify — standard for Visa Infinite products in Canada. If you don't meet the income threshold, TD offers a lower-tier TD Cash Back Visa with reduced earn rates and a lower fee.

Insurance and Extra Benefits

Beyond cashback, TD Visa Infinite includes travel accident insurance (up to $1 million), delayed and lost baggage coverage, rental car insurance, and mobile device protection (up to $1,000). These aren't best-in-class travel benefits, but they add real value for occasional travellers who don't want a separate travel card.

Cashback Redemption

Cashback is automatically credited to your statement once per year in October, or you can redeem at any time in $25 increments through TD's online banking portal. This flexibility beats RBC's annual-only credit.

RBC Cash Back Mastercard — Deep Dive

The RBC Cash Back Mastercard is a no-annual-fee card aimed at everyday Canadians who want simple cashback without the commitment of a premium product. It earns 2% back on groceries up to $6,000 in annual grocery spend, and 1% on everything else.

The $6,000 grocery cap matters: At 2%, the maximum cashback you can earn from groceries is $120/year. A family spending $700+ per month on groceries will hit this cap around late August — after which grocery purchases drop to 1%. TD has no disclosed cap.

No Income Requirement

Unlike the TD Visa Infinite, the RBC Cash Back Mastercard has no minimum income requirement. This makes it accessible to students, recent graduates, newcomers to Canada, and part-time workers who may not qualify for premium cards. It's one of the strongest no-fee, entry-level cashback cards from a Big Six bank.

Cashback Redemption

RBC credits cashback once per year in January, directly to your RBC bank account or as a statement credit. You cannot redeem on-demand mid-year — a minor drawback vs. TD's on-demand option. For most people this won't matter.

The Break-Even Math

TD costs $120/year after the first year. To break even vs. the free RBC card, you need to earn at least $120 more annually from TD's higher rates. Here's how that looks at realistic Canadian spending levels:

At those spending levels — realistic for a working Canadian adult — TD earns $144 more per year, comfortably covering the $120 fee. Net advantage after fee: +$24/year. Spend more on these categories, and that advantage grows fast.

The rough break-even point: if you spend less than $350/month combined on groceries + gas + recurring bills, stick with RBC. Above that threshold, TD pays for itself and earns more.

The Verdict

Our Recommendation

For most employed Canadians who meet the income threshold, TD Cash Back Visa Infinite earns more. The 3% rate across groceries, gas, and recurring bills — with no disclosed spending cap — makes it one of the strongest flat-category cashback cards from any Big Six bank.

TD wins for: Families, commuters, subscription-heavy users, moderate-to-high spenders
RBC wins for: Students, newcomers, low spenders, no-fee seekers, Costco shoppers

Don't qualify for TD Visa Infinite? The RBC Cash Back Mastercard is the best no-fee cashback option from a Big Six bank. No barrier, solid 2% on groceries, and it works at Costco.

One More Option: The Costco Angle

Costco Canada only accepts Mastercard in-store — so the TD Visa Infinite can't be used there. If you do significant Costco grocery shopping, consider using the RBC Cash Back Mastercard specifically at Costco and TD for other groceries. Or look at the Costco Mastercard, which earns 3% at Costco directly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the TD Cash Back Visa Infinite worth the annual fee?

Yes — for most Canadians spending $400+ per month combined on groceries, gas, and recurring bills. The 3% earn rate on those categories generates enough cashback to cover the $120 fee and then some. The first-year fee waiver lets you validate the card risk-free before committing.

Can I get the RBC Cash Back Mastercard as a newcomer to Canada?

Yes. RBC is one of the more newcomer-friendly Big Six banks. The Cash Back Mastercard has no minimum income requirement. You may need to visit a branch with proof of identity and status documents, but it's generally accessible without a Canadian credit history.

Does the TD Cash Back Visa Infinite work at Costco Canada?

No. Costco Canada only accepts Mastercard at checkout — so the TD Visa Infinite cannot be used in-store at Costco. If Costco is a significant grocery destination, use the RBC Mastercard there and TD's card for other grocery stores.

How does cashback redemption compare?

TD allows redemption any time in $25 increments, or auto-credit annually each October. RBC only credits once per year in January — no on-demand option. For most people the annual credit is fine, but TD's flexibility is a minor advantage.

What credit score do I need for each card?

The TD Cash Back Visa Infinite (Visa Infinite tier) typically requires a credit score of 700+ and the income requirement. The RBC Cash Back Mastercard is more accessible — a score of 660+ and no income minimum. Neither bank publishes an exact minimum score.
