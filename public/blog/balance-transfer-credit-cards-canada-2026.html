Best Balance Transfer Credit Cards in Canada 2026

Carrying $5,000 on a 19.99% card costs $1,000 in annual interest. A balance transfer at 0–2.99% gives you time to pay it down — saving hundreds. Here are the best Canadian options.

How Balance Transfers Work in Canada

A balance transfer moves your existing credit card debt from a high-rate card to a new card offering a promotional low rate — often 0%, 0.99%, or 2.99% for a limited period (typically 6–12 months). You pay a one-time transfer fee (usually 1–3% of the balance transferred), then pay the remaining balance at the promo rate instead of 19.99%.

Sample savings on $5,000 balance over 6 months:

Current card (19.99% APR)~$487 in interest
MBNA True Line (0% promo, 3% fee)$150 (fee only)
Scotiabank Value Visa (7.99% ongoing)~$200 interest
Total saved (MBNA route)~$337 in 6 months

Critical warning: Missing a single minimum payment during the promotional period on most 0% offers immediately cancels the promo rate and reverts to the standard rate (often 19.99–22.99%). Set up automatic minimum payments AND pay the balance manually each month.

Step-by-Step Balance Transfer Strategy

Step 1: Calculate your total debt. Sum up all credit card balances and their interest rates. Include store credit cards (often 25–30% APR) as priority transfers.

Step 2: Apply for the transfer card. Don't transfer before you're approved — you can only transfer up to your new credit limit. Apply and confirm the limit before requesting the transfer.

Step 3: Request the balance transfer promptly. Most balance transfer promos require the transfer to be initiated within 30–60 days of account opening. Miss that window and you miss the promo.

Step 4: Set up automatic minimum payments. Immediately set up autopay for the minimum payment on the new card. One missed payment typically kills the promo rate and can result in default interest being applied retroactively.

Step 5: Stop using the old card. Lock it away or cut it up. The goal is to pay off the transferred balance — adding new purchases complicates this.

Step 6: Pay as much as possible each month. Divide your total balance by the number of promo months to calculate the required monthly payment. Aim to pay this or more every month.

Pro tip: Don't close your old card immediately after transferring the balance — it will reduce your total available credit and increase your utilization ratio, temporarily lowering your credit score. Let it sit unused for 6–12 months, then close it if you want to simplify.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does applying for a balance transfer card hurt my credit score?
Yes — any new credit application results in a hard inquiry that can reduce your score by a few points temporarily. However, if the balance transfer reduces your utilization ratio significantly (by increasing your total available credit), the net impact on your score is often positive within 3–6 months.
Can I transfer balances from multiple cards?
Yes — most balance transfer cards allow you to transfer balances from multiple cards, up to a total of your new credit limit minus the transfer fee. Call the new issuer to initiate transfers from multiple sources simultaneously.
What if I can't pay off the full balance before the promo ends?
After the promo period, any remaining balance converts to the card's standard purchase rate (often 12.99–19.99%). If you're on MBNA True Line, the ongoing 12.99% rate is still significantly below a typical 19.99% card. Plan to pay off as much as possible during the promo, then evaluate whether to transfer again or accelerate payments at the standard rate.
Can I transfer a balance from a card at the same bank?
No — most Canadian banks do not allow balance transfers between cards issued by the same institution. You must transfer to a card at a different bank. CIBC balance transfer offers apply to non-CIBC cards; TD offers apply to non-TD cards; and so on.
