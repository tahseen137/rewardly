Credit Card Utilization Canada 2026 — How It Affects Your Score

Utilization is 30% of your credit score — the second-biggest factor. Keep it under 30% per card and under 10% for the best scores.

What Is Credit Card Utilization?

Credit utilization ratio is the percentage of your available credit that you're currently using. It's calculated as: your current balance ÷ your credit limit × 100. If you have a $10,000 limit and a $3,000 balance, your utilization is 30%.

In Canada, credit bureaus (Equifax and TransUnion) calculate this both per card AND across all your cards combined. Both numbers matter to your score.

Example Calculation Card A: $2,500 balance on $5,000 limit = 50% utilization (high — hurts score) Card B: $500 balance on $8,000 limit = 6.25% utilization (excellent) Card C: $0 balance on $2,000 limit = 0% utilization TOTAL: ($2,500 + $500 + $0) / ($5,000 + $8,000 + $2,000) = $3,000 / $15,000 = 20% Fix: Pay down Card A to $1,000 → Card A drops to 20%, total drops to 13.3% Score improvement: Typically 20–40+ points within one reporting cycle

Common misunderstanding: Many Canadians aim for "under 30%" and think that's optimal. In reality, the sweet spot for the best credit scores is under 10% overall utilization. Under 30% avoids penalties; under 10% actively helps maximize your score. If you're targeting a mortgage, aim for under 10% on every card.

Per-Card vs. Total Utilization — Both Are Scored

Canadian credit bureaus score each individual card's utilization AND your overall utilization across all cards. You can have a low total utilization but still be penalized if one individual card is very high.

6 Ways to Lower Your Utilization Fast

💳

Make a mid-cycle payment: Banks report your balance to credit bureaus on your statement date (usually monthly), not when you pay. If your statement date is the 15th and you pay your full balance on the 25th, the bureau still saw your full balance. Make a payment before your statement date to lower the reported balance.

📈

Request a credit limit increase: Doubling your limit while keeping the same balance halves your utilization instantly. At most major Canadian banks, requesting an increase online triggers only a soft pull (no score impact). Wait 6+ months after opening a card and 3+ months of on-time payments before requesting.

🔄

Spread spending across multiple cards: Instead of one card at 60%, put spend on two cards at 30% each. Same total debt, lower per-card utilization. This is one legitimate reason to carry multiple credit cards.

🚫

Don't cancel cards you've paid off: Closing a card removes its limit from your total available credit, which increases your utilization on remaining cards. Keep paid-off cards open (make a small purchase every few months to keep them active) unless there's an annual fee that doesn't make sense.

🏦

Ask for a product switch to a higher-limit card: If you have a standard card, ask your bank to switch (not upgrade — switch) you to a premium card with a higher typical limit. Product switches don't require a hard inquiry at most Canadian banks.

📅

Know your statement dates: Each card has a different statement date. Pay down high-balance cards before their specific statement date, not just before the due date. Call your bank or check your app to find when your balances are reported.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I pay my credit card to $0 every month for best score?
You don't need to reach $0 for best results — you need a low balance on the statement date. If your statement date is July 15 and your limit is $10,000, having $800 on the statement date (8% utilization) is excellent. Paying to $0 before the statement date is ideal but not required — any amount under 10% of your limit works. Note: for avoiding interest, you must pay the full statement balance by the due date regardless.
Does 0% utilization hurt my score?
Technically, having 0% utilization on ALL cards (never using any card) can be slightly worse than 1–5% utilization, because some scoring models want to see active use of credit. In practice, the difference is minimal (a few points at most), and it only applies if ALL your accounts show $0 balance. Using any card lightly — even once every few months — solves this.
How quickly does improving utilization show up on my score?
Utilization is one of the fastest-moving credit score factors. Once your lower balance is reported (usually on your next statement date), your score typically reflects the change within 30–45 days. This is the fastest way to improve your credit score without opening new accounts.
