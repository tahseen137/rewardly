Best Credit Cards for Students in Canada 2026

No income? No credit history? No problem. Student credit cards in Canada are designed to approve you anyway — and the right one builds a credit score that unlocks premium rewards cards when you graduate.

What Makes a Student Card Different?

Student credit cards have two things regular cards don't: no income requirement and no Canadian credit history requirement. Because student cards are designed as first-time credit products, banks approve them based on enrollment status (sometimes) or simply based on being 18+ with a bank account — not on an existing credit score.

The trade-off: lower credit limits ($500–$1,500 typically) and fewer rewards vs. premium cards. But that's fine — the primary purpose of a student card is to start your credit file, not to maximize points. Once you graduate and have 2+ years of history, you qualify for the cards that actually earn serious rewards.

International Students: Extra Considerations

International students in Canada face a particular challenge: no Canadian credit history and potentially no Social Insurance Number (though this isn't always required). Options:

Nova Credit: Some Canadian issuers (check with BMO and RBC) accept international credit history translated through Nova Credit — a service that converts credit reports from select countries (U.S., UK, India, Mexico, Australia, Korea, Philippines, Dominican Republic) into a Canadian-equivalent format.

Secured card route: If no Nova Credit option exists for your home country, the Capital One Guaranteed Secured Mastercard ($75 deposit, guaranteed approval) is the fastest path to establishing Canadian credit. After 12 months, you can apply for an unsecured student or no-fee card.

SIN not required: Most banks do not require a Social Insurance Number to apply for a student credit card — they accept a student permit and passport. Verify with the specific bank before applying.

Smart Credit Card Rules for Students

Rule 1: Pay in full, always. Credit card interest (19.99%) cancels any rewards you earn and then some. Even one missed month of full payment negates months of rewards. Set a calendar reminder 3 days before your due date.

Rule 2: Start with one card. Applying for multiple cards in a short window generates multiple hard inquiries and can hurt a thin credit file. Apply for one card, use it well for 12 months, then consider a second.

Rule 3: Use it, don't max it. High utilization (balance/limit ratio above 30%) hurts your credit score. If your limit is $1,000, keep your monthly balance below $300 before the statement date. You can pay it down mid-month if needed.

Frequently Asked Questions

At what age can I get a credit card in Canada?
The minimum age is 18 in most provinces (19 in BC, Manitoba, Newfoundland, Nova Scotia, NB, PEI, and Yukon — the age of majority). You need to be the age of majority in your province to enter a credit contract independently. Some cards require you to be 19 even if your province's age of majority is 18 — check each issuer's terms.
Do student credit cards have lower credit limits?
Yes — student cards typically start with $500–$1,500 limits versus $5,000–$10,000+ on premium cards. This is intentional — a lower limit reduces risk for both you and the bank while you establish your credit history. Limits usually increase every 12 months as you demonstrate responsible use.
Should I cancel my student card after graduating?
Ideally no — closing a credit account reduces your average account age and your total available credit, both of which can lower your credit score. After graduation, ask the issuer to "convert" or "product switch" your student card to a no-fee adult card (like the Tangerine Money-Back or PC Financial Mastercard). This preserves the account age while getting you a better product.
