The CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite has been Canada's most popular grocery cashback card for years — and it earned that reputation with a 4% earn rate on groceries and gas before many competitors offered similar rates. But in 2026, it faces real competition from the Scotiabank Momentum and BMO CashBack World Elite that outperform it in key areas.

Here's an honest assessment of whether the CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite is still worth it, who it's best for, and when you should pick a competitor instead.

Earn rates in detail

4% — Groceries

Major Canadian grocery chains: Loblaws, No Frills, Real Canadian Superstore, Metro, Sobeys, IGA, Safeway, Longos, Farm Boy. Walmart Supercentres code as grocery on CIBC. Costco does not qualify — it's a warehouse club (MCC 5300), not a grocery store.

4% — Gas & EV charging

All major gas stations including Petro-Canada, Shell, Esso, Husky, and independent pumps. EV charging is explicitly included — works at ChargePoint, FLO, Tesla Supercharger (when billed to card), and most public charging networks. One of the few cards to explicitly call out EV charging at 4%.

2% — Dining

Restaurants, cafes, bars, and food delivery services (Uber Eats, DoorDash, Skip). Note: dining earns 2%, not 4% — this is where Scotiabank Momentum edges ahead by including transit and streaming at 4% instead of dining at 2%.

2% — Transportation

Taxis, rideshares (Uber, Lyft), public transit, GO Train. Works well for daily commuters.

1% — Everything else

Standard base rate on all other purchases.

No caps: Unlike some competitor cards, there are no monthly or annual spend caps on any earn tier. You earn 4% on $50/month or $5,000/month of groceries — same rate.

The annual payout: a real problem

CIBC pays cashback once per year, in January, for all spending from the previous calendar year. This means:

  • If you open the card in January, your first cashback arrives 13 months later
  • You're essentially giving CIBC an interest-free loan of your cashback all year
  • If you cancel mid-year, you may forfeit some or all of your accumulated cashback

Compare this to Scotiabank Momentum, which pays monthly. On $500/month grocery spend at 4%, you'd have $240/year in cashback. With CIBC, you wait until January. With Scotia, you get $20 credited every month — compounding if you're carrying the statement credit forward.

This isn't a reason to avoid the card, but it's worth knowing before you apply. If monthly cashback matters to you, Scotiabank Momentum is the better choice.

Pros and cons

Pros

  • 4% on groceries and gas (tied for best)
  • 4% on EV charging (explicit, rare)
  • No earn caps on any tier
  • Mobile device insurance ($1,000)
  • Strong CIBC ecosystem for existing customers
  • Visa Infinite benefits and concierge

Cons

  • Annual cashback payout (January only)
  • No first-year fee waiver
  • 2.5% foreign transaction fee
  • 2% dining is lower than some rivals
  • 10-day medical travel insurance (Scotia has 15)
  • $30 supplementary card fee

The verdict on the comparison: CIBC wins on dining (2% vs. 1%) and EV charging explicitly, but loses on transit (4% vs 2%), streaming (4% vs 1%), drug stores (2% vs 1%), cashback frequency, and first-year fee waiver. For most Canadians, Scotiabank Momentum is a better card unless you're deeply integrated into CIBC's banking ecosystem.

Who should get this card

✓ Get the CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite if:
  • You already bank primarily with CIBC
  • You drive an EV and want explicit 4% on charging
  • You spend heavily on dining (2% vs Scotia's 1% on "other")
  • You don't mind waiting for January cashback
  • You want a Visa Infinite for Costco shopping (though Costco won't earn 4%)
✗ Consider Scotiabank Momentum instead if:
  • You want monthly cashback payments
  • You use public transit frequently (4% vs 2%)
  • You subscribe to streaming services
  • You want the first year free
  • You're not an existing CIBC customer

FAQ

Does Walmart count as grocery for the CIBC Dividend?

Yes, Walmart Supercentres typically code as grocery (MCC 5411) for CIBC, earning 4%. Walmart Canada stores may vary — if you're not sure, check a small test purchase first.

Can I get cashback earlier than January?

No — CIBC pays cashback once per year in January only. If you cancel the card before January, you may receive a partial payout or forfeit accumulated cashback. Contact CIBC directly if you're considering cancelling.

Is there a first-year annual fee waiver?

No — unlike Scotiabank Momentum (which waives year 1), the CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite charges the full $120 fee from the first year. Factor this into your first-year value calculation.

What's the difference between CIBC Dividend Visa and CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite?

The base CIBC Dividend Visa earns lower rates (3% grocery, 2% gas) with a $29 annual fee and no income requirement. The Visa Infinite version (this review) earns 4% on both, costs $120, and requires $60K income. Unless you don't meet the income requirement, the Infinite version is worth the higher fee.

Does the dining 2% include food delivery apps?

Yes — Uber Eats, DoorDash, and SkipTheDishes typically code as restaurant/dining (MCC 5812) for CIBC and earn 2% cashback.
