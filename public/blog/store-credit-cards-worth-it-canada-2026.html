Are Store Credit Cards Worth It in Canada 2026?

Canadian Tire, HBC, Sport Chek, Walmart — store cards have high earn rates in-store, but are they worth the wallet space? Honest answer: sometimes yes, usually no.

The Core Trade-off With Store Cards

Store credit cards (also called co-branded or proprietary retail cards) offer high earn rates at a specific retailer — but earn very little everywhere else. The proposition works if you spend heavily at that exact store; it fails if you carry the card as a "just in case" option or apply at checkout to get a one-time discount. The biggest risk: a hard inquiry on your credit report for a card that earns 0.5% everywhere else.

The checkout counter trap: Canadian retailers often offer 10–15% off your purchase today when you sign up for their store card. This hard inquiry costs you 5–15 points on your credit score and a new card application that may affect your utilization calculations. The one-time discount is rarely worth it. Get the discount, then don't apply for the card.

Major Canadian Store Cards Reviewed

Canadian Tire Triangle Mastercard (No-Fee)

Worth it for regulars
4% CT Money at Canadian Tire, Mark's, Sport Chek · 1.5¢/L at Gas+ · 0.5% everywhere else

CT Money is worth ~1.4¢ each. So 4% at Canadian Tire = effectively ~1.4% in real value. Earn rate is high IN-STORE but the 0.5% everywhere else is well below even basic no-fee bank cards. A dedicated Canadian Tire shopper (tools, automotive, seasonal) may find it worth carrying as a secondary card. Annual fee: $0.

Worth it if: You spend $1,000+ per year at Canadian Tire/Mark's/Sport Chek AND you already have a strong primary card for all other spending.

Triangle World Elite Mastercard

Maybe — requires $15K income
4% CT Money at CT stores · 3% at grocery · 1.5¢/L at Gas+ · 1% everywhere else

Upgrading to the World Elite version adds 3% at grocery stores (CT Money, not cashback — roughly 1.5x grocery value). The 1% base everywhere else is better than the standard card's 0.5% but still below Rogers Red (2%). Worth considering if you're a heavy Canadian Tire shopper AND buy groceries elsewhere. No annual fee with $15K minimum income. Note: PC Financial WE Mastercard still beats it at Loblaw stores at 4.5%.

Hudson's Bay Mastercard

Skip it
3 Bay rewards per dollar at HBC · 1 point per dollar everywhere else

Bay rewards are worth approximately 0.2¢ each. So 3 pts/$ at HBC = ~0.6% value in rewards — worse than the 2% you'd get from Rogers Red. The everywhere-else earn rate of 1 pt/$ = 0.2% — far below any general-purpose rewards card. The primary use case is accessing exclusive HBC cardholder events and discounts, which have diminished in value as the Bay's Canadian footprint has declined. Annual fee: $0, but that doesn't make it worth your wallet space or credit inquiry.

Walmart Rewards Mastercard

Worth it for dedicated Walmart shoppers
1.25% Walmart Rewards at Walmart · 1% everywhere else

Walmart Rewards are redeemable as cash at Walmart. 1.25% at Walmart is below the 2% from Rogers Red — however, unlike Rogers Red, the Walmart card has no minimum income requirement and is often approved for those with moderate credit scores (650+). For dedicated Walmart shoppers who don't qualify for Rogers Red WE ($80K income), this is a reasonable option. Annual fee: $0.

When a Store Card IS Worth It

A store card makes sense in these situations: you spend $1,500+ per year at that specific retailer, you already have a strong general-purpose card covering everything else, the store card is the only option for a store that doesn't accept Visa or Mastercard, or you're in credit-building mode and need another active account (though secured cards are usually better for this).

The PC Financial exception: The PC Financial World Elite Mastercard earns 45 PC Optimum points per dollar at Shoppers Drug Mart and Real Canadian Superstore — equivalent to 4.5% value. This blows every other store card out of the water and competes with even the best bank rewards cards. If you shop at Loblaw-banner stores, this is NOT a store card to skip — it's arguably the most underrated card in Canada.
