Best Credit Cards for International Students in Canada (2026)

Approved with no Canadian credit history — the real options, ranked by value and approval odds.

Updated July 2026 · 9 min read

Canada is home to over 800,000 international students — and nearly all of them face the same problem when they arrive: no Canadian credit history makes it nearly impossible to get approved for a standard credit card, even with strong finances back home. Canadian banks don't recognize foreign credit files, and most premium cards require established Canadian credit history.

This guide covers the specific options available to international students, how to get your first card, and how to build a Canadian credit score that will serve you long after graduation.

The International Student Credit Challenge

The core problem: Canadian credit cards require Canadian credit history. Your excellent credit in your home country (India, China, the US, the UK, anywhere) is invisible to Canadian bureaus Equifax and TransUnion. You arrive in Canada as a "credit invisible" person — which means most standard card applications will be declined.

There are three paths forward:

  • Student cards — issued specifically for students, with relaxed income and credit history requirements
  • Secured credit cards — you deposit cash as collateral, which becomes your credit limit, essentially eliminating approval risk
  • Newcomer programs — some banks (RBC, Scotiabank, CIBC, BMO) have formal newcomer banking programs that let international students open accounts and get a credit card as part of a bundle
Documentation you'll need: Study permit, passport, SIN (Social Insurance Number) or ITN (Individual Tax Number), proof of enrollment at a Canadian educational institution, and Canadian address. Most banks require you to be physically in Canada to apply for your first card.

Secured Credit Cards — The Guaranteed Approval Path

If you've been declined for any of the above student cards due to absolutely zero Canadian credit history, a secured credit card is your starting point. With a secured card, you deposit cash (typically $200–$2,500) as collateral — that deposit becomes your credit limit. Approval is essentially guaranteed because the bank holds your money as security.

Home Trust Secured Visa
Most popular secured card for credit building in Canada
✓ Guaranteed Approval
No rewards — pure credit building

The Home Trust Secured Visa is Canada's most widely used secured card for credit building. No annual fee (basic version), deposit your chosen amount ($500–$10,000) as your credit limit, use it for regular purchases, pay the full statement balance every month, and Equifax and TransUnion receive monthly reports of your responsible payment history. After 6–12 months of on-time payments, you'll have enough Canadian credit history to qualify for standard student or entry-level rewards cards.

PROS

  • Guaranteed approval (no credit check)
  • No annual fee (basic version)
  • Reports to both Equifax and TransUnion
  • Gateway to unsecured cards in 6-12 months

CONS

  • No rewards earned
  • Your deposit is tied up as collateral
  • Low credit limit (your deposit amount)
Secured card → unsecured upgrade path: Use your secured card for 6–12 months. Pay the full balance every month without exception. Keep your usage below 30% of the limit (e.g., if your limit is $500, keep your balance under $150). After 6 months, your credit score should be 650+ and you can apply for a student rewards card. Many banks will also return your deposit if you upgrade to an unsecured card within the same institution.

How to Build Canadian Credit Quickly

1

Month 1–2: Get a Canadian bank account and SIN

Open a chequing account at any major bank. Apply for a SIN at Service Canada (required for anyone permitted to work in Canada). Start using your bank account for regular transactions to establish your banking relationship.

2

Month 2–3: Apply for a secured card or newcomer student card

Apply for the Home Trust Secured Visa OR use your bank's newcomer program to get your first unsecured student card (RBC and Scotiabank have the strongest newcomer programs). Use the card for all regular spending — groceries, transit, subscriptions.

3

Month 3–12: Pay the full balance every month

This is the most important step. Pay the FULL statement balance by the due date every month. One missed or late payment undoes months of credit building and stays on your report for 6 years. Set up autopay for the full statement balance.

4

Month 6–12: Keep utilization below 30%

Aim to use less than 30% of your credit limit at any time. If you have a $500 limit, try not to carry a balance above $150 when your statement closes. Utilization is the second most important factor in your score after payment history.

5

Month 12–18: Check your score and upgrade

Use Borrowell or Credit Karma (both free, both soft pulls) to monitor your score. If you're at 650+, you're eligible for most student and entry-level rewards cards. Apply for a rewards card that matches your spending — groceries, transit, dining, or travel home.

Common Mistakes International Students Make with Credit

Mistake 1: Applying for Multiple Cards at Once

When you arrive in Canada eager to build credit, the temptation is to apply for several cards simultaneously. Don't. Each application is a hard inquiry that temporarily reduces your score. Apply for one card, use it responsibly for 6 months, then consider a second if needed.

Mistake 2: Paying Only the Minimum

The minimum payment trap is especially dangerous for students on tight budgets. Carrying a balance on a 19.99% card compounds quickly. A $500 balance paid at minimums takes years to pay off and costs hundreds in interest. Always pay the full statement balance.

Mistake 3: Closing the Account When Returning Home

If you plan to stay in Canada after graduation, or return for work, closing your credit card account when you go home for the summer eliminates your credit history. Keep the account open with minimal or zero activity — a card with no activity can be kept alive with one small charge per year.

If you're leaving Canada permanently: Cancel your card and close your bank accounts before you leave. An uncollected balance on a Canadian credit card becomes extremely difficult to resolve from abroad and can have collection implications if you ever return to Canada.

Mistake 4: Using Your Card for Cash Advances

International students sometimes use credit cards to withdraw cash for rent or expenses. Cash advances are extremely expensive: 22.99–24.99% interest with no grace period, plus a 3–5% cash advance fee upfront. Use your bank's debit card for cash needs, or set up an e-transfer for landlords who require it.

Mistake 5: Giving Your Card to Family Members

Some students share their cards with family members visiting Canada. You are legally and financially responsible for every charge on your card, regardless of who made it. If a family member makes a charge you can't repay, your credit score suffers.
