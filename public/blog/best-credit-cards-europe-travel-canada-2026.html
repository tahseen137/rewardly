Best Credit Cards for Travel to Europe from Canada 2026

European travel means spending in Euros, Pounds, Kroner, and more. Without a no-FX-fee card, you're losing 2.5% on every purchase. Here's how to pick the right card for your European adventure.

Why No Foreign Transaction Fee Is Essential for Europe

Most Canadian credit cards charge a 2.5% foreign transaction fee on purchases made in foreign currencies. On a €3,000 European trip (roughly $4,500 CAD), that's $112.50 in fees you're paying just to use your card. With a no-FX-fee card, that money stays in your pocket.

The math: $4,500 CAD in European spending × 2.5% FX fee = $112.50 lost. On a $10,000 European trip: $250 lost to FX fees. A no-foreign-transaction-fee card is the single most important feature to look for when choosing a card for European travel from Canada.

Additionally, for European travel specifically, you want: comprehensive travel medical insurance, trip cancellation coverage, and ideally lounge access for long connecting flights through hub airports like Pearson (YYZ) or Frankfurt (FRA).

Europe-Specific Tips for Canadian Travelers

Visa over Amex: American Express is far less accepted in Europe than Visa or Mastercard, especially at independent restaurants, small shops, and rural areas. Always carry a Visa or Mastercard as your primary card in Europe — and have cash (Euros or local currency) as a backup for places that are cash-only.

Chip-and-PIN vs. Chip-and-Signature: Canadian cards use chip-and-signature at most merchants. Some European automated kiosks (train ticket machines, gas station pumps) require a chip-and-PIN card. If you can set a PIN on your Canadian card, do so before travel. Alternatively, carry cash for these kiosks or use the attended checkout instead.

Dynamic Currency Conversion (DCC): European merchants sometimes offer to charge you in Canadian dollars instead of local currency. Always decline — say you want to pay in the local currency (Euros, Pounds, etc.). The merchant's DCC rate is always worse than your card's rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to notify my bank before traveling to Europe?
Most major Canadian banks no longer require formal travel notifications due to improved fraud detection systems, but it's still a good practice. Log into your online banking or call the number on the back of your card before departure to inform them of your travel dates and destination countries. This prevents your card from being blocked when European transactions appear. Some banks have a "travel notification" feature in their mobile app — use it if available. Also ensure your card doesn't expire during your trip.
Which Canadian card is accepted most widely in Europe?
Visa is accepted most widely across Europe, including in smaller towns and at merchants that don't accept Amex. Mastercard has almost equivalent acceptance to Visa in Europe. American Express is accepted at major retailers, large restaurants, and hotels in big European cities, but is frequently not accepted at independent restaurants, local shops, or in rural and Eastern European areas. For the broadest coverage in Europe, prioritize Visa or Mastercard.
