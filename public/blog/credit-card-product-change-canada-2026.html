Credit Card Product Change in Canada 2026

How to switch to a better card at the same bank — no hard inquiry, no closed account, no lost credit history. Here's exactly how it works.

What Is a Product Change?

A credit card product change (also called a product switch or card conversion) is when you switch from one credit card to a different card offered by the same bank — without closing your existing account and opening a new one. Your account number may change or stay the same depending on the bank, but the credit history, account age, and credit limit typically transfer to the new card.

This is fundamentally different from closing a card and applying for a new one. A product change typically involves no hard credit inquiry, preserves your account age (which contributes to your credit score's length-of-history factor), and keeps your credit limit intact. You call your bank, tell them you want to switch products, and they issue you a new card on the same account.

Advantages

+No hard inquiry on your credit report
+Account age preserved (protects credit score)
+Credit limit maintained or transferred
+Existing pre-authorized payments typically transfer
+Avoid annual fee without fully closing account

Disadvantages

−No welcome bonus on the new card (product changes don't qualify)
−Limited to cards at the same bank
−Bank must offer product changes — not all do
−Points currency may change or reset depending on programs

Critical: You do NOT get a welcome bonus when doing a product change. If earning a new sign-up bonus is your goal, you need to apply for a new card — not do a product change. Product changes are for fee avoidance or when you simply want a different earn structure without a hard pull.

When Product Changes Make Sense

Good use case: Annual fee avoidance

You've had the TD First Class Travel Visa Infinite ($139/year) for 3 years. Your travel has slowed down and the fee no longer makes sense. Instead of closing the account (which would reduce your available credit and account age), you ask TD to switch you to the no-fee TD Rewards Visa. Same account, same history — you just stop paying $139/year and get a lower earn rate.

Good use case: Spending pattern changed

You had the CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite for travel but now fly rarely. Switching to the CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite keeps your account at CIBC but switches you to a cashback earn structure that better matches your current grocery and gas spending — without a hard inquiry.

Bad use case: Wanting a new sign-up bonus

You've had the TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite for 2 years and want the 70,000 Aeroplan point welcome bonus again. You cannot get this through a product change — the bonus is only for new accounts. You'd need to apply for a new card (different issuer, or wait for TD's eligibility windows).

Bad use case: Switching banks

You want to leave CIBC and go to Scotiabank. Product changes are within the same bank only. For a bank switch, you have to apply for a new card — which means a hard inquiry but also a new welcome bonus opportunity.

Which Canadian Banks Allow Product Changes?

TD

TD Bank

✓ Product changes available

TD offers product changes between their Visa card lineup — for example, switching from TD First Class Travel Visa Infinite to TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite, or downgrading to a no-fee TD card. Call TD at 1-888-751-9000 or visit a branch. TD typically does not require a new application or hard pull for a same-network product change. Allow 7–10 business days for the new card.

RBC

RBC

✓ Product changes available

RBC allows product changes within their Visa portfolio (e.g., RBC Avion Visa Infinite to RBC ION Visa). Avion points typically transfer to the new account. Call RBC at 1-800-769-2512 or use their online banking to initiate. RBC sometimes processes these as account modifications rather than new applications.

Scotiabank

Scotiabank

✓ Product changes available

Scotiabank allows product changes within their Visa lineup (Passport to Momentum, for example) and within their Amex products. Scene+ points transfer between Scotiabank cards. Call the number on the back of your card. Note: switching from Amex to Visa or vice versa at Scotiabank may not be possible as a product change (different networks).

BMO

BMO

◐ Limited — varies by product

BMO allows some product changes but is less consistent about it than TD or RBC. Switching between Visa and Mastercard products at BMO may not be available as a product change. Call BMO at 1-800-263-2263 to discuss options. BMO Rewards points may or may not transfer depending on the destination card.

CIBC

CIBC

✓ Product changes available

CIBC allows product changes within their Visa portfolio. Switching from CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite to CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite (or a lower tier) is a common product change. Aeroplan points in your Air Canada account are not affected since they're held by Air Canada, not CIBC. Call CIBC at 1-800-465-4653.

Amex Canada

American Express Canada

✓ Product changes available

Amex Canada offers product changes within their card family (Gold to SimplyCash, Cobalt to Green, etc.). Amex MR points typically transfer to your account. Amex is generally flexible about downgrades, especially when a customer is considering cancellation. Call Amex retention at 1-800-869-3016 — they sometimes offer annual fee waivers or statement credits to retain cardholders before doing a product change.

How to Do a Product Change: Step-by-Step

The process is straightforward but success depends on what you ask for and how you ask for it. Here's the approach that works:

Call the number on the back of your card and tell the agent you want to change your credit card product — not close your account. Specify which card you want to switch to. If the agent says they can't do product changes, ask to speak with the retention or escalations department. Banks have significant flexibility that front-line agents may not immediately offer.

If you're trying to avoid an annual fee, call when the annual fee posts to your statement (usually within 30 days). Many issuers will refund the fee if you call promptly to downgrade or cancel. Some will offer a partial year fee waiver or a statement credit to keep you on the existing card — accept this if the card value justifies it.

Timing tip: The best time to initiate a product change is within 30–60 days before your annual fee renewal date. This gives you leverage and avoids paying a fee for a card you're about to change.

Product Change vs. Cancelling vs. New Application

Option Hard Inquiry? Account Age Preserved? Welcome Bonus? Annual Fee
Product Change No Yes No New card's fee
Cancel & Apply New Yes (new application) No (old account closed) Yes (if eligible) New card's fee
Keep Card Open (no changes) No Yes No Existing fee
Downgrade to no-fee at same bank No Yes No $0

The sweet spot: If you've already earned the welcome bonus on a card and no longer benefit from paying its annual fee, do a product change to a no-fee card at the same bank. You keep your account age, maintain your credit limit, avoid the fee, and can keep the account open indefinitely as a low-utilization booster.
