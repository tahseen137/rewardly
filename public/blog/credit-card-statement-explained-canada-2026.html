How to Read Your Credit Card Statement in Canada (2026 Guide)

Every line on your statement explained — in plain language, with real examples.

Updated July 2026 · 10 min read

Your credit card statement arrives every month and most Canadians glance at the balance, pay the minimum, and close the app. That habit costs the average Canadian cardholder hundreds of dollars per year in avoidable interest — and obscures the information you need to build credit properly.

This guide walks through every section of a typical Canadian credit card statement and explains exactly what it means, what to watch for, and what action to take.

The Statement Overview (Summary Section)

The first page or screen of your statement is a summary. Here's what each field means:

ACCOUNT SUMMARY — STATEMENT DATE: JUNE 30, 2026
Account Number: xxxx xxxx xxxx 4821
Credit Limit: $10,000.00
Available Credit: $6,847.32
Statement Balance: $3,152.68
Previous Balance: $1,205.00
Payments/Credits: $1,205.00
New Charges: $3,152.68
Minimum Payment Due: $63.00
Payment Due Date: JULY 21, 2026
Interest Rate (Purch.): 19.99% per year
Interest Rate (CA): 22.99% per year
Field by Field
Credit Limit
The maximum you can charge to the card before it's declined. Set by the issuer based on your income and creditworthiness. You can request an increase — banks often approve 25–50% increases for cardholders in good standing.
Available Credit
Credit Limit minus your current outstanding balance. This is NOT what you owe — it's how much more you can spend. Keeping this above 70% of your limit (using less than 30% of your credit) is important for a healthy credit score.
Statement Balance
Total amount owed as of the statement closing date. THIS is the number you should pay in full by the due date to avoid all interest. Paying this amount resets your grace period.
Previous Balance
What you owed at the start of this statement period (i.e., your balance from the prior statement). Ideally this equals zero — meaning you paid last month's statement in full.
Payments/Credits
Total of all payments you made plus any credits (refunds, returns, billing dispute credits) applied during this statement period.
New Charges
Total of all new purchases, fees, and cash advances made during this billing cycle.
Minimum Payment Due
The smallest amount you must pay by the due date to keep your account in good standing. Paying only the minimum is expensive — see the interest calculation section below.
Payment Due Date
You must pay at least the minimum by this date. Pay the full statement balance by this date to avoid all interest. Canadian regulations require this be at least 21 days after the statement close date.

Understanding the Three Balance Types

Three different balance figures appear on most Canadian statements, and confusing them is the most common costly mistake cardholders make.

Statement Balance
Pay this to avoid interest. The amount owed as of your statement closing date. This is fixed — it doesn't change as you make new purchases. Pay it in full by the due date and you pay zero interest on those purchases.
Current Balance
Your real-time balance right now — including all purchases made since your statement closed. This is higher than your statement balance if you've kept using the card. You don't need to pay this amount to avoid interest; paying the statement balance is sufficient.
Minimum Payment Due
The floor payment. Paying only this keeps your account current but interest accrues on the remaining balance at your card's purchase interest rate (typically 19.99–22.99% in Canada).
Golden rule: Always pay the Statement Balance by the Payment Due Date. Not the current balance, not the minimum — the statement balance. That eliminates interest entirely and protects your grace period on new purchases.

Minimum Payment: What It Actually Costs You

Canadian banks calculate minimum payments as either a flat amount (typically $10) or a percentage of the balance (usually 2–3%), whichever is greater. Making only the minimum payment on a typical balance is dramatically expensive over time.

Balance: $3,152.68
Interest rate: 19.99% per year
Minimum payment: $63.00 (2% of balance)
 
If you only ever pay the minimum:
→ Time to pay off: 17+ years
→ Total interest paid: ~$3,800+ (more than the original balance)

As of 2025, banks are required by federal regulation to include a "minimum payment warning" on your statement showing how long it will take and how much interest you'll pay if you only make minimum payments. Look for this disclosure — it's a sobering number.

Critical: Missing the minimum payment triggers consequences. A late or missed payment results in: (1) a late fee (typically $25–$48), (2) possible interest rate increase on your balance, (3) a negative mark on your credit report that stays for 6 years, and (4) loss of promotional interest rates.

Interest Charges — How They're Calculated

Most Canadian credit cards charge 19.99% per year on purchases and 22.99% on cash advances. But interest isn't charged as a flat annual amount — it compounds daily.

The Daily Periodic Rate

To find what you're actually being charged per day, divide the annual rate by 365:

Annual purchase rate: 19.99%
Daily periodic rate: 19.99% ÷ 365 = 0.05477% per day
 
On a $1,000 balance carried for 30 days:
$1,000 × 0.0005477 × 30 = $16.43 in interest
→ That's $197/year on a $1,000 balance you never pay down

When Interest Starts

If you paid your previous statement balance in full, new purchases enjoy a grace period — no interest accrues until the new statement due date. However, if you carried any balance forward from last month (paid less than the full statement balance), interest begins accruing on new purchases immediately — the grace period is lost. This is why carrying even a small balance forward is so costly.

Interest Charge Types
Purchase Interest
Charged on regular purchases when you carry a balance. Typically 19.99%/year in Canada. Only applies if you don't pay the full statement balance. Many low-rate cards offer 9.99–12.99% as an alternative.
Cash Advance Interest
Charged on cash advances (ATM withdrawals, balance transfers counted as advances, some bill payments). Typically 22.99–24.99%. No grace period — interest starts the moment of the advance. Plus a 3–5% cash advance fee upfront.
Balance Transfer Interest
Some cards offer 0% promotional rates for 6–12 months on balance transfers. After the promo period, the rate jumps — often to 22.99%. Check your statement for the promo expiry date.
Overlimit Fee / Interest
If you exceed your credit limit, you may face a fee or declined transaction depending on your agreement. Under Canadian regulations, you must opt in to overlimit transactions — they can't be approved by default and charged a fee without consent.

What to watch for
Foreign Transaction Fees
These appear as a separate line item (e.g., "FOREIGN TRANSACTION FEE $3.12") associated with a USD or foreign currency purchase. You're being charged 2.5% of the converted amount. Use a no-FX card to eliminate these entirely.
Annual Fee
Charged once per year on the anniversary of your account opening (or statement close date). It appears in the transaction listing. If you're closing the card, do it before the annual fee posts — some banks will waive or refund it if you cancel within 30 days of being charged.
Duplicate or Unrecognized Charges
Review every transaction. Merchant names are often abbreviated or use corporate parent names. If you see something you don't recognize, check your receipts before filing a dispute. Genuine fraud should be reported immediately to your issuer.
Pending vs. Posted Transactions
Your statement only shows posted (settled) transactions. Pending transactions visible in your app or online banking may not appear on your statement until the next billing cycle — especially hotel holds, restaurant tips, and pre-authorized amounts.

The Rewards Summary Section

Cards with rewards programs include a summary of your points or cashback earned this period, running total, and sometimes redemption options. Key things to understand:

Points Earned This Period
Points or cashback earned on this statement's purchases. On cards with bonus categories (e.g., 5x on groceries), you'll see breakdowns by category or a total with the earn rate applied.
Points Balance
Your total accumulated rewards. For cashback cards, this may show as a dollar value. For points cards (Aeroplan, MR, Scene+), this is in points. Check the expiry policy — most major Canadian program points don't expire while the account is open and in good standing.
Rewards Applied as Credit
If you redeemed points for a statement credit, it appears as a negative amount in your transactions (a credit reduces your balance). Statement credits count as payments toward your balance.
Cashback credit timing: Many cashback cards don't credit your rewards monthly — they accumulate and post once per year (often on your anniversary or in January). Scotiabank Momentum, for example, posts cash rewards annually. TD and BMO cashback cards typically post monthly. Check your card's terms.

Grace Period and Payment Due Date

Under Canadian federal banking regulations, credit card issuers must provide a minimum 21-day interest-free grace period between the statement close date and the payment due date. Most Canadian banks offer 21–25 days; American Express typically provides 28 days.

Grace period killer: If you carry any unpaid balance from your previous statement, your grace period on NEW purchases disappears. Interest accrues on new purchases from the transaction date — even on purchases made after you made a partial payment. This is the most expensive mistake most cardholders don't know they're making.

What to Do After Reading Your Statement

Step 1: Review Every Transaction

Scroll through the full transaction list. Flag anything you don't recognize. You typically have 30–60 days from the statement date to dispute a charge — check your card agreement. Call your issuer immediately if you see fraud.

Step 2: Note Three Numbers

Write down (or set a reminder for): your statement balance, your payment due date, and your available credit utilization percentage. These three numbers tell you everything you need to manage your card health.

Step 3: Pay the Full Statement Balance

Set up the payment through your bank's bill payment system (add your credit card as a payee). Always pay at least 3–5 business days before the due date to account for processing delays. Better yet, set up automatic full payment to never miss it.

Best practice: Set up autopay for the statement balance. Every major Canadian bank allows you to auto-pay the full statement balance monthly — not just the minimum. This eliminates interest charges entirely, protects your grace period, and prevents late fees. TD, RBC, Scotiabank, CIBC, BMO, and Amex all offer this in their online banking settings.

Step 4: Check Your Rewards Balance

Make sure points or cashback are accumulating as expected. If you bought $500 in groceries and your card earns 4x Scene+ on groceries, you should see 2,000 points earned. If the number looks wrong, check your category breakdowns or call the issuer — some merchants code unexpectedly.

Step 5: Look for Optimization Opportunities

Where did you spend the most last month? If the bulk of your spending is in a category your current card doesn't bonus, that's a signal to either add a specialized card or consider a product change. This is exactly what Rewardly helps you calculate.
