Credit Card Annual Fee Waiver Canada 2026

Canadian banks have retention teams whose job is to keep you from cancelling. A 5-minute phone call can save you $120–$799 per year — or get you a bonus to stay. Here's the exact playbook.

Do Canadian Banks Actually Waive Annual Fees?

Yes — more often than most cardholders realize. Canadian banks (TD, RBC, Scotiabank, CIBC, BMO, National Bank) all have retention teams specifically trained to handle cancellation calls and offer incentives to keep customers. These incentives commonly include:

Full annual fee waiver (the entire fee credited back to your account). Partial annual fee waiver (50% credit). Bonus points offered to stay (often worth more than the fee itself). Upgrade to a higher-tier card. Product downgrade to a no-fee card (keeping account open, preserving credit history).

The right timing: Call within 30 days of your annual fee posting to your statement. This is when you can ask for a credit on the fee that just charged. Banks are most motivated to retain you when you've already been billed — calling before the fee posts is less effective.

The Exact Script That Works

Call the number on the back of your card. When prompted, say "I'm calling about my account" or "I'd like to speak to someone about my card" — avoid saying "cancel" in the IVR as some banks will try to escalate you directly to cancellation instead of retention.

YOU:
"Hi, I'm calling because my annual fee of [$X] just posted and I'm reconsidering whether this card is right for me. I love the rewards, but I'm getting good offers from other cards with no annual fee. Is there anything you can do about the annual fee this year?"

BANK REP:
[Various responses — may offer partial waiver, bonus points, or say they can't help]

IF THEY SAY NO:
"I understand. Could you tell me what my options are if I wanted to downgrade to a no-fee card instead of cancelling?"

IF THEY OFFER PARTIAL WAIVER:
"I appreciate that. Is there any way to make it a full waiver? I've been a customer for [X] years and I've been putting a lot of spend on this card."

IF THEY OFFER BONUS POINTS:
"Thank you for that. Just to confirm — those [X] points will be deposited to my account, and I'll keep the full annual fee waived/credited as well?"

Leverage that works: Mention you're a long-standing customer (X years), that you've been putting significant spend on the card, and that you've received a competing offer with no annual fee. You don't need a specific competing offer — you can mention Tangerine, Rogers, or any no-fee card. Banks value retention highly because acquiring a new customer costs 5–7x more than retaining an existing one.

Downgrading Instead of Cancelling

If the bank won't waive the fee and you're not getting value, downgrading (product change) is almost always better than cancelling. When you downgrade:

Your account number and history remain intact. Your credit limit may transfer (or be adjusted). Your credit age continues to accumulate — crucial for your credit score. The annual fee disappears because you move to a no-fee product. You don't lose your credit history on the account.

When you cancel instead: the account eventually falls off your credit report (typically after 6–10 years), reducing your average credit age and potentially increasing your overall utilization ratio. This can drop your credit score by 15–40 points.

Cards That Waive First-Year Annual Fee for New Applicants

Several Canadian cards regularly run promotions waiving the annual fee for the first year. This is different from a retention waiver — it's offered upfront to new applicants. Common first-year waiver offers (check current promotions — these change):

TD First Class Travel VI Infinite (fee waived first year). CIBC Aventura VI (fee waived first year). BMO World Elite MC (sometimes first year free). National Bank World Elite MC (periodic first-year promotions). These offers typically appear via bank websites or credit card comparison sites — not always through branch applications.

Strategy for maximizers: Apply for a first-year-free card, earn the welcome bonus (typically 20,000–60,000 points in the first year), get value from the first year at zero cost, then call at month 11 and ask for a fee waiver for year 2. If denied, downgrade to the no-fee version to preserve account history. Repeat with a new card. This "card churning" approach is advanced and works best if your credit score is strong (740+) and you're not planning to apply for a mortgage within 12 months.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many times can I call to get the annual fee waived?
Banks typically allow retention waivers once per year, per card. Calling every year to request a waiver works at some banks (particularly TD and CIBC for good customers) but isn't guaranteed each time. If you've received a waiver two years in a row and are denied in year three, that's a signal that the bank considers you fully retained and no longer needs to offer incentives. At that point, downgrading is the financially rational move.
Will calling to negotiate my annual fee hurt my credit score?
No — calling to discuss your annual fee does not trigger a hard credit inquiry. Negotiating, requesting a downgrade, or even cancelling a card does not directly affect your credit score through an inquiry. The indirect effect (account closure reducing credit age and available credit) is why downgrading is recommended over cancelling.
