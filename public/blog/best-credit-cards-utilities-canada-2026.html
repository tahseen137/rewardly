The Utility Bill Problem: Most Cards Earn Almost Nothing

Utility bills — hydro/electricity, natural gas, internet, home phone, and water — are among the largest recurring expenses for Canadian households. Yet most premium travel cards earn only 1 point per dollar on these payments, and many store-branded cards don't earn anything at all.

The core issue is that most utility providers in Canada code as generic services or utilities, not as a bonus category for any rewards card. That means you're relying on base-rate earn unless you choose a card that earns a flat rate everywhere — or one of the few cards with a utility-specific category.

Rogers Red World Elite Mastercard

The winner for utilities is the same card that wins for most base-rate spending: Rogers Red earns 2% cashback on all purchases where no bonus category applies — which is exactly how utility bills code. BEST IF YOU DON'T QUALIFY FOR ROGERS RED

Tangerine Money-Back Credit Card

The Tangerine Money-Back card earns 2% in up to 3 categories of your choice, and one of those categories is Recurring Bill Payments — which explicitly includes utility bills, internet, phone, and streaming services. There's no income requirement beyond standard creditworthiness, and no annual fee.

If you choose Recurring Bill Payments as one of your 2% categories, every utility direct debit or monthly bill pays out at 2%. The catch: you only get 2 categories at 2% by default (the third requires Tangerine savings deposits). All other spending is 0.5%.

Annual fee: $0  |  Income required: $12,000  |  Earn rate on utilities: 2% (if you select Recurring Bill Payments category)

BEST PREMIUM OPTION

SimplyCash Preferred Card from American Express

Amex SimplyCash Preferred earns 2% cashback on all purchases, everywhere Amex is accepted, with no category restrictions. Utility bills that accept Amex (or are paid through a platform that does) earn 2% flat. The card also earns a 10% welcome bonus on the first $2,000 spent in the first 3 months.

The $99/year annual fee is the trade-off. But if you spend $6,600+ per year on purchases in total, the 2% minus the fee beats the 1% cards. Amex acceptance at utility providers is lower than Visa/Mastercard, so check before relying on this for all your bills.

Annual fee: $99  |  Income required: No minimum specified  |  Earn rate on utilities: 2%

BEST FOR LOYALTY POINTS

The PC World Elite earns 3 PC Optimum points per $1 everywhere (roughly 0.9% cashback equivalent when redeemed at Shoppers/Loblaws), but it earns 45 PC Optimum points per $1 at PC-affiliated stores including PC Insurance. But if you're already earning PC Optimum points for Shoppers and grocery shopping, consolidating utilities here is fine. The welcome bonus of 20,000 PC Optimum points ($20 value) is modest.

Should You Pay Utilities Through Plastiq?

Plastiq is a payment service that lets you pay virtually any bill with a credit card for a 1.75% processing fee. For utilities, this is rarely worth it for ongoing rewards — a 2% cashback card nets only 0.25% after the fee, which on $500/month is just $1.25/month.

Plastiq exception: If you need to hit a welcome bonus spending threshold, paying utilities through Plastiq can make sense. For example, if you need to spend $3,000 in 3 months to earn 60,000 Aeroplan points worth ~$900–1,200, paying $3,000 in utilities through Plastiq costs $52.50 in fees but gets you the bonus. One-time use only — never for ongoing rewards.

The Telecom Exception: Internet Codes Differently

Internet and phone bills at Rogers, Bell, Telus, and Shaw code differently depending on the card network and the specific provider. Here's what matters:

On the Rogers Red World Elite Mastercard, Rogers internet and phone purchases earn 3% cashback (not just the 2% base rate), since Rogers services are a bonus category on their own card. If you're a Rogers customer paying Rogers internet/phone and also using Rogers Red, you earn 3% on those specific bills.

On most other cards, internet and phone bills are generic services earning 1 point or 1% per dollar. The Tangerine Recurring Bill Payments category (2%) is the best widely-available option for these.

The Strategy: Use Two Cards

The optimal setup for Canadians who want to maximize utility rewards is straightforward. Use Rogers Red for hydro, gas, and internet at non-Rogers providers (2% flat). If you're a Rogers internet/phone customer, Rogers Red earns 3% on those bills. Use Tangerine with Recurring Bill Payments selected for any utility that doesn't accept Mastercard or where you want to consolidate.

Simple rule: If you only have room for one card, Rogers Red is the default utility winner. The 2% flat rate with no annual fee and wide Mastercard acceptance beats every other option for most Canadians.

Pre-Authorized Payments vs. Manual Payments

Most utility providers give you two options: set up pre-authorized debit (PAD) from your bank account, or pay by credit card. The PAD option often comes with small discounts from some providers — but those discounts are rarely more than 1%, and you lose all credit card rewards. At Rogers Red's 2%, the credit card almost always wins even without any PAD discount.

Check your utility statements for any PAD incentives before switching to credit card payment. For most Canadians, the math favours credit card payment and rewards over any PAD discount.
