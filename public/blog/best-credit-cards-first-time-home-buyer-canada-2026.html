Best Credit Cards for First-Time Home Buyers Canada 2026

Your credit card strategy in the 12 months before your mortgage application can save you $5,000–$40,000 over the life of your mortgage. Here's exactly what to do.

Why Credit Cards Matter Before a Mortgage

Getting the best mortgage rate in Canada is fundamentally a credit score competition. At the Big Six banks, borrowers with 760+ scores typically receive rates 0.3–0.6% lower than those with 700 scores. On a $600,000 mortgage over 25 years, 0.4% is approximately $42,000 in additional interest. Your credit card behavior is the single fastest thing you can change to improve your score.

The 12-Month Credit Card Timeline Before Buying

12 Months Before

Stop applying for new cards — completely

Each new application creates a hard inquiry (-5 to -15 points). Stop all new card applications at least 12 months before your target mortgage application date. New accounts also lower your average account age, which hurts your score further.

9–12 Months

Pull your free credit reports from Equifax and TransUnion

Canadians are entitled to one free credit report per year from each bureau at annualcreditreport.ca. Check for errors — 1 in 5 Canadians has a material error. Dispute any inaccuracies in writing (takes 30–45 days to resolve). Common errors: old accounts not closed, accounts belonging to someone else, incorrect late payment records.

6 Months

Get credit utilization below 10%

Credit utilization (balance / limit) affects 30% of your credit score. Lenders see your utilization at the time of the credit pull — not just your payment history. If you have $20,000 in limits and typically carry $8,000 in balances, you're at 40% utilization. Pay down balances to bring utilization below 10% ($2,000 on $20K limits). Each major bureau updates this monthly, so improvements show within 30–60 days.

6 Months

Do NOT close old credit card accounts

Closing an old card reduces your total available credit (raising utilization) AND reduces your average account age. Both hurt your score. Keep your oldest cards open, even if you rarely use them. Put a small recurring charge (like a $5/month subscription) on old cards to keep them active and prevent the bank from closing them for inactivity.

3 Months

Set up autopay on every card — minimum payment at minimum

Payment history is 35% of your credit score — the single largest factor. A single missed payment can drop your score by 50–100 points and stays on your report for 6 years in Canada. Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment on every card. During busy house-hunting periods, missed payments from distraction are a real risk. Autopay eliminates it.

Mortgage Month

Don't make large new purchases on cards before closing

Some lenders re-check your credit score between pre-approval and closing (within 5–10 days of closing). A sudden large balance increase on your cards can trigger a re-underwriting review. Keep card activity normal in the 30 days before closing. Never buy furniture or appliances on credit before you have the keys.

Do's and Don'ts Summary

✅ DO

  • Keep all existing cards open
  • Pay every card in full each month
  • Set up autopay
  • Keep utilization below 10%
  • Request a credit limit increase (no hard pull on existing cards)
  • Dispute credit report errors in writing
  • Keep a small recurring charge on old cards

❌ DON'T

  • Apply for any new credit cards in the 12 months before
  • Close any credit card accounts
  • Make large balance transfers or cash advances
  • Buy furniture on credit before closing
  • Miss a single payment
  • Suddenly pay off all debt right before applying (looks suspicious to some lenders)
  • Co-sign a loan for someone else

Frequently Asked Questions

Will getting pre-approved for a mortgage hurt my credit score?
Yes — a mortgage pre-approval triggers a hard inquiry, which can lower your score by 5–15 points. However, most credit bureaus treat multiple mortgage inquiries within a 14-day window as a single inquiry (rate shopping exception). So if you're comparing rates from multiple lenders, do all applications within 2 weeks to minimize the hit. Don't spread them over several months.
Should I pay off my credit cards completely before applying?
Yes, you want to bring utilization down — ideally below 10–15%. However, don't do it all in one lump sum the week before your application if you've been carrying balances for years, as some lenders may flag this as unusual activity. Gradual paydown starting 3–6 months before your application is more natural and more effective for your score.
Can having too many credit cards hurt my mortgage application?
The number of cards matters less than the utilization and payment history on those cards. Having 4–6 cards with low balances and a long history is generally viewed positively by lenders. What hurts is multiple recently opened accounts (all under 2 years old), high balances relative to limits, or any missed payments. Quantity alone is not a red flag.
