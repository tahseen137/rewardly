Your credit score follows you for life. Start building it in school and you'll graduate with a 700+ credit score, qualifying for better apartments, better rates on car loans, and premium rewards cards the day you land your first job.

All the cards below have no income requirement and are designed for people with limited or no credit history. Most can be obtained with just a valid SIN, a student ID, and a Canadian bank account.

Why get a credit card now

  • Credit history takes time — each month you hold a card in good standing adds a month to your credit age. Start at 20, graduate at 24 with 4 years of clean history.
  • Average age of accounts matters — opening a card now increases your account age, which improves your score when you open cards later.
  • Free money — student cards still earn cashback or points. 1–2% back on all your student spending adds up to $100–$300/year depending on your budget.
  • Emergency fund alternative — having a credit line means a surprise dental bill or car repair doesn't wipe out your savings.

What is a secured card? You deposit money as collateral — that deposit becomes your credit limit. The card reports to Equifax and TransUnion just like a regular card. After 12–18 months of on-time payments, you can request an upgrade to an unsecured card and get your deposit back.

Who needs this: Students with no Canadian credit history at all (including international students), or students who were declined for regular student cards.

✓ Best option if every other card has declined you. Secured cards build credit equally well — no penalty for using one.

How to build credit fast as a student

  1. Pay your full balance every month. Never carry a balance. Interest charges (20%+ APR) will cost far more than any rewards you earn. Treat the card like a debit card with better rewards.
  2. Keep utilization under 30%. If your limit is $1,000, try not to have more than $300 on the card at any given time — even if you pay it off. High utilization hurts your score mid-month if it's reported at the wrong time.
  3. Don't apply for multiple cards at once. Each application triggers a hard credit inquiry, which dings your score by 5–10 points for 6–12 months. Get one student card now, use it for a year, then consider a second.
  4. Set up autopay. A single missed payment tanks your credit score by 60–100+ points. Set autopay for at least the minimum payment — ideally the full balance.
  5. Ask for a limit increase after 6 months. A higher limit with the same spending lowers your utilization ratio, which improves your score. Most issuers will grant a small increase after 6 months of on-time payments.

5 mistakes new cardholders make

  1. Only paying the minimum. The minimum payment is designed to keep you in debt as long as possible. At 20% APR, a $500 balance paid at minimum takes 10+ years to clear. Pay the full balance every month.
  2. Using a credit card for cash advances. Cash advances have no grace period — interest starts the moment you take the advance, at 21–23% APR. Never use your credit card at an ATM.
  3. Closing the card after using a welcome bonus. Closing a card removes it from your average account age. If you got a student card for the welcome bonus, keep it open and put one small recurring charge on it (like a $1.29/month app subscription).
  4. Not checking your credit report. Pull your free credit reports from Equifax and TransUnion once a year to check for errors or fraud. Both offer free annual reports by mail or through Borrowell/Credit Karma online.
  5. Spending more because you're earning rewards. A 2% cashback rate does not offset 20% credit card interest. The math only works if you pay your balance in full every month.

FAQ

Can international students get a Canadian credit card?

Yes, but most regular student cards require a Canadian credit history or SIN. If you're new to Canada, start with Home Trust Secured Visa — they accept applications from people with no Canadian credit history. You'll need a Canadian bank account to fund the deposit.

Do I need income to get a student credit card?

No. Student cards like BMO SPC, Scotiabank Scene+ Student, and CIBC Dividend Student all have no income requirement. You verify your student status instead. You do need a Canadian bank account and a valid SIN.

What credit score do I need for a student card?

Most student cards are designed for people with no credit history at all — so a credit score of 0 (no history) is fine. If you have bad credit from a previous account, you may be declined and should try a secured card instead.

Should I get a no-fee card or pay for a premium card as a student?

Start with no-fee cards — the difference in earn rates between a free student card and a premium card doesn't justify the fee while you're a student with limited income. Wait until you graduate and your income qualifies you for premium cards like the Scotia Momentum Visa Infinite or BMO CashBack World Elite.

How long until I can upgrade to a better card?

Most people are able to get standard (non-student) premium rewards cards within 2–3 years of opening their first card, assuming clean payment history, no collections, and meeting income requirements. Some premium cards have no minimum income requirement (like Tangerine), which can be a good intermediate step post-graduation.
