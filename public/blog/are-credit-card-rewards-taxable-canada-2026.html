Are Credit Card Rewards Taxable in Canada? (2026 Guide)

The short answer: for most Canadians, credit card rewards are NOT taxable. But there are important exceptions — especially for business cards, employee cards, and sign-up bonuses. Here's what the CRA says.

The CRA's Position on Credit Card Rewards

The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) has addressed credit card rewards in its interpretation bulletins. The general position is that rewards earned through personal credit cards used for personal spending are considered a discount on purchases — not income — and are therefore not taxable.

✅ NOT Taxable: Personal Credit Card Rewards

Cash back, Aeroplan miles, Scene+ points, and other rewards earned on a personal credit card used for personal purchases are generally not considered taxable income by the CRA. You do not report them on your tax return.

This applies to: cash back deposited as statement credits, travel points redeemed for flights and hotels, retailer loyalty points (PC Optimum, Air Miles, Scene+), and travel miles (Aeroplan).

The Key Exceptions

Exception 1: Business Credit Cards Used for Business Expenses

⚠️ Potentially Taxable: Business Card Rewards

If you use a business credit card to pay business expenses and claim those expenses as deductions, any rewards earned on those expenses may need to be reported as business income. The CRA considers rewards earned on deductible business expenses as a reduction in your deductible cost — and if the rewards have cash value, they may constitute a taxable benefit.

Example: A self-employed consultant charges $50,000 in deductible business expenses on an Amex Business Gold card and earns $2,000 in MR points. The CRA's position is that the $2,000 effectively reduces the cost of those business expenses and should be included in income in the year received — or the deduction claimed should be reduced by the reward value.

In practice, this is rarely audited for small business owners with modest rewards, but the technical obligation exists. Consult a tax professional if your annual business card rewards are material.

Exception 2: Employee Benefits (Employer-Paid Expenses)

⚠️ Potentially Taxable: Employee Rewards on Business Expenses

If your employer pays your credit card expenses (travel, meals, etc.) and you keep the rewards, the CRA considers this a taxable employee benefit. Your employer should include the value of those rewards on your T4. This is most common in companies where employees book corporate travel on personal cards and collect the points.

The CRA issued IT Bulletin IT-470R (Employees' Fringe Benefits) which addresses loyalty points. When an employer reimburses an employee for expenses and the employee retains the points, this could constitute a taxable benefit. Most employers don't track this precisely, and employees rarely report it — but technically the obligation exists.

Exception 3: Referral Bonuses and Sign-Up Promotions

🔶 Depends: Sign-Up Bonuses and Referral Rewards

Sign-up bonuses (e.g., 60,000 Aeroplan points for meeting a spending threshold) are generally considered promotions/rebates — not income. However, referral bonuses paid in cash (e.g., "earn $50 when your friend signs up") are more likely to be considered income, similar to other referral fees. Cryptocurrency rewards or high-value cash payments from card issuers could also be income.

Are Annual Fees Tax Deductible?

For personal credit cards: No — annual fees are not deductible.

For business credit cards used entirely for business: the annual fee may be deductible as a business expense. If the card is used for mixed personal and business spending, only the business-use proportion of the fee is deductible. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

Important: Interest paid on personal credit cards is NOT deductible, even if you use the card for business expenses. Interest on a business card used for investment income may be deductible — this is a complex area that requires professional advice.

Practical Implications for Most Canadians

For the vast majority of Canadian credit card users — employees using their own personal card for personal spending — credit card rewards are completely tax-free. You earn Aeroplan miles, Scene+ points, or cash back, and owe no tax on them at any point, including when you redeem them.

Summary for most Canadians: Personal credit card rewards = not taxable. Earn as much as you want. Redeem for travel, merchandise, or statement credits. File your taxes as normal. The CRA is not interested in your personal Aeroplan balance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to declare Aeroplan points on my tax return?
No — for personal Aeroplan points earned on personal spending, there is no reporting requirement. Aeroplan points are treated as a discount on purchases, not as income. You do not include them on your T1 return.
What if I earn rewards on both personal and business expenses on the same card?
This is a gray area. Strictly speaking, rewards earned on the business portion of your spending may be considered income or a reduction in deductible expenses. In practice, most small business owners who mix personal and business spending on one card don't track rewards by category. If your annual business rewards are material (thousands of dollars), consult a CPA. If they're modest (a few hundred dollars), the risk of audit for this specific issue is very low.
Are PC Optimum points taxable when I redeem them at Shoppers?
No. PC Optimum points redeemed at Shoppers Drug Mart or Loblaws stores are a discount on your grocery/pharmacy purchase — not income. They're treated the same as a coupon discount. No tax reporting required.
Is this guidance official CRA policy?
The CRA's position is set out in Interpretation Bulletin IT-470R (Employees' Fringe Benefits) and related guidance. For personal rewards on personal spending, the CRA has consistently treated these as non-taxable discounts. Tax law can evolve — for large amounts or complex situations, consult a tax professional who can review the most current CRA guidance for your specific circumstances.
