Best Balance Transfer Credit Cards in Canada 2026

Carrying a balance at 19–20% interest? Moving it to a 0% or low-rate card can save you hundreds — or thousands — in interest this year.

The average Canadian credit card charges 19.99% interest on balances. If you're carrying $5,000 on a typical rewards card and only making minimum payments, you're paying roughly $1,000/year in interest — money that disappears and builds zero equity for you.

A balance transfer card lets you move that debt to a card with a 0% or low promotional rate (typically 1.99%–9.99%) for 6–12 months. The goal: pay down principal aggressively during the promotional window before the rate resets.

⚠️ Balance transfer rules to know before applying: Most cards charge a transfer fee of 1%–3% of the transferred amount. The promotional rate applies only to the transferred balance, not new purchases (new charges typically accrue at the full 19.99% rate immediately). Missing a minimum payment can void the promotional rate. Plan to pay the full transferred amount during the promotional window.

Balance Transfer Strategy: How to Make It Work

  1. Calculate your target monthly payment. Divide your total transferred balance by the number of months in your promotional window. This is the amount you must pay every month to hit zero before the promo rate expires.
  2. Set up automatic payments. Never miss a payment — missing one can void the promotional rate at most issuers, reverting your balance to the standard 19.99% immediately.
  3. Don't use the balance transfer card for new purchases. New purchases on most BT cards accrue interest immediately at the standard rate. Use a different card (or cash) for everyday spending during the paydown period.
  4. Apply for the BT card before transferring. You'll need to have the new card approved and active before initiating the transfer. The transfer can take 1–2 weeks to process — during that time, keep making minimum payments on your old card.
  5. Plan what happens at month 12. If you still have a balance when the promo rate expires, consider: (a) applying for another BT promotion at a different issuer, (b) moving to a low-rate card like National Bank Syncro, or (c) aggressively increasing payments in months 10–12 to clear it before reset.
Multiple balance transfers: Opening multiple credit card accounts in a short period can hurt your credit score (each application is a hard inquiry). Space out applications by at least 6 months if you plan to do sequential transfers. Also, closing old accounts after a transfer reduces your total credit limit, which can raise your utilization ratio — another credit score factor. Consider keeping old accounts open with zero balance.

Balance Transfer vs Debt Consolidation Loan

Another option for high-interest debt is a personal debt consolidation loan from your bank. Key comparisons:

  • Balance transfer card: 0% for 6–12 months (promotional), then resets. Best for debt you can pay off within the promo window. No monthly loan payment — you control payment amounts.
  • Debt consolidation loan: Typically 6–12% interest, fixed term (24–60 months). Better for large amounts ($15,000+) that would take years to pay off. Fixed monthly payment provides structure.

For under $15,000 that you can realistically eliminate in 12 months, a balance transfer card at 0% is usually better. For larger amounts or longer payoff timelines, a consolidation loan at 6–12% beats a BT card that will revert to 19.99%.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a balance transfer hurt your credit score?

Applying for a new card involves a hard credit inquiry, which can temporarily lower your score by 5–10 points. However, successfully paying down your balance improves your credit utilization ratio over time — which has a larger positive impact on your score long-term. Net effect: usually positive after 6–12 months of consistent payments.

Can I transfer a balance from one bank to another?

Yes — balance transfers between different banks are standard and accepted. You cannot transfer a balance between two cards from the same bank (e.g., CIBC to CIBC). If your high-interest debt is with BMO, apply for the MBNA True Line or Scotiabank Value Visa to transfer it. If it's with Scotia, apply for MBNA or CIBC Select Visa.

What happens if I don't pay off the balance in the promotional period?

The remaining balance is subject to the card's standard purchase interest rate (typically 12.99%–19.99%) from the day the promotional period ends. Some issuers apply back-interest retroactively on the entire original transfer amount if you don't pay it in full — read the cardholder agreement carefully. MBNA's True Line does NOT back-date interest; it simply converts the remaining balance to the standard rate going forward.

Are there limits on how much I can transfer?

Most banks limit balance transfers to 95% of your approved credit limit on the new card. If your new card has a $6,000 limit, you can transfer up to $5,700. Apply for a high enough credit limit to cover your entire balance — and note that the balance transfer fee itself counts against the limit.

Can I earn rewards on a balance transfer card?

No — balance transfer cards and low-rate cards generally don't earn rewards points or cashback. They're debt management tools, not rewards tools. Once you've paid off the balance, close or stop using the BT card and switch to a rewards card like Scotiabank Momentum, Amex SimplyCash Preferred, or CIBC Dividend to start earning on your spending.
