Canadians spend $60–$150/month on streaming. The right credit card earns 2–5% back on Netflix, Spotify, Disney+, Crave, Apple TV+, and all recurring bills — without any extra effort.

How Streaming Services Code on Credit Cards

Streaming services and digital subscriptions typically code under MCC 4899 (Cable, Satellite, and Other Pay Television and Radio Services) or MCC 5968 (Direct Marketing — Subscription Merchants). Cards with "recurring bill" or "subscription" earn bonus rates activate on these MCCs.

The Subscription Creep Audit

The average Canadian has 5–8 streaming or subscription services, many of which auto-renew on a credit card without the cardholder noticing. An annual audit often reveals subscriptions for services you no longer use.

Strategy: Route all subscription charges to one dedicated card, then review that card's statement quarterly. Any recurring charge you can't immediately identify is a subscription worth cancelling. Canadians commonly discover $15–$40/month in forgotten subscriptions during this audit.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my credit card earn more on streaming from the US (USD billed)?
Some streaming services (Apple TV+, Amazon Prime, older Netflix plans) may bill in USD if your account is tied to US billing. In that case, you'll pay a 2.5% FX fee on standard Canadian cards on top of losing out on streaming bonus earn rates if the card's system doesn't recognize the USD charge as a streaming MCC. The Scotia Passport VI and Scotia Gold Amex (both 0% FX) are better choices for USD-billed streaming subscriptions.
Does Netflix earn at the streaming rate or recurring bill rate on my card?
It depends on your card issuer's MCC classification. Some cards classify Netflix under "streaming" (MCC 4899), others under "recurring bills" (auto-charges). Cards like BMO eclipse rise have a specific streaming category. Cards like Scotia Momentum VI earn 4% on recurring bills — which covers Netflix regardless of MCC. When in doubt, make one Netflix payment and check your statement's category coding, or call your issuer.
