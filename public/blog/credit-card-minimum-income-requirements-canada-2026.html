Credit Card Minimum Income Requirements in Canada 2026

Visa Infinite needs $60K. World Elite needs $80K. Here's the full breakdown — and what to do when you don't quite qualify.

Why Income Requirements Exist

Credit card income thresholds are set by a combination of card network mandates (Visa and Mastercard impose minimums on their Infinite and World Elite tier cards) and issuer-specific criteria based on risk management. The income threshold exists to ensure cardholders can manage the credit responsibly and are likely to spend enough to make the card profitable for both the cardholder and issuer.

Visa and Mastercard set floors for their premium tiers — but individual banks can set their own minimums higher. RBC's Avion Visa Infinite, for example, may have slightly different income verification practices than CIBC's Aeroplan Visa Infinite, even though both are Visa Infinite cards with the same network minimum.

Important: Income requirements are minimums, not guarantees. You can meet the income threshold and still be denied if your credit score is low, you have too much existing debt, or you've applied for too many cards recently. Meeting the income requirement just means you pass that one filter.

What If You Don't Meet the Income Threshold?

Your Options When Income Falls Short

Apply for the Amex version instead

American Express doesn't use Visa/MC income tiers. The Amex Cobalt and Gold Rewards offer comparable or better rewards than many Visa Infinite cards — with no stated income minimum. Amex leans on credit history and spending more than income documents.

Use household income if applicable

If your household income meets the threshold (even if personal doesn't), you qualify. A household earning $105,000 combined ($60K + $45K) meets the $100K Visa Infinite threshold even if individually neither person hits $60K.

Build to the threshold first

If you're at $55K income and targeting a $60K-minimum card, getting there may be 6–18 months away. In the meantime, use no-fee alternatives like Tangerine or Simplii to build rewards and payment history.

Try a different bank's Visa Infinite

All Visa Infinite cards have the same $60K/$100K floor — but enforcement varies. Some banks verify income documents; others rely on stated income. Apply honestly, but recognize that not all issuers are equally strict.

The Amex Advantage: No Income Minimums

American Express Canada is the most significant exception to income-gating in the Canadian credit card market. Amex does not use Visa or Mastercard's tier system and sets its own approval criteria. The Cobalt Card ($191.88/year) earns 5x Amex points on dining and groceries — competitive with any Visa Infinite — and has no income requirement published anywhere on their site.

This makes Amex cards the go-to recommendation for Canadians who earn strong rewards-card-worthy spending patterns but haven't yet crossed the $60K income threshold. A $45K income earner who spends $800/month on dining and groceries will get far more value from the Cobalt than from a no-fee cashback card.

Summary: If your income is under $60K and you want premium rewards, start with Amex Cobalt. If you're between $60–79K, go Visa Infinite. If you're at $80K+, World Elite cards are accessible and often offer better base rates (Rogers Red, BMO Ascend) than Visa Infinite equivalents.

Income Verification: Do Banks Check?

Most credit card applications are approved based on stated income — the number you enter on the form. Banks do not always request income documentation (T4, NOA, or pay stubs) unless the application falls into certain risk categories: very high credit limit requests, high debt loads, or inconsistencies in the application.

Never overstate your income. Providing false income information on a credit application is considered fraud in Canada. Banks can request income verification at any time, including after the card is issued. If they find a discrepancy, they can close the account and pursue collections on any balance. Always state your accurate income.
