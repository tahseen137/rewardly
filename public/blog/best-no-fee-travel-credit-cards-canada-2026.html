Best No-Fee Travel Credit Cards in Canada 2026

You don't need to pay an annual fee to earn meaningful travel rewards. The right $0-fee card earns Aeroplan points, cash back for flights, or flexible travel redemptions — for nothing extra each year.

Do No-Fee Cards Really Work for Travel Rewards?

Yes — with one important caveat. No-fee cards earn fewer points per dollar than premium fee cards, and almost never include travel insurance (emergency medical, trip cancellation). They're best for: light travelers who want to accumulate points slowly, as a "keeper" card for credit history alongside a fee card, or as a primary card for someone who rarely travels but wants to earn toward occasional flights.

The math: A no-fee card earning 1% on $30,000/year in annual spend = $300/yr in rewards. A premium card at 3% on $30,000/yr = $900/yr, minus $120 annual fee = $780. The premium card earns more. But if you wouldn't pay the fee, $300 for free beats $0.

Best No-Fee Cards for Travel Rewards

#1

BMO eclipse rise Visa Card

$0 Fee · BMO Rewards → Travel
Best No-Fee for Travel Redemptions
5x BMO Rewards on 5 categories

The BMO eclipse rise earns 5x BMO Rewards on grocery, dining, gas, drugstores, and streaming — all redeemable for travel through BMO's travel portal at ~0.67¢/point. 5x on everyday categories = ~1.7% toward travel, with no annual fee.

BMO Rewards can also be redeemed as statement credits at ~0.5¢/pt (less than the travel rate), so travel remains the best redemption path. No income requirement, no cap on 5x earning.

Best for: Anyone who wants to earn toward travel without a fee, particularly those with high grocery/dining/gas spend. The most versatile no-fee travel-adjacent card in Canada.
#2

PC Financial World Elite Mastercard

$0 Fee · PC Optimum → PC Travel
Best No-Fee for PC Travel
4.5% at Loblaws/SDM → PC Travel

The PC Financial WE MC earns 4.5% in PC Optimum points at Loblaws and Shoppers — redeemable for travel through PC Travel or as direct grocery savings. No annual fee, travel insurance included (unusual for a no-fee card), $80K income requirement.

PC Optimum points can be applied to PC Travel bookings: flights, hotels, car rentals. 10,000 pts = $10 on any booking. This makes the PC Financial WE MC the rare no-fee card that both earns strongly on groceries AND can be redeemed for travel.

Best for: PC Optimum grocery shoppers who want to convert grocery rewards to travel. The free travel insurance is a standout benefit for a no-fee card.
#3

Simplii Financial Cash Back Visa

$0 Fee · Cash Back
4% on Dining → Use for Travel
4% on restaurants ($5K/yr cap)

Simplii earns 4% cash back on restaurant and bar purchases (up to $5,000/year), 1.5% on gas and pharmacies, 0.5% everywhere else. Cash back is deposited monthly to your Simplii account — you choose what to spend it on, including travel.

No annual fee. "Cash back for travel" means you use the cash-back deposit to offset travel expenses. Not a direct travel program, but functionally earns 4% on dining — excellent if restaurants are a key spending category.

Best for: Restaurant-heavy spenders who want cash to offset travel costs without managing a points program. The 4% dining rate is the best no-fee dining rate in Canada.
#4

Home Trust Preferred Visa

$0 Fee · 1% Cash Back · 0% FX
Best for International Travelers
1% cash back + 0% FX fee

The Home Trust Preferred Visa earns only 1% on all purchases — but it has 0% foreign transaction fees. No annual fee. For Canadians who spend significantly in foreign currencies while traveling, saving 2.5% FX on every international purchase is far more valuable than earning 1% extra on those same purchases.

Example: on $5,000 USD spending while traveling, Home Trust saves $125 in FX fees vs. a standard 2.5% card. A card earning 2% but charging 2.5% FX effectively earns -0.5% on that spend. Home Trust at 1% + 0% FX = effectively 3.5% better than a 2%/2.5% FX card on foreign purchases.

Best for: Frequent international travelers who don't want to pay an annual fee but want to avoid the 2.5% FX tax on every foreign purchase. Best used ONLY abroad — the 1% base rate means a fee card wins for domestic spending.

At 1 Aeroplan pt/$ and ~1.5¢/pt on economy Air Canada flights, that's effectively ~1.5% return — modest but with Aeroplan flexibility (potential 3–4¢/pt if you use points for premium cabins). The free first checked bag benefit is unusual for a no-fee card.

Best for: Occasional Air Canada travelers who want to accumulate Aeroplan points at no cost and want the free bag benefit on Air Canada flights.

When to Upgrade to a Fee Card

A no-fee travel card makes sense as a starting point, but there's a clear breakeven threshold where upgrading to a premium fee card earns more:

Breakeven calculation: If you earn 1% on a no-fee card and a $120 fee card earns 3%, the fee card earns 2% more. On $120 / 2% = $6,000 in annual spending. If you spend more than $6,000/year on the card, the premium card wins net of the fee. Most Canadian households who use their card for all daily spending (groceries, gas, dining, bills) clear this threshold easily.

The exception: travel insurance. No-fee travel cards almost never include emergency medical insurance. If you travel internationally and don't have other coverage, a $120–$150 travel fee card's insurance benefit can be worth hundreds in standalone policy savings alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I earn Aeroplan points for free (no annual fee)?
Yes — CIBC offers a no-fee Aeroplan Visa. TD also offers a no-fee Aeroplan Visa. Both earn 1 Aeroplan point per dollar with no annual fee. The earn rate is lower than the $139 Visa Infinite versions, but points accumulate in the same Aeroplan account and have identical redemption flexibility.
Is it worth getting a fee card just for travel insurance?
Often yes. A standalone travel medical insurance policy for a couple costs $200–$600+ per trip depending on age and destination. A $120–$150 fee card covering 2+ trips per year is frequently cheaper. Run the numbers: if your annual travel insurance spend would exceed the annual fee, the card pays for itself on insurance alone (with points as a bonus).
Can I hold a no-fee card alongside a premium fee card?
Absolutely — this is a common strategy. Use a premium fee card for the categories it earns best on, and keep a no-fee card like Home Trust Preferred Visa specifically for international travel (0% FX). Or keep a no-fee Aeroplan card as a backup for places that don't accept Amex while your primary card is a Scotia Gold Amex.
