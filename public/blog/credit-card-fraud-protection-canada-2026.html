Credit Card Fraud Protection Canada 2026

Canadian credit card holders have strong legal protections against fraud. Zero liability means you're not responsible for unauthorized charges — if you report them promptly and follow the rules.

Zero Liability: What It Means for Canadians

All major credit card networks operating in Canada — Visa, Mastercard, and American Express — offer Zero Liability protection. This means if your card is used fraudulently without your knowledge or authorization, you are not responsible for those charges. The issuing bank bears the loss.

NetworkZero Liability PolicyKey Conditions
Visa Canada100% — $0 liability for unauthorized transactionsMust report promptly, must not be grossly negligent (sharing PIN)

Canadian law reinforces this: Under the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC) Code of Conduct for Credit and Debit Card Industry, Canadian cardholders must be protected from unauthorized transactions. Zero liability is not just a network policy — it's backed by regulatory guidance. This is why credit cards are safer than debit cards for most Canadians.

When Zero Liability Does NOT Apply

Zero liability has limits. You may be liable if:

SituationYour Liability
You shared your PIN or password with someone elseMay lose protection — sharing credentials is considered gross negligence
You did not report the fraud promptlyDelayed reporting can limit recourse — report within 30 days of statement
You were complicit in the fraudFull liability — zero liability never covers intentional fraud
Debit card used (not credit card)Debit fraud protection is weaker — zero liability may not apply depending on the bank and circumstances
Business credit card (some issuers)Zero liability may not cover business cards — check your cardholder agreement

What to Do Immediately If Your Card Is Compromised

1

Call your card issuer immediately — 24/7 fraud line

Every major Canadian bank has a 24/7 fraud line on the back of your card. Call and report suspicious activity. Ask them to immediately freeze or cancel the compromised card and issue a replacement. The fraud clock starts when you're first made aware of suspicious charges — don't wait.

2

Review your full statement for all suspicious transactions

Fraudsters often make small test charges ($1–$5) before larger purchases to confirm the card works. Review your full statement — not just the large transactions. Report every unauthorized charge, no matter how small.

3

File a dispute for each unauthorized charge

Ask the bank to open a dispute (chargeback) for each fraudulent transaction. The bank investigates and typically credits your account within 5–10 business days (sometimes faster). You may receive a provisional credit while the investigation is ongoing.

4

Update all saved card information

If you store your card number with merchants or services (Amazon, Netflix, your gym, insurance, etc.), update those to your new card number as soon as your replacement arrives. Forgotten autopays on the old cancelled card will decline and could lapse services.

5

Consider placing a fraud alert on your credit file

If you believe your personal information (not just card number) was compromised, contact Equifax Canada (1-800-465-7166) or TransUnion Canada (1-800-663-9980) to place a fraud alert or security freeze on your credit file. This requires creditors to verify your identity before opening new accounts in your name.

Credit Card vs. Debit Card: Why Credit Is Safer

Credit cards have stronger fraud protection than debit cards for a fundamental reason: credit card fraud uses the bank's money until resolved, while debit card fraud takes your money immediately. With a credit card, the fraudulent charge shows on your statement — you dispute it, and the bank refunds you after investigation. With debit, the money leaves your account immediately and recovery can take longer and be less certain.

Recommendation: Use a credit card (not debit or prepaid) for all purchases where fraud risk is elevated — online shopping, travel, unfamiliar merchants, gas stations with external card readers, and international travel. Pay the credit card balance in full monthly to avoid interest.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to dispute a fraudulent credit card charge in Canada?
Most Canadian credit card issuers require you to dispute a charge within 30–90 days of the statement date on which the charge appeared. For fraud (unauthorized transactions), report as soon as you become aware — even if the statement period has passed. Visa and Mastercard chargeback rules allow disputes for fraud up to 120 days from the original transaction date in most cases. The sooner you report, the stronger your case. Never wait until "next month" to report suspected fraud.
Will disputing a fraudulent charge affect my credit score?
No — filing a fraud dispute with your credit card issuer does not affect your credit score. The fraudulent transaction itself (if it caused you to miss a payment or pushed your utilization to 100%) could have an indirect impact, but the dispute process itself is neutral. If a replacement card is issued, it will have a different card number but the same credit account — no new inquiry, no change to your credit history.
What is the FCAC and what role does it play in credit card fraud?
The Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC) is the federal regulator for financial services consumers. FCAC enforces consumer protection provisions for credit cards under the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada Act. If you've exhausted your issuer's dispute process and believe your rights were violated, you can file a complaint with FCAC at canada.ca/en/financial-consumer-agency. FCAC does not resolve individual disputes but can investigate systemic issues and enforce compliance with consumer protection rules.
