Credit Card Chargeback & Dispute Canada 2026

Charged for something you didn't receive, didn't authorize, or a fraudulent transaction? You have strong rights in Canada — here's exactly how to exercise them.

Dispute vs. Chargeback — The Difference

Dispute: A formal complaint filed with your credit card issuer (your bank) about a charge on your account. You're asking your bank to investigate the transaction. This is always the first step.

Chargeback: The bank-initiated reversal of a charge after the dispute is reviewed in your favour. When your bank files a chargeback, the funds are removed from the merchant's account and returned to you. The merchant has the right to contest the chargeback, which can lead to further investigation.

Common reasons Canadians file disputes and chargebacks include: item not received (ordered but never arrived), merchandise not as described (significantly different from what was advertised), unauthorized transaction (fraud or unknown charge), duplicate charge (charged twice for same transaction), defective goods, cancelled subscription still being charged, merchant closed before delivering goods/services.

Don't wait: The 120-day window runs from the transaction date, not the date you notice the problem. For purchases that haven't arrived (e.g., "item not received"), the clock starts when the expected delivery date passed. For subscriptions, each billing cycle has its own 120-day window. File as soon as you determine there's a problem.

How to File a Credit Card Dispute in Canada — Step by Step

  1. Contact the Merchant First

    For disputes that aren't outright fraud (item not received, defective goods), contact the merchant first and document the attempt. Credit card networks require evidence that you tried to resolve the issue with the merchant. Keep records: email threads, chat transcripts, phone call dates, any merchant response. If the merchant resolves the issue, no further action needed.

  2. Contact Your Credit Card Issuer

    Call the number on the back of your card (or log in to online banking) and tell them you want to dispute a charge. For unauthorized/fraud transactions, this is your first call — do not contact the merchant for fraud. Provide: transaction date and amount, merchant name, reason for dispute, and documentation if available.

  3. Your Bank Issues a Provisional Credit

    Most Canadian banks provide a provisional (temporary) credit to your account while the investigation is underway. This removes the disputed amount from your balance. If the investigation finds in the merchant's favour, the provisional credit is reversed. Timeline for provisional credit: typically 5–15 business days after filing.

  4. Submit Supporting Documentation

    Your bank will likely request documentation to support your claim. Common evidence: screenshots of your order confirmation, tracking information showing non-delivery, photos of defective items, email records with the merchant, screenshots of the merchant's website description vs. what you received, subscription cancellation confirmations.

  5. Wait for Investigation Resolution

    Canadian banks typically resolve disputes within 30–45 business days. During this time, the merchant may submit a rebuttal (counter-evidence). If the bank finds in your favour, the provisional credit becomes permanent. If against you, the provisional credit is reversed and you'll receive a letter explaining the decision.

  6. If Denied — Escalate to FCAC

    If you believe your dispute was wrongly denied, you can escalate to the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC) at canada.ca/en/financial-consumer-agency. FCAC oversees credit card dispute resolution rules. You can also file a complaint with your bank's internal ombudsman, then with the ADR Chambers Banking Ombuds Office (adrbo.com) or Ombudsman for Banking Services and Investments (OBSI). These are free services.

What ISN'T a valid chargeback reason: Changed your mind, didn't read return policy before buying, dissatisfied with a service that was correctly rendered, or purchases made under a gambling or subscription you authorized but forgot. Misuse of chargebacks (called "friendly fraud") is taken seriously by banks and can result in account closure.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does disputing a charge hurt my credit score?
No — filing a dispute does not affect your credit score. Credit scores reflect your payment behaviour as a borrower, not your use of consumer protection rights. The disputed amount may be temporarily credited to your account during the investigation, which can actually lower your utilization ratio and slightly help your score.
Can I dispute a charge at a restaurant or retail store I visited in person?
Yes, but with limitations. You can dispute in-person charges for unauthorized use (someone used your card fraudulently), for items that were never delivered after you paid, or for goods that were significantly defective. You generally cannot successfully dispute an in-person charge because you simply didn't like the food or service — the merchant can prove the transaction was authorized (you were present, signed, or tapped). For in-person disputes of legitimate charges, your remedy is typically through the merchant's customer service, not a chargeback.
