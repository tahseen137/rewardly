Can You Buy Crypto With a Credit Card in Canada? (2026)

The honest answer: usually no — or yes, but at a cost most people don't realize. Here's what actually happens when you try.

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read

⚠️ The Short Answer

Most major Canadian banks either block crypto purchases outright or classify them as a cash advance — triggering a 22.99%–25.99% interest rate with a 3–5% transaction fee and zero grace period. Interest starts the moment the transaction posts, not at the end of the billing cycle. Using a credit card to buy crypto is almost always a losing financial move.

Cryptocurrency exchanges accept payment in a variety of ways: Interac e-Transfer, bank wire, debit card, prepaid card, and sometimes credit card. The problem for Canadian credit card users isn't that exchanges won't take the payment — it's what happens on the bank's end when they do.

How Canadian Banks Classify Crypto Purchases

When a merchant processes a credit card payment, the merchant category code (MCC) assigned to the transaction determines how the bank treats it. Cryptocurrency exchanges typically register under one of two MCCs:

MCC 6051 — Quasi-Cash/Non-Financial Institutions: This is the most common MCC for crypto exchanges. Canadian banks treat MCC 6051 transactions identically to cash advances — which means the cash advance interest rate (not the purchase rate) applies immediately, with no grace period.

MCC 6012 — Financial Institutions: Some exchanges code under this, which may also trigger cash advance treatment at some banks.

A few exchanges use standard retail MCCs (like 5999 — Miscellaneous Retail), which some banks process as regular purchases. However, many banks have added crypto exchange names to their own blocklists, declining the transaction regardless of MCC. The landscape shifts constantly as banks update their policies.

The Real Cost of a Cash Advance on Crypto

If your bank allows the transaction but classifies it as a cash advance, here's the true cost math:

Example: $1,000 Bitcoin purchase on a TD credit card
Cash advance fee: 3% = $30 (charged immediately)
Interest rate: 22.99% annually (most major banks)
Grace period: $0 — interest starts immediately
If you pay in full at next statement (30 days): $30 + ($1,000 × 22.99% / 12) = $30 + $19.16
→ True cost: $49.16 on a $1,000 purchase (4.9%) before any exchange fee

And that's the best-case scenario where you pay immediately. If the crypto drops in value (as it often does) and you carry the balance even one additional month, the math gets worse quickly. Crypto purchases on credit are one of the rare cases where the transaction cost alone can significantly erode your investment before you've made a single trade decision.

Rewards points? You won't earn them. Cash advances do not earn rewards points, cashback, or any other card benefits on most Canadian cards. The purchase rate and grace period that would apply to a normal retail purchase — including rewards earn — do not apply to cash advances. You'll pay the cash advance rate AND receive no rewards for the transaction.

Major Exchange Payment Policies for Canadians

Newton (Canadian)
Credit Cards: NOT accepted

Newton is one of Canada's largest dedicated crypto exchanges (zero trading fees). It does not accept credit cards at all. Funding methods: Interac e-Transfer (most popular, same-day), bank wire transfer. No credit card option on the platform.

Shakepay (Canadian)
Credit Cards: NOT accepted

Shakepay focuses on Bitcoin and Ethereum for Canadians with zero exchange fees. Funding is exclusively via Interac e-Transfer. No credit card or debit card option — by design, to avoid payment processing fees that would force them to charge trading fees.

Coinbase (US, available in Canada)
Credit Cards: Accepted but expensive

Coinbase accepts Visa and Mastercard credit cards for instant purchases, but charges a 3.99% credit/debit card fee on every transaction. Combined with what your bank may classify as a cash advance (another 3% + 22.99% rate), the total cost of a Coinbase credit card purchase in Canada can reach 7–10% before any spread. Coinbase strongly recommends using bank transfer (Interac e-Transfer in Canada) to avoid card fees.

Kraken (available in Canada)
Credit Cards: NOT directly accepted for CAD

Kraken Canada does not accept credit cards for CAD deposits. Funding options are Interac e-Transfer and bank wire. Kraken's Global exchange (USD trading) accepts cards in some jurisdictions but this route involves FX conversion costs for Canadian users.

Bitbuy (Canadian, now owned by WonderFi)
Credit Cards: NOT accepted

Bitbuy accepts Interac e-Transfer and bank wire only. No credit card payment option for Canadian accounts.

Binance (Global)
Credit Cards: Technically accepted, high fees

Binance accepts Visa/Mastercard via a third-party payment processor with fees typically around 4–4.5%. Many Canadian banks decline Binance transactions anyway due to compliance screening. Binance is not registered with Canadian securities regulators for certain trading activities — use with awareness of regulatory status.

Better Ways to Fund Crypto Purchases in Canada

The best approach for Canadians buying crypto is almost always to avoid credit cards entirely and use one of these instead:

Interac e-Transfer (recommended): All major Canadian crypto exchanges — Newton, Shakepay, Bitbuy, Coinbase, Kraken — accept Interac e-Transfer for CAD deposits. Transfers are instant or same-day, completely free (exchanges absorb the cost), and processed at the bank exchange rate. This is the standard funding method for Canadian crypto buyers. No credit card fees, no cash advance classification, no interest.

Debit card: Some exchanges that accept debit cards process them differently from credit cards — as a direct bank debit rather than a quasi-cash transaction. However, debit purchases still carry exchange-side processing fees (typically 1.5–3%). Check whether your exchange charges less for debit vs. e-Transfer before choosing this route.

Bank wire transfer: For large purchases ($5,000+), bank wire transfers (EFT) offer competitive exchange rates and lower platform fees on most exchanges. Best suited for infrequent large purchases, not regular DCA (dollar-cost averaging) buys.

The KOHO Prepaid Workaround

KOHO is a Canadian prepaid Visa that works differently from a credit card — it's funded by your bank account directly and spends like a debit card on the Visa network. Some Canadians have used KOHO to load funds and then use the KOHO card at exchanges that accept Visa, avoiding the cash advance classification issue.

KOHO for crypto — key points: KOHO processes as a prepaid Visa (not a credit card), so no cash advance interest applies — you can only spend what you've loaded. Some exchanges that accept Visa cards will process a KOHO payment where a regular bank's credit card would be declined or coded as a cash advance. KOHO charges no foreign transaction fee on purchases in CAD. Note: this doesn't eliminate exchange-side card fees (Coinbase still charges 3.99% for card payments regardless of card type). The benefit is avoiding cash advance interest — not avoiding processing fees.

The bottom line on workarounds: Even if you navigate the bank-side classification issue, the exchange-side card fee (typically 3.99% on Coinbase) makes crypto credit/debit card purchases expensive. For all but the smallest convenience purchases, Interac e-Transfer remains the lowest-cost option for Canadians — zero fees on most major Canadian exchanges.

Best practice for Canadian crypto buyers: (1) Use Newton, Shakepay, or Bitbuy — zero trading fees, fund via Interac e-Transfer for free. (2) If using Coinbase or Binance, fund via bank transfer, not card. (3) If you must use a card, KOHO prepaid avoids the cash advance interest issue — but exchange fees still apply. (4) Never use a major bank credit card directly — you'll likely be blocked, or hit with a cash advance rate before earning a single satoshi.
