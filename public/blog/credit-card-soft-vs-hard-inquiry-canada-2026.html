Soft vs Hard Credit Inquiry in Canada: What's the Difference?

Soft pulls are invisible to lenders and don't affect your score. Hard pulls do — but here's exactly how much, and for how long.

Updated July 2026 · 9 min read

Every time you apply for a credit card, mortgage, car loan, or even some utility services, the lender checks your credit report. How that check is done — as a "soft" inquiry or a "hard" inquiry — determines whether your credit score takes a hit. This is one of the most misunderstood aspects of Canadian credit, and getting it wrong can cost you points at the worst possible time.

Soft vs Hard Inquiry: The Core Difference

SOFT INQUIRY

Soft Pull

  • ✓ Does NOT affect credit score
  • ✓ Only visible to you, not lenders
  • ✓ No consent required in all cases
  • ✓ Can happen multiple times with no effect
  • ✓ Still stays on your report (personal view)
HARD INQUIRY

Hard Pull

  • ✗ Reduces credit score temporarily
  • ✗ Visible to other lenders for 3 years
  • ✗ Requires your consent
  • ✗ Multiple hard pulls compound impact
  • ✗ Stays on report for 3 years in Canada

A soft inquiry is a credit check that doesn't impact your credit score and is only visible to you when you review your own credit report. A hard inquiry (also called a hard pull) is a full credit check that temporarily lowers your score and remains visible to all lenders who review your credit file for the next three years.

The key rule: You must give explicit consent for a hard inquiry. Before any credit application, the fine print will include authorization for the lender to pull your credit. By signing or clicking "agree," you're consenting to the hard pull. Soft inquiries don't require this consent.

What Triggers Each Type in Canada

Common Soft Inquiry Triggers

  • Checking your own credit score — via Equifax, TransUnion, Credit Karma, or Borrowell. Always a soft pull.
  • Credit monitoring services — daily or monthly monitoring updates.
  • Pre-approved credit offers — when banks send you pre-approval letters, they used a soft pull to screen you.
  • Employer background checks — some employers check credit (with your consent) as a soft inquiry.
  • Landlord rental applications — most landlords do soft pulls (though some do hard pulls — ask before applying).
  • Utility or cellphone pre-screening — initial screening before a formal service agreement.
  • Credit limit increase review (some banks) — TD, RBC, and Scotiabank often do soft inquiries for modest limit increase requests.

Common Hard Inquiry Triggers

  • Credit card applications — virtually always a hard pull at all major Canadian banks.
  • Mortgage applications — hard pull at every lender you apply to.
  • Auto loan applications — hard pull when you finance at a dealership or bank.
  • Personal loan applications — including online lenders like Fairstone, Borrowell Loans, etc.
  • Line of credit applications — home equity, personal LOC.
  • Credit limit increase requests (some banks) — CIBC and BMO typically hard pull for credit limit increases. Always ask first.
Always ask before a credit limit increase request: The type of inquiry varies by bank. TD, RBC, and Scotiabank often use soft pulls for modest credit limit increases — but CIBC and BMO may use hard pulls. Asking "will this require a hard inquiry?" before submitting the request could save you unnecessary score damage.

How Much Hard Inquiries Hurt Your Score

In Canada, credit scores are calculated by Equifax and TransUnion using their own proprietary models. The FICO Score model (widely used by lenders) allocates approximately 10% of your score to "new credit," which includes hard inquiries.

Typical Hard Inquiry Impact

Single hard inquiry -3 to -10 points
2–3 hard inquiries within 6 months -15 to -30 points
5+ inquiries within 6 months -30 to -50+ points (serious risk signal)
Time until impact fades significantly 6–12 months
Time until inquiry removed from report 3 years (Canada)

Note: the exact point drop varies significantly by your current score. Someone with a 780 score who applies for one card might drop only 3–5 points. Someone with a 650 score with multiple recent inquiries might drop 10+ points per application. Lenders also weigh inquiries differently — an inquiry from 2.5 years ago matters far less than one from last week.

Rate shopping exception: When shopping for a mortgage or auto loan, multiple hard inquiries within a 14–45 day window are typically treated as a single inquiry by scoring models. This is designed to let consumers comparison shop for major loans without being penalized. This exception does NOT apply to credit card shopping — each card application is a separate inquiry event.

How Long Inquiries Stay on Your Report

Day 1

Hard inquiry appears

Immediately visible to you and to any lender who pulls your credit report.

3M

Impact begins to fade

The score impact typically starts declining after 3–6 months as lenders see you're not continuing to accumulate new credit.

6M

Score largely recovered

Most scoring models have nearly fully reversed the score impact of a single inquiry within 6–12 months, assuming no other negative factors.

3Y

Inquiry removed from report

In Canada, hard inquiries are removed from Equifax and TransUnion reports after 3 years. In the US it's 2 years — Canadian reports hold them longer.

Two bureaus in Canada: Equifax and TransUnion both operate in Canada. Some lenders pull from one, some pull from both. If TD pulls only Equifax and BMO pulls only TransUnion, applying to both in the same week means each bureau only sees one inquiry — though both affect your overall credit profile.

When to Apply and How to Minimize Impact

Rule 1: Space Applications 6+ Months Apart

The most impactful strategy: wait at least 6 months between credit card applications. This gives each hard inquiry's score impact time to substantially fade before you invite the next one. For mortgage-seekers especially, don't apply for any new credit in the 6–12 months before a mortgage application.

Rule 2: Apply Before Major Credit Milestones

If you know you'll need to apply for a mortgage in 18 months, apply for the credit card you want now — not in 6 months. By application time, the inquiry impact will have largely faded. The timing of when you apply matters as much as whether you apply.

Rule 3: Never Apply for Multiple Cards in One Day

Unlike rate shopping for mortgages, multiple credit card applications on the same day are treated as separate inquiries. Applying for 3 cards in one afternoon generates 3 hard pulls that each independently reduce your score.

Rule 4: Ask If It's Hard or Soft Before You Apply

For credit limit increases, always ask your bank whether the request triggers a hard or soft inquiry before submitting. Most banks will tell you honestly. If they say it's hard and you're planning a major loan application soon, wait until after.

Mortgage-specific guidance: If you're shopping for a mortgage, have all lenders pull your credit within the same 14-day window. Most scoring models recognize rate shopping and count these multiple mortgage inquiries as a single event. Apply to all mortgage lenders you're considering within that window — not spread over weeks. This does NOT apply to credit card applications.

Common Myths About Credit Inquiries — Debunked

Myth: "Checking your own credit hurts your score"

False. Checking your own credit through Equifax, TransUnion, Credit Karma, or Borrowell is always a soft inquiry and never affects your score. You should check your credit report regularly — the Government of Canada recommends reviewing it at least once per year for accuracy.

Myth: "Pre-approval checks are hard pulls"

False (usually). When a bank sends you a pre-approval offer, they used a soft pull to pre-screen. When you actually apply and accept the pre-approved offer, THAT triggers a hard pull. Don't confuse "you're pre-approved" with "your credit hasn't been pulled yet."

Myth: "Hard inquiries stay on your report forever"

False. In Canada, hard inquiries are removed from your Equifax and TransUnion report after 3 years (in the US, it's 2 years). The score impact fades much sooner — within 6–12 months for most consumers.

Myth: "If you're denied, the inquiry doesn't count"

False. The hard inquiry counts regardless of whether your application is approved or denied. The inquiry reflects the check, not the outcome. If you're denied, the inquiry still reduces your score and stays on your report for 3 years.

Myth: "Cancelling a card removes the hard inquiry"

False. Cancelling a credit card does not remove the hard inquiry from when you originally applied. The inquiry record is separate from the account record and persists independently.
