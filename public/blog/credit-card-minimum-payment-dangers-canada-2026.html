Credit Card Minimum Payment Dangers Canada 2026

$3,000 in credit card debt at 19.99% APR, making only minimum payments: you'll still be paying in 2056 — and hand the bank $7,500 in interest. Here's the math and how to escape.

The True Cost of Minimum Payments — Real Math

Why minimums are designed this way: Minimum payments are calculated to keep you in debt as long as possible. A typical minimum is 2–3% of your outstanding balance. As your balance falls, so does the minimum payment — which means your payoff accelerates only slightly over time. Banks earn massive interest while you pay down almost no principal in the early years.

FCAC Rules — What Your Statement Must Show

Since 2010, federal regulations (enforced by FCAC) require Canadian credit card issuers to disclose on every monthly statement:

1. The number of months it will take to pay off the full balance if you make only the minimum payment.
2. The total interest you will pay if you make only the minimum payment.
3. The monthly payment required to pay off the balance in 36 months (3 years).

This box appears on your statement near the payment due date. If you've been ignoring it, check your next statement. The number of months should be alarming enough to motivate action.

Debt Avalanche vs. Debt Snowball — Which Strategy Works?

Debt Avalanche (mathematically optimal): Pay minimums on all debts. Put all extra money toward the debt with the highest interest rate. When it's gone, redirect to the next highest. This minimizes total interest paid.

Debt Snowball (psychologically effective): Pay minimums on all debts. Put all extra money toward the debt with the smallest balance. When it's gone, redirect to the next smallest. This creates faster early wins that maintain motivation.

If you have a cash advance balance at 22.99–24.99% alongside a purchase balance at 19.99%, use the avalanche — the rate difference is significant enough to matter.

5 Strategies to Escape the Minimum Payment Trap

  1. Set a Fixed Monthly Payment — Ignore the Minimum

    Calculate what you need to pay to be debt-free in 24–36 months and set that as a fixed automatic payment. On a $3,000 balance at 19.99%, paying $150/month (vs. the ~$60 minimum) clears the debt in about 25 months and costs ~$680 in interest instead of $7,600. The difference is dramatic.

  2. Consider a Balance Transfer Card

    Several Canadian cards offer 0% promotional rates on transferred balances for 6–12 months. MBNA True Line and Scotiabank Value Visa periodically run promotions. A 0% transfer on $3,000 means 100% of your payments go toward principal for the promotional period. Use Rewardly's balance transfer guide to compare current offers.

  3. Call and Ask for a Rate Reduction

    This works more often than people expect. If you have a history of on-time payments and have been a customer for 1+ years, call your card's retention line and ask to reduce your interest rate. Banks have discretion to reduce rates — sometimes temporarily to 13–15% to retain a good customer. Worst case: they say no. Best case: you save hundreds in interest.

  4. Use a Low-Rate Card for the Carrying Balance

    If you know you'll carry a balance, switch to a low-interest card: MBNA True Line Mastercard (12.99% APR, $0 annual fee) or BMO Preferred Rate Mastercard (13.99% APR, $20 annual fee). On a $3,000 balance, the difference between 19.99% and 12.99% saves ~$210/year in interest charges alone.

  5. Stop Charging New Purchases Until Paid Off

    This is obvious but critical: you can't drain a bathtub if the tap is still running. Freeze the card (literally put it in a bag of water in the freezer if needed), switch daily spending to debit or a KOHO prepaid, and focus 100% of payment capacity on the existing balance. Even a $50–$100 increase in monthly payment vs. minimum dramatically shortens payoff time.

The Right Cards If You Carry a Balance

If you carry a balance month-to-month, rewards cards are the wrong choice — the interest you pay (19.99%+) will far exceed any rewards you earn (typically 1–5% cashback). The right strategy is a low-rate card while you pay down debt, then switch to a rewards card once you're in the habit of paying in full.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum payment on a Canadian credit card?
Minimum payments vary by issuer, but typically it's the greater of: $10 (or the statement balance if under $10), OR 2–3% of the outstanding balance, OR the interest charges plus $10. So on a $2,000 balance at a bank that uses 3% minimum: the minimum is roughly $60. As the balance drops, the minimum drops too — which is why minimum-only repayment takes so long.
Does paying more than the minimum hurt your credit score?
No — paying more than the minimum only helps your credit score. Your credit score benefits from lower credit utilization (balance ÷ credit limit). If your $3,000 balance sits on a $5,000 limit card, your utilization is 60% — significantly negative. Paying the balance down to $1,000 drops utilization to 20%, which is considered healthy and can boost your score by 20–50 points over several months.
What if I can't even make the minimum payment?
Contact your bank immediately before missing a payment. Most Canadian banks have hardship programs that can temporarily reduce your minimum payment or interest rate. Missing a payment triggers a missed payment fee ($25–$48), potentially raises your interest rate to a penalty rate (29.99%+), and damages your credit score. A proactive call to your bank almost always leads to better outcomes than simply missing the payment and hoping it resolves itself.
