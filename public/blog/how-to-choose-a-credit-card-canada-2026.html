How to Choose a Credit Card in Canada 2026

A clear, step-by-step guide to picking the right card for your situation — whether it's your first card or your fifth.

📖 14 min read

Choosing a credit card in Canada is genuinely confusing. There are over 400 personal credit cards from a dozen major issuers, each with its own rewards program, fee structure, and benefit package. Ratehub lists hundreds. NerdWallet offers rankings that differ from Greedyrates, which differ from the bank's own claims.

Here's the thing: the "best" credit card does not exist. The best card for you depends on what you spend on, whether you carry a balance, how much you travel, and which perks you'll actually use. This guide walks you through how to find that card systematically — in about 15 minutes.

Step 1: Answer the One Question That Changes Everything

Do you pay your balance in full every month?

If YES: The interest rate on the card is irrelevant to you. Focus entirely on rewards, perks, and fees. The rewards will outweigh any other consideration.

If NO (or sometimes): The interest you pay may dwarf any rewards you earn. A card earning 2% cash back on $500/mo in purchases earns $120/yr — but carrying even $1,000 at 19.99% for a year costs $200 in interest. If you carry a balance, a low-interest card like the MBNA True Line (8.99%) almost always makes more financial sense than a rewards card. Fix the interest cost first, then optimize for rewards later.

If you sometimes carry a balance but also want rewards: get a low-rate card for balance management and use a separate rewards card for purchases you will pay off each month. Two cards, two jobs.

Step 3: Choose Your Rewards Type

Canadian credit cards offer three main reward structures. Each has tradeoffs:

Cash Back

Straightforward: you earn a percentage of your spending back as cash, applied to your statement. No points charts to learn, no loyalty programs to manage. Cash back rates range from 0.5% (base) to 5% (category bonus). Best for people who want simplicity and flexibility — cash is usable anywhere.

Best cash back cards: CIBC Dividend VI (4% grocery+gas), Scotia Momentum VI+ (4% grocery+bills, 2% gas), BMO Cash Back WE MC (5% grocery, 3% gas), Simplii Financial (4% dining, no fee).

Points Programs

You earn points in a program like Scene+, PC Optimum, or RBC Avion Rewards. Points have fixed or near-fixed values and are redeemable at specific partner stores or travel portals. Less flexible than cash back but can offer high value if you shop at partner stores (Sobeys for Scene+, Shoppers for PC Optimum) or use the travel portal.

Best points cards: Scotia Gold Amex (Scene+, 5× dining and grocery), RBC ION+ (Avion, 3× on 6 categories).

Travel Miles / Flexible Points

Cards earning Aeroplan, Amex Membership Rewards, or similar flexible currencies that transfer to airline and hotel programs. Can yield extremely high value (2¢+ per point) when redeemed strategically for premium cabin flights or high-value hotel nights. Requires more knowledge and planning to maximize. Not ideal for beginners.

Best travel cards: Amex Cobalt (MR points, 5× dining/grocery), TD Aeroplan VI (Aeroplan, checked bag benefit), CIBC Aeroplan VI (Aeroplan, companion pass option).

💡 Rule of thumb: If you're choosing your first rewards card, start with cash back. It's transparent and you can't make a mistake redeeming it. Move to points programs once you understand the ecosystem and are ready to optimize.

Step 4: Assess the Annual Fee

Annual fees range from $0 to $700+. Higher fees generally come with higher earn rates and better perks. The question isn't whether a fee is low or high — it's whether the rewards and perks exceed the fee for your specific spending.

The $120–$150 tier is where most Canadians find the best value. Cards like the Scotia Gold Amex ($120), CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite ($120), and National Bank World Elite ($150) offer comprehensive travel insurance, strong earn rates, and useful perks that justify their fees for average Canadian households.

Step 6: Decide If Insurance Benefits Matter to You

Travel insurance is a major differentiator between cards and can be worth hundreds of dollars per year if you travel. Key questions:

  • Do you take at least one international trip per year? If yes, emergency medical insurance becomes very important. Compare how many days coverage applies (10 days vs 21 days vs 60 days) and age cutoffs (especially important if you're over 65).
  • Do you rent cars? Many cards include collision damage waiver (CDW) on rental cars — worth the rental company's $20–$35/day coverage fee.
  • Do you buy large-ticket items? Extended warranty (doubles manufacturer warranty) and purchase protection (covers damage/theft in first 90 days) matter for electronics and appliances.

If you never travel internationally, insurance add-ons are meaningless to you and should not factor into your decision. If you travel frequently, a card with 21-day emergency medical coverage (like TD First Class Travel VI) may be worth more than its earn rate alone suggests.

Step 7: Match Your Profile to a Card

Based on your answers above, here are the most common Canadian spending profiles and their recommended cards:

🛒 Family — high grocery and gas spend, pays balance in full

Best fit: Scotia Momentum Visa Infinite+ (4% grocery/bills, 2% gas, $120/yr) or CIBC Dividend VI (4% grocery+gas, $120/yr) or BMO Cash Back WE MC (5% grocery, 3% gas, $139/yr). At $800/mo grocery + $150/mo gas, you earn $420–$500/yr — well above the fee.

✈️ Frequent traveler — earns points, redeems for flights

Best fit: Amex Cobalt ($155/yr, 5× dining/grocery → Aeroplan via Membership Rewards transfers) or TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite ($139/yr, free checked bag saves $30 per round trip). Pair with Scotiabank Passport Visa Infinite ($150) for no FX fee and lounge access on international trips.

🍕 Urban renter — high dining/delivery, no car, transit user

Best fit: Simplii Financial Cash Back Visa ($0, 4% dining) for restaurants, paired with RBC ION+ Visa ($48, 3× transit/streaming) for everyday urban spending. Zero or minimal annual fees.

💻 Remote worker — mostly online shopping, some US purchases

Best fit: Rogers Red WE MC ($0, 1.5% everywhere + 3% on USD spend) for general and US-merchant purchases. No FX fee effective return means you keep the full 1.5%+ on any USD transaction.

🎓 Student / new to credit

Best fit: Start with Scotiabank Scene+ Visa (no fee, easy approval, earns Scene+ points) or BMO SPC Mastercard. Build 12+ months of on-time payment history, then upgrade to a premium card once you have a stronger credit profile.

💳 Carries a balance sometimes

Best fit: MBNA True Line Mastercard ($0, 8.99% interest — half of standard 19.99%). Save hundreds in interest, then consider a rewards card once the balance is cleared.

Step 8: Only Apply for One Card at a Time

Each credit card application triggers a hard inquiry on your credit report, which temporarily (3–6 months) reduces your credit score by a small amount. Applying for multiple cards within 30 days is a red flag to lenders and stacks multiple hard inquiries. Apply for one card at a time and wait until you're approved before considering another.

📌 First card tip: If this is your first credit card and you don't know your credit score, check it for free at creditkarma.ca or through your bank's app (TD, RBC, Scotiabank, CIBC all offer free credit monitoring to customers). If your score is below 650, start with a secured credit card or a student card to build history before applying for premium products.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many credit cards should I have?

Most optimizers recommend 2–3 cards for most Canadians: one primary card for your biggest spend category, one supplemental card for a secondary category (or for foreign spending), and optionally a low-rate card for any balance you carry. More than 3 cards requires active management that most people don't want to do.

Will applying for a credit card hurt my credit score?

Yes, briefly. A hard inquiry from a card application typically reduces your score by 5–10 points temporarily. If approved, responsible use (on-time payments, low utilization) builds your score back quickly. The initial dip is minor for people with established credit history — only matters more if you are actively trying to get a mortgage or large loan in the next 3–6 months.

Is it better to have one great card or two good cards?

Two well-chosen cards almost always outperform one card in pure dollar returns, because no single card offers the best rate on every category. A 4% grocery card plus a 1.5% flat-rate card will capture more value than any single card. The tradeoff is management complexity — two cards means two statements, two payment dates, two apps. For most people, the extra $100–$200/yr in rewards from an optimized two-card wallet is worth it.

What should I look for in my first credit card?

Approval likelihood (check income/credit requirements), no or low annual fee (reduces risk), simple rewards (cash back or a fixed-value points program), and an issuer you already bank with (easier to manage). After 12 months of on-time payments, you can upgrade to a premium card with a stronger rewards profile.

Can I cancel a card without hurting my credit score?

Cancelling a card can affect your credit score if it reduces your total available credit or shortens your average account age. Keep older cards open if possible, even at low usage. If the annual fee is the issue, ask the issuer to downgrade you to a no-fee version of the same card — this preserves the account age without the cost.
