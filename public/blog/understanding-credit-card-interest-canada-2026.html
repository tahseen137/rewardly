Understanding Credit Card Interest in Canada 2026

Most Canadian credit cards charge 19.99% APR. But what does that actually mean for your balance — and how do you avoid paying a dollar of it?

What Is APR (Annual Percentage Rate)?

APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate. It's the yearly interest rate charged on any balance you carry on your credit card from one month to the next. In Canada, the standard credit card APR is 19.99% for purchases — though premium travel rewards cards sometimes charge 20.99%, 22.99%, or higher. Low-rate cards charge as little as 8.99%–12.99%.

The APR is expressed annually, but credit card interest is actually calculated daily using the daily periodic rate (DPR).

Daily Periodic Rate Calculation

Daily Periodic Rate = Annual APR ÷ 365

19.99% ÷ 365 = 0.054767% per day

On a $1,000 balance: 0.054767% × $1,000 = $0.548 of interest per day

$0.548/day × 30 days = $16.44 in interest on a $1,000 balance over one month

The Grace Period — Your Free Money Window

The grace period is the window between when a charge appears on your statement and when interest starts accruing. In Canada, credit card issuers are legally required to give cardholders at least 21 days after the statement closing date before interest kicks in on new purchases.

How the grace period works: If you pay your full statement balance by the due date every single month, you NEVER pay interest. Every purchase made between your statement closing date and your payment due date gets a full grace period — you essentially get an interest-free loan for 21–55 days (depending on when in the billing cycle you made the purchase).

Grace period loss: The grace period DISAPPEARS if you carry even $1 of balance from the previous month. Once you carry a balance, interest starts accruing immediately on ALL new purchases — from the day you make them, with no grace period. This is the single most important thing to understand about credit card interest in Canada.

How Compound Interest Works on Credit Cards

Credit card interest compounds. This means interest is added to your balance, and then next month you pay interest on the interest.

$3,000 Balance at 19.99% — Monthly Interest Example

Month 1: Balance $3,000 → Interest: $3,000 × (19.99%/12) = $49.98 → New balance: $3,049.98

Month 2: Balance $3,049.98 → Interest: $3,049.98 × 1.666% = $50.81 → New balance: $3,100.79

Minimum payment of 2% = ~$60. Of that: $49.98 is interest, only $10.02 reduces principal. At minimum payments only: takes 30+ years and $7,000+ in interest to pay off $3,000.

Why minimum payments are dangerous: On a $3,000 balance, a minimum payment of ~$60/month barely covers the interest. To actually reduce the balance meaningfully, you need to pay well above minimum — ideally 3–5× minimum or a fixed amount that pays the card off within 12–18 months.

Never use your credit card for a cash advance unless it's an emergency. Cash advances in Canada charge 22.99%–24.99% APR with NO grace period — interest starts accumulating the minute the cash hits your hand. A $500 cash advance costs $8–$10 in interest per month immediately, plus often a cash advance fee of 1–3% charged upfront.

How to Pay Zero Interest on Your Credit Card

The rule is simple: pay your full statement balance by the due date, every month. Not the minimum — the full statement balance. When you do this consistently, the grace period means you never pay a single dollar of interest. You're using the bank's money for free for 21–55 days on every purchase.

This is also why credit card rewards are so valuable — if you pay in full monthly, you're earning 1%–5% back on spending that costs you nothing in interest. The rewards are essentially free money.

Set up autopay: The simplest fraud protection against missed payments — and accidental interest charges — is to set up autopay for your full statement balance. This ensures you never miss a payment, never pay interest, and never lose your grace period.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average credit card interest rate in Canada?
The standard credit card APR in Canada is 19.99% for purchases, which has been the dominant rate since the 1980s. Some premium travel rewards cards charge 20.99%–22.99%. Low-rate cards charge 8.99%–12.99%. The Bank of Canada's overnight rate affects variable-rate products but most Canadian credit cards have a fixed rate — 19.99% doesn't change when the Bank of Canada raises or lowers rates.
If I pay the minimum payment, will I get charged interest?
Yes. Paying only the minimum payment means you're carrying a balance. When you carry any balance, interest accrues on that balance at 19.99% (or whatever your card's rate is). The minimum payment prevents late fees and keeps your account in good standing, but it does NOT prevent interest charges. Only paying your full statement balance by the due date eliminates interest charges. Minimum payments are specifically designed to keep you in debt as long as possible — always pay more than the minimum.
Why does my interest charge seem higher than 19.99% would suggest?
Several reasons. First, interest may have accrued mid-cycle and then compounded. Second, if you lost your grace period (by carrying a balance last month), new purchases also accrue interest from day one. Third, some cards calculate interest on the average daily balance rather than the closing balance — meaning even a balance that you paid down during the month generated interest every day it was outstanding. Check your statement's interest calculation section for details specific to your card.
