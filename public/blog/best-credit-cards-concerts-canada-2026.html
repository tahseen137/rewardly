How Concert Tickets Are Coded

Ticketmaster and StubHub purchases in Canada typically fall under MCC 7929 (Bands, Orchestras, Actors, and Other Entertainers), MCC 7922 (Theatrical Producers, Ticket Agencies), or occasionally MCC 7999 (Recreation Services, Not Elsewhere Classified). A few cards have explicit "entertainment" categories that capture these — most don't.

In-venue purchases (drinks, merch, concessions) often code separately as food/beverage (MCC 5812 dining) or retail (5999). This means the best card for buying your ticket might differ from the best card for drinks at the bar — though for simplicity, most people use one card all night.

The real reason Amex wins for concerts: It's not the reward rate — it's Amex Front of the Line presale access. On major tours, Amex cardholders often have a 48–72 hour window before tickets go on general sale. For sold-out shows, that access is worth far more than any percentage of rewards.

Amex Front of the Line: Is It Worth It?

Amex Front of the Line (FoTL) gives American Express cardholders early access to presale tickets for major concerts, sporting events, theatre, and more in Canada. The presale window is typically 48–72 hours before general sale on Ticketmaster and participating venues.

For popular artists — Taylor Swift, The Weeknd, Drake, Billie Eilish, Coldplay — general sale tickets sell out within minutes. FoTL presale access essentially guarantees you can buy tickets at face value rather than paying 2–5x on the secondary market. On a pair of $150 tickets, avoiding a $300 markup on StubHub is worth far more than any reward percentage.

How to use Amex Front of the Line: Register at americanexpress.ca/frontoftheline or the Amex Canada app. You'll need to use your Amex card to purchase during the presale window. Most presales require the full Amex card number at checkout — prepaid Amex gift cards typically don't qualify.

Every personal American Express card issued in Canada qualifies for FoTL — including the no-annual-fee SimplyCash Card. So even if you're not spending heavily on Amex, the free SimplyCash gives you presale access. That said, you have to actually use your Amex to buy the tickets during the presale window.

Smart Strategies for Concert Season

Use Amex for Presale, Visa/MC as Backup

The optimal setup: hold at least one Amex card for presale access (the no-fee SimplyCash works). Use your highest-earning Visa or Mastercard as a backup if Amex isn't accepted or the presale is sold out by the time you log in. Many dedicated concert-goers carry the Amex Cobalt plus Rogers Red for this reason.

Put Ticket Purchases Toward Welcome Bonus Thresholds

Concert tickets are large, infrequent purchases — perfect for hitting welcome bonus spend thresholds. A $500 welcome bonus with a $3,000 spend requirement in 3 months is achievable if you're buying tickets for major shows. Time your card applications before big purchases like festival passes or expensive seats.

Buy Gift Cards in Advance to Boost Your Rate

Some grocery stores sell Ticketmaster gift cards. If your grocery card earns 4% (like Scotia Momentum) and Ticketmaster doesn't code as a bonus category on your entertainment card, buying a Ticketmaster gift card at the grocery store nets you 4% back on the ticket purchase. Availability varies by store and region.

Festival strategy: Multi-day festival passes (Osheaga, NXNE, Boots and Hearts) are single large purchases — use them to hit a welcome bonus. Weekend passes often run $400–$800+ CAD, making them ideal for meeting minimum spend requirements on a new premium card.

Watch for USD Pricing on Resale Platforms

StubHub Canada prices many tickets in USD. A 2.5% foreign transaction fee from a standard Canadian card wipes out most reward earnings on a $150 USD ticket. Use a no-FX card (Rogers Red, Scotia Passport Visa Infinite, Scotia Gold Amex, Home Trust Preferred Visa) for resale purchases.

Amex Not Accepted? This Happens More Than You Think

Some smaller venues, third-party box offices, or festivals only accept Visa/Mastercard. If you planned on using your Amex Cobalt for 5x on drinks, carry a backup. The Rogers Red Mastercard at 2% no-FX is an excellent backstop for these situations.

Bottom Line

For most Canadian concert-goers, the Scotia Gold American Express is the strongest single card: 3x on ticket purchases, 5x on venue food/drinks, no FX fees, and Amex Front of the Line presale access — all for $120/yr.

If you're already an Amex Cobalt holder, your setup is already excellent for concerts: 5x on in-venue food/drinks plus Front of the Line presale access covers both priorities. Add the Rogers Red as a Mastercard backup for venues that don't take Amex.

For those who don't want any annual fee and primarily care about presale access, the Amex SimplyCash Card gives you Front of the Line at $0/yr — a legitimate hack for casual concert-goers who just need early ticket access without complex rewards optimization.
