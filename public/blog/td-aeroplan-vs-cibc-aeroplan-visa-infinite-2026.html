TD Aeroplan vs CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite 2026

Same earn rates, same annual fee, same Aeroplan currency. But critical differences in travel insurance, perks, and bank benefits mean one is likely better for your situation. Here's the verdict.

Quick Overview: More Alike Than Different

Both the TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite and CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite are co-issued by Air Canada Aeroplan as the "standard tier" Aeroplan credit cards. They share the same earn structure, the same annual fee, and the same Aeroplan points currency. The differences are in the fine print — but those fine print differences can matter a lot depending on your lifestyle.

Where TD Wins

Trip Interruption: TD Aeroplan covers $5,000/person for trip interruption; CIBC covers only $2,500/person. This difference is most relevant when trips are interrupted mid-journey — evacuation, medical emergency, missed connections. On a family trip, this could be a $5,000–$10,000 difference in coverage.

Emergency Medical Window: TD covers you for 21 days per trip; CIBC covers 15 days. For longer international trips, those extra 6 days matter. If you take 3-week international trips, CIBC's coverage lapses on day 16 while TD's continues through day 21.

Nexus Fee Rebate ($50): TD Aeroplan cardholders receive an annual $50 statement credit toward their NEXUS application or renewal fee ($50 USD = ~$68 CAD). NEXUS speeds up Canada-U.S. border crossing and can also be used for TSA PreCheck at U.S. airports. Effectively reduces TD's net fee from $139 to ~$89 for frequent U.S. travelers.

Air Canada Priority Check-In: TD cardholders receive priority check-in at Air Canada kiosks — shorter queues, dedicated counters at most airports. CIBC cardholders use standard check-in. For frequent Air Canada flyers, this is a real time-saver.

Preferred Boarding/Seating: TD cardholders are eligible for preferred seating selection and earlier boarding on Air Canada flights. CIBC offers neither.

Where CIBC Wins

Mobile Device Insurance ($1,000): CIBC Aeroplan VI includes mobile device insurance covering theft, loss, and damage up to $1,000 for phones purchased on the card. TD Aeroplan VI has no mobile device insurance. For a $1,200 iPhone purchased on the card, this is meaningful coverage.

CIBC Pace It: CIBC's instalment plan feature lets you convert large purchases into fixed monthly payments at a lower interest rate. This isn't a rewards feature, but it's useful for managing cash flow on large travel bookings or purchases. TD doesn't offer an equivalent.

CIBC Banking Integration: CIBC Aeroplan cardholders who also bank at CIBC get streamlined benefits — Aventura points on CIBC accounts, preferred pricing on CIBC banking packages, and integrated digital banking. If you're already a CIBC customer, the integration is smoother.

Which Card Is Better? The Verdict

Choose TD Aeroplan VI if:

You cross the U.S. border regularly (NEXUS saves ~$68/yr)

You fly Air Canada frequently and want priority check-in + seating

You take trips longer than 15 days (21-day medical window)

You're a TD banking customer

Travel insurance limits matter — especially the $5K trip interruption

Choose CIBC Aeroplan VI if:

You carry your phone on a plan and want mobile device coverage

You're an existing CIBC banking customer

You value instalment payment flexibility (CIBC Pace It)

Your trips are 15 days or shorter (medical window is sufficient)

You'd never use NEXUS or priority boarding

Overall winner: TD Aeroplan Visa Infinite. For most Aeroplan collectors, TD's advantages in travel insurance (21-day medical, $5K trip interruption), Air Canada perks (priority check-in, preferred seating), and the NEXUS rebate collectively outweigh CIBC's mobile device insurance. The NEXUS rebate alone reduces TD's effective annual fee to ~$89 for frequent U.S. crossers.

The One Scenario Where Both Lose

Neither card has 0% FX. Both the TD and CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite cards charge the standard 2.5% foreign transaction fee. If you spend significant amounts in foreign currencies abroad, you're losing 2.5% on every transaction vs. the Scotiabank Gold Amex or Scotiabank Passport VI. Many seasoned Aeroplan collectors use their Aeroplan card for Air Canada purchases and Canadian spending, but switch to a 0% FX card when traveling internationally.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I hold both the TD and CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite simultaneously?
Yes — you can hold both cards and Aeroplan points from both would flow to the same Aeroplan account number. However, there's rarely a reason to hold both — the earn rates are identical. The welcome bonus strategy (collecting both bonuses in different years) is one scenario, but normal card-splitting doesn't apply here.
Do both cards give a free first checked bag on Air Canada?
Yes — both TD and CIBC Aeroplan Visa Infinite cards include one free checked bag for the primary cardholder and up to 8 companions on the same Aeroplan booking. This benefit saves $35–$60 per bag per direction. For a family of 4 on a round trip, that's up to $480 in bag fee savings.
How do Aeroplan points value compare across these cards?
Both cards earn identical Aeroplan points deposited into your Aeroplan account. The point value is the same regardless of which card you used to earn them. At 1.5 pts/$ on grocery/gas/drug purchases and redemption at ~1.5¢/pt for economy North American flights, the effective return is ~2.25% on those categories.
Is there a World Elite version of these cards?
Yes — both TD and CIBC offer the Aeroplan Visa Infinite Privilege card at $599/year with significantly more perks: lounge access, higher earn rates, travel credits, and superior insurance. The Privilege is for high spenders who will extract value from premium perks — the Visa Infinite (reviewed here) is the mid-tier option.
