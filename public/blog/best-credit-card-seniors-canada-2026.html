Best Credit Cards for Seniors in Canada 2026

Retirement changes your spending. Travel insurance age limits, grocery cash back, prescription spending, and no-annual-fee simplicity — the cards that work best for Canadian retirees.

Rewardly Research · Updated July 2026

The best credit card for a retiree isn't the same as the best card for a 35-year-old. Seniors often have different spending priorities — more grocery spending, prescription costs, and leisure travel — and critically, many premium cards impose age-based restrictions on travel insurance that younger cardholders rarely encounter.

This guide highlights the best cards for seniors in Canada, with special attention to which insurance policies work at what age, and which cards deliver ongoing value without requiring high spending to justify their fees.

⚠️ The Travel Insurance Age Problem — Critical for Seniors

Most Canadian credit card travel insurance policies have age-based caps. Common patterns:

  • Under 65: Full coverage typically applies (15–60 day trips)
  • Age 65–74: Reduced trip duration (often 4–15 days) and sometimes reduced limits
  • Age 75+: Many cards provide zero travel medical coverage

This is critical. Using a credit card with an age cutoff for travel insurance is financially dangerous — a medical emergency in the U.S. or Europe without insurance can cost $100,000+. Always read the insurance certificate carefully and purchase a standalone travel medical policy when card insurance doesn't apply.

🥇 Top Pick — Cash Back

Scotiabank Momentum Visa Infinite — $120/yr

4% cash back on groceries, 4% on recurring bills (prescriptions, utilities, streaming), 2% on gas and transit, 1% on everything else. For retirees with predictable spending heavily weighted toward groceries and bills, this consistently earns the most cash back.

Why it wins for seniors: Grocery and pharmacy spending is typically the largest discretionary category for retirees. 4% on both groceries and recurring bills (which often include pharmacy auto-billing) is the highest cash-back rate available. Cash back is simple — no points, no redemption portals.

Insurance age note: Emergency medical covers trips up to 25 days for under 65; reduced to 3 days for cardholders 65+. Seniors traveling longer should purchase supplemental insurance.

Best Low-Interest Option

For seniors who occasionally carry a balance, the standard 19.99% credit card interest rate is punishing. Low-interest credit cards cap the purchase rate at 8.9–12.99%, saving hundreds per year if any balance is carried.

🥇 Low Rate

MBNA True Line Mastercard — $0/yr

8.99% purchase rate (variable), $0 annual fee. No rewards, but the dramatically lower interest rate is the point. For seniors who occasionally carry balances — perhaps during travel, home renovation, or irregular expense months — saving 10%+ on interest far outweighs any rewards on a balance-carrying card.

Rule of thumb: If you carry a balance even 2–3 months per year, a low-rate card with no rewards often saves more money than a rewards card with 19.99% interest. At $2,000 balance for 3 months: 19.99% = ~$100 interest; 8.99% = ~$45 interest. $55 saved — more than most cards earn on typical spending in that period.

Tips for Seniors Choosing a Credit Card

  • Check insurance age cutoffs before traveling. Your card may provide zero coverage if you're over 65 or 75. This is easy to miss in the fine print.
  • Prefer cash back over points if you travel infrequently. Points require active management; cash back deposits automatically.
  • Consider simplifying to 1–2 cards. A rewards card for spending + a low-rate card if you occasionally carry balances covers most scenarios.
  • Include a no-FX-fee card if you winter abroad. 2.5% FX fees on 3–6 months of U.S. spending adds up to hundreds of dollars per year.
  • Shoppers Drug Mart and pharmacy spending matters. PC Financial and cards that categorize drugstores as "pharmacy" earn significantly on prescription and health spending.
  • Authorized user cards for spouses. Many premium cards include additional cards at no or low cost. Both spouses can earn on the same account.
