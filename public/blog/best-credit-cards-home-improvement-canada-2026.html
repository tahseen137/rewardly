How Home Improvement Stores Code

Most home improvement and hardware stores in Canada code under one of three MCCs:

  • MCC 5200 (Home Supply Warehouse Stores) — Home Depot, Rona, Lowe's. This is the primary MCC for large format home improvement retailers.
  • MCC 5251 (Hardware Stores) — Local hardware stores, Ace Hardware, smaller chains.
  • MCC 5712 (Furniture, Home Furnishings) — Canadian Tire (occasionally), IKEA, home décor retailers.
  • MCC 5999 (Miscellaneous Retail) — Fallback MCC for mixed-use stores.

No standard Canadian credit card explicitly bonuses MCC 5200, 5251, or home improvement categories. This means the best earn rates come from cards with broad everyday spending multipliers or general retail bonus categories.

Canadian Tire is the exception: Canadian Tire's own Canadian Tire Money rewards program is separate from credit card rewards and stacks with your credit card. The Canadian Tire Mastercard earns additional Canadian Tire Money on Canadian Tire purchases — but at a lower base rate than some general rewards cards. The question is whether the Canadian Tire Money + a weaker card outperforms a stronger general card without Canadian Tire Money.

Store Financing Cards vs. Rewards Cards

Home Depot Canada, Rona, and other home improvement retailers offer proprietary store financing cards with 0% interest for 12–24 months on large purchases. These are different from rewards credit cards and serve a different purpose.

When to use store financing: If you're making a $5,000+ purchase (kitchen appliances, windows, flooring) that you need to pay off over 12–24 months, the 0% financing can save significant interest versus putting it on a rewards card and carrying a balance. The math: 0% for 24 months on $5,000 saves $995+ in interest vs. a standard 19.99% card.

The trap: Most store financing cards become deferred interest arrangements — if you don't pay the FULL balance before the promotional period ends, you're charged all of the interest that would have accrued from day one. A $5,000 purchase with 24-month 0% financing that you fail to pay off in full by month 24 could result in a $1,000+ interest charge appearing suddenly.

Best strategy for large renovations: If you need financing, use the store's 0% interest financing. If you can pay in full within 30 days, use your best rewards card (Rogers Red 1.5–2% or BMO Eclipse Rise ~1.7%). A $10,000 renovation on Rogers Red earns $150–$200 in cashback — but carrying that balance at 19.99% for 6 months costs $1,000 in interest, wiping out the rewards entirely.

Strategy for Large Renovation Projects

Time Your Welcome Bonus

A major renovation is the perfect time to apply for a premium card. A $10,000 kitchen renovation satisfies virtually any welcome bonus minimum spend threshold ($3,000–$5,000 in 3 months). Apply for the card with the best welcome bonus right before your renovation begins — the bonus alone (often $500–$750 value) dwarfs the regular earn rate on the purchases.

Scotia Momentum VI welcome bonus: $500 cashback
Min spend: $3,000 in first 3 months
Renovation spend: $8,000 (easily exceeds threshold)
Regular earn at 1% on $8,000: $80
→ Total earned: $580 from one renovation project

Split Purchases Between Stores

Since BMO Eclipse VI does not earn bonus points on Amazon.ca (it earns 1x on general retail), Rogers Red (1.5–2% flat) is the consistent choice for both Amazon.ca orders and in-store hardware runs. Route large-format materials (lumber, concrete) and Amazon supplies equally to Rogers Red for a reliable 1.5–2% on all home improvement spend.

Use Purchase Protection for Appliances

Extended warranty benefits on credit cards are particularly valuable for appliances — washer/dryers, dishwashers, refrigerators purchased at Home Depot or Rona are typically warrantied for 1 year by the manufacturer. A card that extends this to 2–3 years saves the $150–$400 extended warranty upsell retailers push hard.
