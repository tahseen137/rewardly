The Scotiabank Momentum Visa Infinite and CIBC Dividend Visa Infinite are Canada's two most popular grocery cashback cards — and they're often compared because both earn 4% on groceries, but CIBC earns 4% on gas while Scotia earns 2% on gas. But underneath that headline rate, they're quite different cards.

Bottom line up front

For most Canadians: Scotiabank Momentum wins — better streaming (4% vs 1%) and recurring bills (4% vs 2%), free first year, and no-fee supplementary cards. CIBC Dividend edges ahead if you drive a lot (4% gas vs 2%) or dine out frequently (2% dining vs 1%).

Earn rates: where each card dominates

Where they're identical

Both cards earn exactly 4% on groceries, but their gas rates differ: CIBC Dividend VI earns 4% on gas and EV charging, while Scotia Momentum earns 2% on gas and transit. For high-volume drivers, CIBC Dividend has a meaningful edge.

Where Scotiabank Momentum wins

  • Streaming (4% vs 1%): Netflix, Disney+, Spotify, Amazon Prime, Crave, Apple Music. CIBC earns only the base 1% here. At $50/month streaming: $24/year on Scotia vs $6/year on CIBC.
  • Recurring bills (4% vs 2%): Internet, wireless, utilities. Scotia earns 4% on eligible recurring bill payments vs CIBC's 2% — a meaningful difference if you pre-authorize several bills.
  • Free first year + no-fee supplementary cards: Scotia waives the $120 annual fee in year one and charges $0 for additional cardholders. CIBC charges $30 per supplementary card.

Where CIBC Dividend wins

  • Gas & EV charging (4% vs 2%): CIBC earns double what Scotia earns at the pump or charging station. On $200/month gas: $96/year on CIBC vs $48/year on Scotia.
  • Transit (tie — both 2%): Both cards earn the same 2% on eligible transit purchases including TTC, GO, OC Transpo, and TransLink.
  • Dining (2% vs 1%): Restaurants, bars, food delivery (Uber Eats, DoorDash). If you dine out frequently, CIBC earns double what Scotia earns on this category.
  • Drug stores (2% vs 1%): Shoppers Drug Mart, Rexall, Jean Coutu. CIBC earns double Scotia at pharmacies.
  • Mobile device insurance: CIBC covers your phone up to $1,000 when purchased on the card. Scotia Momentum doesn't include this.

Payout timing: the most underrated difference

Both cards now offer on-demand cashback redemption — no more waiting for an annual payout:

  • Scotiabank Momentum lets you redeem anytime via the Scotia app or online banking, with a minimum $25 redemption. Cash back is applied as a statement credit.
  • CIBC Dividend lets you redeem anytime via the CIBC app or online banking, with a minimum $10 redemption. Cash back is applied as a statement credit or transferred to your CIBC account.

CIBC's lower $10 minimum gives slightly more flexibility, but in practice both cards put you in control of when you get paid back. This was previously a big edge for Scotia (which had monthly automatic payouts vs CIBC's annual January payout), but the gap has closed.

Who wins each scenario

Choose Scotia Momentum if:

  • You subscribe to multiple streaming services (4% vs CIBC's 1%)
  • You have large recurring bill payments — internet, wireless, utilities (4% vs 2%)
  • You're signing up now and want a free first year (CIBC has no first-year waiver)
  • You plan to add a spouse/partner at no extra cost ($0 supp. fee vs $30)
  • You want the better travel medical insurance (15 vs 10 days)

Choose CIBC Dividend if:

  • You drive frequently — CIBC earns 4% on gas vs Scotia's 2%
  • You dine out or order food delivery often (2% vs Scotia's 1%)
  • You spend at drug stores like Shoppers, Rexall, or Jean Coutu (2% vs 1%)
  • You want mobile device insurance ($1,000 replacement coverage)
  • You already bank with CIBC and prefer one ecosystem

FAQ

Can I have both cards?

Yes. Some people use CIBC Dividend for gas, dining, and drug stores, and Scotia Momentum for streaming and recurring bills. But the combined $240/year in fees (year 2 onward) only makes sense if you spend heavily enough in each card's bonus categories to cover both. Use Rewardly to check if it's worth it for your spending.

Does Walmart count as grocery on both cards?

Yes on both. Walmart Supercentres typically code as grocery (MCC 5411) for both Scotia and CIBC, earning 4% on both cards.

Which card is better if I don't drive and don't take transit?

Without gas or transit spending, the comparison narrows. Focus on groceries (tied at 4%), dining (CIBC wins at 2%), and streaming (Scotia wins at 4%). For high gas spenders, CIBC Dividend is the clear winner at 4% vs Scotia's 2%. Run the numbers based on how much you spend on each.

Can I switch from one to the other without hurting my credit score?

Applying for a new card triggers a hard inquiry (-5 to -10 points, recovers in 12 months). Closing the old card removes it from your credit history and reduces your average account age, which could lower your score. A safer path: downgrade to a no-fee card at the bank you're leaving, rather than closing the account entirely.

Does Scotia Momentum's first-year free offer still exist?

As of July 2026, yes — the annual fee is waived for the first year, including supplementary cardholders added in year one. This is subject to change; confirm directly with Scotiabank.
